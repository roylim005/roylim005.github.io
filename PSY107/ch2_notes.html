2.1

Billions of Neurons — Communication in the Brain

Key Numbers

Brain contains ~86 billion neurons (revised down from the old estimate of 100B).
Similar number of glia cells — once thought only supportive, now known to assist neural signalling and network formation.
Glia cells form myelin — the whitish fatty sheath that insulates axons and speeds up signal transmission.
Clinical Link — Multiple Sclerosis (MS)
MS destroys the myelin sheath → neural signals slow or stop entirely → progressive loss of motor control and sensory function (e.g. numbness, vision loss).

Anatomy of a Neuron

Cell body (soma) — contains nucleus with DNA.
Dendrites — branch-like structures that receive incoming signals. ("Head" end.)
Axon — long tail that carries signals away from cell body. Covered by myelin sheath in segments. ("Tail" end.)
Axon bulb / terminal buttons — contain vesicles packed with neurotransmitters.
Synapse — junction between two neurons. Includes the synaptic gap (physical space between pre- and postsynaptic neurons).
Scale fact
~100 trillion synapses but only ~86 billion neurons — because each neuron can synapse with up to 10,000 other neurons.
2.1.2

How Neurons Fire — Action Potentials

Resting Potential

At rest: inside of neuron is more negative than outside.
Resting potential = −70 mV (mammalian neurons).
Ion channels at rest block Na⁺ from entering → maintains the negative charge inside.

Firing an Action Potential

Incoming excitatory signals raise the internal charge toward the threshold of excitation (~−55 mV).
At threshold → Na⁺ ion channels open → Na⁺ floods in → inside becomes rapidly positive → action potential fires.
All-or-None Law
Action potentials are either full strength or nothing — like a light switch. Once threshold is reached, the impulse travels the full length of the axon to the synapse.

Refractory Period (Returning to Rest)

As action potential travels, K⁺ (potassium) is pumped OUT → restores negative charge inside.
Brief period of refractory (a few milliseconds) during which the neuron cannot fire again.
Resting potential (−70 mV) is then restored, ready for the next signal.
Memory sequence
REST (−70mV) → EXCITE → THRESHOLD (−55mV) → Na⁺ IN → FIRE → K⁺ OUT → REST
2.1.3–4

Synaptic Transmission & Reuptake

Jumping the Synapse

Action potential reaches axon bulb → neurotransmitters released into synaptic gap.
NT molecules drift across gap → bind to receptor sites on postsynaptic dendrites (lock-and-key model).
Excitation — NT makes postsynaptic cell more positive → more likely to fire.
Inhibition — NT makes postsynaptic cell more negative → less likely to fire.
Postsynaptic axon sums all incoming excitatory & inhibitory signals. Fires only if net sum reaches threshold.

Reuptake

Reuptake = leftover NTs in synapse are reabsorbed by the presynaptic neuron.
Purpose 1: Replenishes NT supply for next signal.
Purpose 2: Clears synapse — prevents runaway excitation/inhibition.
Drug relevance
SSRIs (e.g. Prozac) block serotonin reuptake, keeping more serotonin active in the synapse → antidepressant effect.
2.2

Neurotransmitters & Neuromodulators

100+ identified neurotransmitters. Neuromodulators affect neural signalling indirectly (e.g. protecting neurons, modulating NT release) without directly changing resting potential.

Acetylcholine (ACh)
Memory, consciousness, muscle excitation, heart inhibition. First NT discovered.
↓ ACh → Alzheimer's disease (memory loss)
Dopamine
Movement, learning, attention, motivation, reward/pleasure.
↓ Dopamine → Parkinson's disease  |  ↑ Dopamine → Schizophrenia
Serotonin
Sleep, mood, arousal, eating, pain perception.
↓ Serotonin → Depression, anxiety, eating disorders. Treated by SSRIs.
Norepinephrine (NOR)
Sleep, arousal, mood regulation. Synapse development in childhood. Recovery after TBI.
Implicated in depression; also functions as endocrine hormone (adrenaline-related).
GABA
Chief inhibitory NT. Regulates arousal. ~⅓ of all synapses.
↓ GABA → Seizures, anxiety. Increased by benzodiazepines, alcohol, barbiturates.
Glutamate
Chief excitatory NT. >50% of synapses. Essential for nearly all brain processes.
Excess release after brain injury → excitotoxicity (neurons fire to death).
Endorphins
Natural painkillers. Released during stress/exertion. Bind opioid receptors → reduce pain + mild euphoria.
Released ~15 min after eating fat; triggered by exercise. Mimicked by opioid drugs (heroin, morphine).
Mnemonic — major NTs
Ace Doesn't Sleep — Nor Goes Glutamate Easily  →  ACh · Dopamine · Serotonin · NOR · GABA · Glutamate · Endorphins
2.3

Structure of the Nervous System

CNS vs PNS

Central Nervous System (CNS) — brain + spinal cord.
Peripheral Nervous System (PNS) — all other neurons. Two roles: (1) sensory input to CNS via sensory neurons; (2) motor output from CNS via motor neurons.
Interneurons — carry signals within CNS during processing.

Somatic vs Autonomic

Branch Controls Key Feature
Somatic NS Skeletal muscles Voluntary movement
Autonomic NS Smooth muscles, heart, glands Involuntary / automatic
↳ Parasympathetic Internal organs at rest "Rest and digest" — normal HR, BP, digestion active
↳ Sympathetic Fight-or-flight response ↑ HR, BP, breathing; ↓ digestion; dilated pupils; blood to muscles
2.4

The Brain & Spinal Cord — CNS Structures

Three Regions Overview

Hindbrain — base of brain above spinal cord. Most "primitive." Life-sustaining functions.
Midbrain — connects hindbrain ↔ forebrain. Keeps forebrain active via reticular formation.
Forebrain — largest region. Complex cognition, emotion, motivation, language.

Hindbrain Structures

Medulla oblongata — heartbeat, respiration, sneezing, coughing, vomiting. Even minor damage = death.
Pons — "bridge" for sensory signals between hemispheres. Respiration, sleep, dreaming, facial movement, consciousness.
Cerebellum — balance, muscle tone, motor skills, motor learning. Contains ~80% of cerebral neurons but only 10% of brain mass. Also implicated in memory, emotion, depression, ADHD.
Alcohol & Cerebellum
Alcohol impairs cerebellum → staggering, clumsiness, slowed reaction time.

Midbrain — Reticular Formation

Network running from hindbrain through midbrain into forebrain.
Regulates arousal, attention, sleep, consciousness, cardiovascular activity, body movement.

Forebrain — Limbic System

Amygdala (almond-shaped, behind temples) — fear and aggression, perception of emotional facial expressions. Damage → inability to feel or recognise fear.
Hippocampus — learning and memory (especially forming new long-term memories). Neuroplasticity: expands with spatial memory use (e.g. London taxi drivers). Damaged by chronic stress hormones. Meditation linked to larger hippocampus.
Case Study — H.M.
Hippocampus surgically removed (to control epilepsy) → anterograde amnesia: could not form new declarative memories. Could still learn new motor skills but had no memory of learning them. Foundational evidence for hippocampus in memory formation.
Case Study — S.M.
Amygdala destroyed by disease → completely unable to experience fear from external stimuli. Could still feel fear from internal bodily sensations (e.g. feeling unable to breathe) — suggests amygdala is only involved in external fear processing.

Forebrain — Thalamus & Hypothalamus

Thalamus — relay station for sensory information to cortex. Also involved in attention and certain sleep stages (REM).
Hypothalamus — maintains homeostasis (temperature, hunger, thirst, sleep, sexual/reproductive functions, hormone levels). Connects nervous system to endocrine system via the pituitary gland.

The Cortex

Thin (~2mm) wrinkled layer covering cerebral hemispheres. Highest-level processing: cognition, language, planning, decision-making. Most developed in humans of all species.
75% = association cortex (higher cognition). 25% = motor/sensory functions.
Divided into 4 lobes (each duplicated L & R).

The Four Lobes

LobeLocationKey Functions
Frontal Front / forehead Primary motor cortex (voluntary movement). Broca's area (speech production). Prefrontal: personality, judgment, planning, emotion regulation. Phineas Gage case.
Parietal Top, behind frontal Primary somatosensory area (touch, pressure, pain). Body mapping (somatosensory homunculus).
Occipital Back of skull Primary visual area — processes visual input. Damage → functional blindness.
Temporal Sides, behind ears Auditory cortex (hearing). Wernicke's area (speech comprehension). Damage to Wernicke's → receptive aphasia.

Lateralisation & the Corpus Callosum

Brain is wired contralaterally — right brain controls left body, and vice versa.
Language mostly in left hemisphere. Spatial processing more in right hemisphere.
Corpus callosum — dense band connecting L & R hemispheres so they share information.
Women tend to have larger corpus callosa, possibly enabling more integrated (less lateralised) brain function.
Split-Brain Research (Gazzaniga)
Corpus callosum cut to control severe epilepsy → right and left hemispheres operate independently. Object seen in left visual field (processed by right brain) cannot be named — but can be pointed to with left hand. Confirmed left hemisphere handles speech production.

2.6

The Endocrine System

Overview

Chemical communication system using hormones released by endocrine glands into the bloodstream.
Slower than the nervous system (travels via blood) but produces longer-lasting effects.
Governs: sexual activity, eating, sleeping, arousal, growth.
Hypothalamus → Pituitary gland → other endocrine glands → target organs. (The "master" control chain.)

Key Glands

GlandLocationSecretes / Controls
PituitaryUnder hypothalamus"Master gland" — regulates all other endocrine glands via hormones
Gonads (Ovaries / Testes)Abdominal / testiclesEstrogens (F) & Androgens (M) — sexual development & reproduction
Adrenal glandsAbove kidneysMedulla: epinephrine (adrenaline) & norepinephrine → fight-or-flight. Cortex: adrenal androgens, immune interaction.
ThyroidNeckRegulates energy use (metabolism)
PancreasAbdomenInsulin — regulates blood sugar
Pineal glandBrainMay influence sexual maturation
Nervous System vs Endocrine System
Nervous system = fast, short-lived signals (electrochemical, milliseconds). Endocrine system = slow, long-lasting signals (hormones via blood, seconds to hours). Both regulated by the hypothalamus.
