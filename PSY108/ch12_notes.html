Section 1
What is Stress?
Stress

The physical and mental changes that occur in response to a stressor — an emotional, physical, and mental response. It is a psychological state, uniquely perceived by each individual, accompanied by predictable physiological and behavioural changes.

Stress is complex because the same event can be stressful to one person and not to another — this depends on how the individual appraises or interprets the situation (cognitive appraisal). Stress can be an advantage in the short term (helps us focus and respond) but becomes a disadvantage if prolonged.

✅
Advantages of Stress

Enables focus on the threatening event. Sets us ready to respond. Short-term mobilisation of resources.

⚠️
Disadvantages of Stress

Threatens health if prolonged. Leads to heart disease, stroke, high blood pressure, mental disorders.

Section 2
What Causes Stress? Sources of Stress

Stress is a general term for the psychological and bodily response to a stimulus that alters your bodily equilibrium (homeostasis). Stressors come in different types, with varying effects on different individuals.

Type of Stressor Nature Example
Acute Short-term Surprise quiz from your instructor without notice
Chronic Long-term Constant work deadlines with no relief; living in a crowded home
Physical Applies to most people Natural disasters, war, terrorist attacks
Psychological Subjective (individual) Going to a nightclub — exciting for some, a nightmare for others
Key insight: Stressors are "in the eyes of the beholder." Our response to stress depends on our perception of the stressor, not simply its objective nature. The same stressor can have different effects on different individuals.
Section 3
Types of Stressors

The seminar slides present stressors under five main categories. The key exam-relevant categories are:

💥
Traumatic Events

Unexpected catastrophic events — floods, earthquakes, war — perceived as threats by almost everyone. Can lead to PTSD.

🔁
Chronic Stressors

Ongoing stressors: social stressors, burnout, major life events, daily hassles. Continuous and wearing.

😔
Grief

Loss of loved ones; bereavement triggers a stress response that can affect immune function.

🧠
Posttraumatic Stress

Lingering effects after a traumatic event — flashbacks, nightmares, hyperarousal (PTSD).

📅
Major Life Events

Significant changes (marriage, divorce, job loss) that require adjustment. Can be positive or negative but still stressful.

😤
Daily Hassles

Minor everyday irritants (traffic, queues, arguments) that accumulate into significant stress over time.

Section 4
Internal Conflicts as Stressors

Internal conflicts occur when people face difficult choices. There are four types — each generating different levels of stress:

Approach–Approach

Both alternatives are positive. Least stressful — you can't really lose. E.g., choosing between a holiday to Paris or Rome.

Avoidance–Avoidance

Both alternatives are unpleasant. More stressful — people often feel "frozen" and avoid deciding. E.g., mop the floor or do the dishes.

Approach–Avoidance

One option has both positive and negative aspects. You're attracted and repelled simultaneously. E.g., wanting to save your pet from a burning building but fearing injury.

Multiple Conflicts

Several alternatives each with both positive and negative features. E.g., choosing between a stylish uncomfortable shirt or a comfortable but plain one.

Section 5
The General Adaptation Syndrome (GAS)

Selye's GAS describes the body's three-stage response to any stressor — mentally, physically, and emotionally.

1
Alarm Reaction

The body mobilises its resources to cope with the stressor. "Fight or flight" kicks in — the sympathetic nervous system activates, adrenaline and cortisol are released.

2
Resistance

The body adapts to the continued presence of the stressor. Physiological arousal remains elevated but slightly lower than the alarm stage. The body tries to repair itself.

3
Exhaustion

The body depletes its resources. Vulnerability to illness increases. Can lead to illness/death if stress is extreme and prolonged.

If the stressor is resolved during the resistance stage, the body returns to normal (successful resistance). If not, exhaustion occurs.
Section 6
The Alarm Reaction & Fight-or-Flight Response

The fight-or-flight response is hardwired into our brains — it is a genetic mechanism designed to protect us from bodily harm. During the alarm stage, the body undergoes a cascade of physiological changes:

🧠 Brain Signal

The hypothalamus signals the adrenal glands to produce adrenaline & cortisol, releasing them into the bloodstream.

❤️ Cardiovascular

Heart rate, breathing rate, and blood pressure all increase. Blood vessels widen to deliver more blood to large muscle groups.

👁️ Sensory

Pupils dilate to improve vision. Muscles are placed on high alert for rapid action.

⚡ Energy

The liver releases stored glucose to increase energy available for the body's response.

🌡️ Cooling

Sweat is produced to cool the body down during the heightened physiological activity.

⚠️ The Problem

Stress hormones flow for non-dangerous events (work deadlines, relationships), wearing out the body's reserves and weakening the immune system.

Long-term effects of chronic fight-or-flight activation: Depleted reserves, feeling overwhelmed, weakened immune system, leading to illness and other health problems.

Section 8
Cognitive Appraisal
Cognitive Appraisal

The process by which you interpret an event as stressful or non-stressful. It leads to individual differences in stress responses — the same event can be stressful for one person and not for another.

Aaron Beck's insight captures this well: "It's not the problem that's the problem, but how we see the problem that is the problem."

Key principle — Perceived demand vs. Perceived ability: Stress arises when the perceived demand of a task exceeds our perceived ability to handle it. When ability clearly exceeds demand, there is no stress. When demand clearly exceeds ability, stress occurs.

Appraisal can be negative (creating emotional, physiological, and behavioural stress responses) or positive (resulting in no stress or even motivation).

The ABC Model of Cognitive Appraisal
A
Activating Event

The trigger or potential stressor that occurs

→
B
Belief / Interpretation

How you interpret the event (negative or positive). Recognise the thought causing anxiety; challenge that thought.

→
C
Consequences

The behaviour or emotion that results from your interpretation

The ABC model shows that B (belief/interpretation) — not A (the event) — determines C (the emotional consequence). Changing how we appraise a situation can change our stress response.

Section 9
Stress-Related Personality Types

Cognitive appraisal is influenced by personality. People with certain personality types are more prone to stress-related responses such as impatience, competitiveness, perfectionism, aggressiveness, and hostility.

Type A
  • Competitive
  • Verbally aggressive
  • Overcommitted to achieving
  • Impatient
  • Hostile when frustrated
  • Sense of time urgency
  • Workaholic / easily angered
Type B
  • Easygoing
  • Calm & relaxed
  • Patient & trusting
  • Good-natured
  • Lower need for achievement
  • Supportive
Type C
  • Cautious & careful
  • Detached
  • Introspective
  • Sensitive to criticism
  • Serious
  • Results oriented
  • Suppressed emotion
Type D
  • Distressed / worrisome
  • Isolated & sad
  • Irritated & lonely
  • Fear of rejection
  • Suppressed emotion
Health links: Type A — anger and hostility are key risk factors for heart disease. Type D — combination of negative emotions + social inhibition increases risk of heart problems and depression. Type C — suppression of negative emotions may be linked to some health risks.
Section 10
Moderators of Stress: Locus of Control
Locus of Control

One's perception about the underlying main causes of events in their life. It moderates (buffers or amplifies) the impact of stressors.

Internal Locus of Control

Believe their actions determine outcomes.

  • Happier, more free, less stressed
  • Physically healthier
  • Feel in control
  • Perceive stressors as manageable
  • More active, alert
  • Longer lifespan
  • Better immune system
External Locus of Control

Believe outside forces control outcomes.

  • More susceptible to stress
  • More prone to depression
  • More health problems overall
  • Feel little control
  • Perceive stressors as overwhelming
Important: Perceived lack of control in aversive situations can lead to Learned Helplessness — a passive response to stressors based on prior experiences of uncontrollable negative events. People give up trying even when a solution is available.
Section 11
Moderators of Stress: Hardiness
Hardiness

A mental quality of resistance to stress, based on three core characteristics. Considered the most effective moderator of stress — and it can be learned.

🎯
Challenge

View stressors and difficulties as challenges to overcome, not threats. Embrace growth through difficulty.

🤝
Commitment

Maintain a strong sense of purpose and engagement in work, relationships, and values — even under pressure.

🕹️
Control

Believe you have influence over events in your life. Approach stressors actively rather than feeling helpless.

Hardy individuals are unusually resistant to stress. Unlike Learned Helplessness, hardiness promotes a positive worldview where stressors are challenges — leading to more problem-focused coping and greater resilience.

Section 12
Coping Strategies

Coping refers to what we do to manage a perceived stressor. There are two broad strategies:

1. Problem-Focused Coping

Doing something constructive to resolve or change the stressful situation. Best used when you feel you can do something about it.

  • Identifying stressors
  • Monitoring stress levels
  • Identifying stress antecedents (triggers)
  • Acquiring new skills
  • Setting new goals
  • Time management
  • Assertiveness training
2. Emotion-Focused Coping

Responding to stress by addressing one's emotional reactions. Best used when the situation requires acceptance (e.g., waiting for medical results).

  • Constructive ways to regulate negative emotions
  • Journalling / talking to others
  • Relaxation techniques
  • Exercise / hobbies
  • Deep breathing exercises
  • Getting enough sleep
  • Cognitive reappraisal (see below)
2.1 Cognitive Reappraisal (Emotion-Focused)
Cognitive Reappraisal

Reappraising stressors — seeing a situation in a more positive or realistic light. It is the cornerstone of CBT (Cognitive Behavioural Therapy) and is particularly effective for chronic stress. Uses the ABC model: recognise the belief (B) causing anxiety, then challenge that belief.

Example: Viewing an unexpected quiz not as a disaster ("There's no way I can do this") but as an opportunity ("I'll do my best — this shows what I know"). This prevents the cascade of emotional, physiological, and behavioural stress responses.

Practical Stress Management Tips (Study Unit Notes)
  • Set realistic goals in whatever you do
  • Get enough sleep
  • Do deep breathing exercises when you encounter a stressful event
  • Exercise regularly
  • Engage in hobbies
  • Use the ABC model to cognitively reappraise stressors
  • Build your hardiness (challenge, commitment, control)
  • Strengthen your internal locus of control
Section 13
Defense Mechanisms

Defense mechanisms are a subcategory of emotion-focused coping. Unlike cognitive reappraisal (which is conscious and deliberate), defense mechanisms operate unconsciously and automatically — we use them to reduce anxiety and protect our self-image without realising we are doing so.

Key distinction from cognitive reappraisal: Cognitive reappraisal changes the interpretation driving anxiety. Defense mechanisms avoid or disguise the anxiety without resolving the underlying stressor — the source of stress remains, and may resurface later.
Adaptive vs. Maladaptive
ADAPTIVE

Redirects energy constructively. Does not harm others. Temporary relief that does not prevent long-term resolution.
Example: Sublimation

MALADAPTIVE (when overused)

Prevents resolution of the real problem. Keeps person unaware of true source of distress. Can harm relationships and health over time.

Mechanism What it is Scenario Example Watch out for
Repression Unconsciously blocking distressing thoughts, memories or feelings from conscious awareness A person who witnessed a traumatic accident has no memory of the event years later — not by choice, but because the mind buried it Differs from suppression (conscious). Repression is involuntary.
Denial Refusing to acknowledge a threatening or painful reality — acting as if it does not exist A heavy smoker insists "I'm perfectly healthy — nothing's wrong with me" despite persistent coughing and a doctor's warnings The person genuinely does not accept the reality, not just ignoring it.
Projection Attributing your own unacceptable thoughts, feelings or impulses to someone else A student who secretly dislikes a classmate insists "She hates me — I can tell she can't stand being around me" The feeling originates in the person using the mechanism, not the person it is projected onto.
Displacement Redirecting emotions (usually anger) from the real target to a safer, less threatening one A man reprimanded by his boss comes home and shouts at his family over a minor issue The substitute target is safer or less powerful than the real source of frustration.
Rationalisation Creating plausible, logical-sounding justifications for behaviour or decisions that are actually driven by unacceptable motives A student who failed an exam says "The questions were unfair and badly worded" rather than acknowledging they did not study enough The explanation sounds reasonable but is not the real reason. Self-deception dressed as logic.
Sublimation Adaptive Redirecting unacceptable impulses or frustrations into socially acceptable and often productive activities A person who feels intense anger after a difficult breakup throws herself into training for a marathon and channels that energy into performance The only widely considered adaptive defense mechanism. The underlying impulse is transformed, not suppressed or misdirected.
Reaction Formation Expressing the opposite of what you actually feel — converting an unacceptable impulse into its opposite A person who feels deep jealousy toward a colleague goes out of their way to lavishly praise that colleague to everyone The expressed emotion is exaggerated and seems performative — often a clue that the opposite feeling is present underneath.
Regression Reverting to behaviours typical of an earlier developmental stage when facing stress or anxiety A 30-year-old who is going through a divorce starts throwing tantrums, sulking, and refusing to engage in adult conversation The behaviour is childlike relative to the person's age and normal functioning. It is a retreat to a perceived safer, simpler time.
Intellectualisation Emotionally detaching from a distressing situation by focusing on its abstract, intellectual, or analytical aspects — avoiding the feelings entirely A person diagnosed with a serious illness spends all their time researching statistics and treatment mechanisms, discussing it clinically, but never acknowledges how frightened they are The person engages deeply with the topic but avoids any emotional processing. Information becomes a shield against feeling.
Critical exam point — Sublimation vs. Adaptive Emotion-Focused Coping: When someone consciously chooses exercise or a hobby to manage stress, that is adaptive emotion-focused coping. When someone unconsciously channels an unacceptable impulse (anger, frustration, anxiety) into a productive activity without realising the connection, that is sublimation. The key distinguishing factor is conscious vs. unconscious and whether an underlying unacceptable impulse is being transformed.
How to Identify Defense Mechanisms in Scenarios
  • No memory of a distressing event? → Repression
  • Refuses to accept an obvious reality? → Denial
  • Accuses others of feelings that seem to belong to themselves? → Projection
  • Takes frustration out on someone unrelated to the cause? → Displacement
  • Gives a logical but unconvincing excuse for behaviour? → Rationalisation
  • Channels frustration into something productive? → Sublimation
  • Acts the opposite of what they likely feel, exaggeratedly? → Reaction Formation
  • Acts childishly or reverts to immature behaviour under pressure? → Regression
  • Analyses a painful situation clinically, avoids all emotion about it? → Intellectualisation
