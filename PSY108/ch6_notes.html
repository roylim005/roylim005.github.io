Kim Peek — Chapter Anchor Case

Kim Peek (the real-life inspiration for Rain Man) could memorise entire books but could not learn to brush his teeth. His case frames the chapter: learning ability is not a single dimension. Classical and operant conditioning explain automatic/emotional learning; observational learning explains skill acquisition through modelling — and even Kim relied on daily modelling by his father for basic motor tasks.

6.1 — Habituation

Definition
Habituation is a decrease in responding to a stimulus as it is repeated over and over. It is widely considered the simplest form of learning, found in virtually all organisms, including newborn infants and even fetuses.

6.1.1 Orienting Reflex → Habituation

1
Novel stimulus appears → Orienting reflex: organism stops current activity and orients sense organs toward the stimulus.
2
Stimulus assessed as harmless → Organism returns attention to prior task.
3
Stimulus repeats → Orienting reflex diminishes with each repetition — this is habituation.
Key Purpose
Prevents wasting mental resources on irrelevant, non-threatening stimuli; allows selective attention to be directed where it matters.

6.1.2 Protective Function — Migraines Link

Research shows people with migraines often have reduced ability to habituate to stimuli at the neuronal level. The brain becomes hyperactive — unable to "tune out" — potentially causing migraine pain. Teaching habituation techniques to migraine sufferers reduces attack frequency.

6.1.3 Dishabituation

Definition
Dishabituation: When an organism begins responding more intensely to a stimulus it had previously habituated to, because the stimulus has changed significantly or sufficient time has elapsed.
TriggerResult
Quality of stimulus changes significantlyImmediate dishabituation → re-orienting
Passage of time (e.g., long pause in stimulus)Brief dishabituation, then rapid re-habituation

6.1.4 Real-World Applications

Vertigo / Motion Sickness
Physical therapists repeatedly expose clients to motion-sickness stimuli to train habituation. Used with figure skaters, pilots, and astronauts.
PTSD — Prolonged Exposure Therapy
Clients repeatedly face fear-producing stimuli, habituating to anxiety triggers and improving quality of life.
Anxiety Disorders
Mindfulness-based stress reduction and CBT enhance habituation in the HPA axis, lessening physiological stress responses.

6.2 — Classical Conditioning

Core Definition
Learning that occurs when a neutral stimulus is repeatedly paired with an unconditioned stimulus, until the neutral stimulus alone can produce the response. Also called Pavlovian conditioning.

6.2.1 The Three-Step Process

1
Unconditioned Stimulus (US) + Unconditioned Response (UR) A stimulus that naturally and reliably causes a response without prior learning.
Example: Food (US) → Salivation (UR)
2
Neutral Stimulus (NS) Does not naturally produce the UR before conditioning.
Example: Buzzer — a dog does not naturally salivate to a buzzer
3
Pair NS with US → NS becomes Conditioned Stimulus (CS) → Conditioned Response (CR) After repeated pairings, the NS loses neutrality and acquires the power of the US.
Example: Buzzer (CS) alone → Salivation (CR)
NS + US → UR   [repeated]   →    CS → CR

6.2.2 Factors Affecting Conditioning

Contiguity
NS/CS and US must occur close together in time. Best conditioning occurs when CS immediately precedes US (forward/delayed conditioning). Backward conditioning (US before CS) is weakest.
Contingency
CS must reliably predict the US. Inconsistent pairing weakens the association. Both contiguity AND contingency are required for strong conditioning.

Real-World Applications

Higher-Order Conditioning
Conditioning that builds on existing conditioned responses. CS1 (e.g., snakes → fear) can be paired with a new neutral stimulus to produce CS2 (e.g., hiking trails → fear), without re-involving the original US.
Little Albert (Watson & Rayner, 1920)
An 11-month-old was conditioned to fear a white rat (NS) by pairing it with a loud noise (US). The resulting fear (CR) then generalised to other furry objects — rabbits, fur coats, Santa beards. Landmark but ethically problematic study.
ConceptDescriptionExample
Stimulus Generalisation CR occurs to stimuli similar to the CS (never paired with US) Fear of bees generalises to wasps and yellow jackets
Stimulus Discrimination CR occurs only to the specific CS, not similar stimuli Zoo reptile keeper fears king cobras but not all snakes
Taste Aversion Nausea conditioned to a food after single pairing; intervals up to 7h in humans Peppermint ice cream → sickness → lasting aversion
Taste Aversion — Two Unique Features

1. Single-trial learning — one pairing often sufficient (unlike most CC requiring many trials).
2. Long CS–US interval — up to 24h in non-humans; up to 7h in humans. Explained by biological preparedness: natural selection favoured genes enabling quick aversion to poisonous substances.

6.2.4 Removing Conditioned Responses

Extinction
Present the CS repeatedly without the US. CR gradually decreases. Does not erase the association — re-acquisition is faster than original learning.
Spontaneous Recovery
A temporary re-emergence of the CR after extinction. Brief; organism quickly re-enters extinction.
Counterconditioning
Pair the CS with a US that produces an incompatible positive response (e.g., pairing feared object with candy to replace fear with happiness).
Aversion Therapy (Alcoholism)
Drug disulfiram + alcohol → severe nausea → conditioned aversion to alcohol taste. Modestly effective but not a standalone cure. Side effects limit use. Also being studied for cocaine and methamphetamine abuse.

6.3 — Operant Conditioning

Core Definition
Learning from the consequences of behaviour. Unlike classical conditioning (respondent), operant behaviour is chosen — the organism operates on the environment to produce a consequence.

6.3.1 Thorndike's Law of Effect

Law of Effect
Behaviours that lead to satisfying consequences are strengthened (more likely to be repeated). Behaviours that lead to discomforting consequences are weakened (less likely to be repeated).

Demonstrated with cats in puzzle boxes: cats initially escaped by trial-and-error; through reinforcement of the successful response, escape time decreased over trials.

6.3.2 The Four Consequences of Behaviour

Consequence What happens Effect on behaviour Example
Positive Reinforcement Add something pleasant (+) ↑ Behaviour increases Praise for answering correctly
Negative Reinforcement Remove something unpleasant (−) ↑ Behaviour increases Cat escapes puzzle box, removing confinement
Positive Punishment Add something unpleasant (+) ↓ Behaviour decreases Electric shock when rat presses lever
Negative Punishment Remove something pleasant (−) ↓ Behaviour decreases Losing screen time for misbehaving
⚠ Common Confusion
Negative reinforcement ≠ Punishment. "Negative" refers to removing a stimulus, not a bad outcome. Both positive and negative reinforcement increase behaviour. Punishment always decreases behaviour.

6.3.4 Schedules of Reinforcement

Schedule Rule Response Rate Extinction Resistance Real-World Example
Continuous Every response reinforced High Lowest Reaching for objects
Fixed Ratio (FR) Reinforced after set number of responses High Moderate Paid per 100 units assembled
Variable Ratio (VR) Reinforced after unpredictable number Highest Highest Slot machines
Fixed Interval (FI) First response after set time is reinforced Scallop pattern (low then burst) Low–Moderate Annual performance review
Variable Interval (VI) First response after unpredictable time interval Steady High Pop quizzes
Key Summary — Schedules

Ratio schedules produce higher response rates than interval schedules.
Variable schedules produce greater resistance to extinction than fixed schedules.
Continuous reinforcement produces the fastest extinction once reinforcement stops.

6.3.6 Shaping

Shaping — Definition
Reinforcing successive approximations of the final desired behaviour until that behaviour is achieved. Used when the target behaviour would never occur spontaneously.

Example: Training a dog to walk on hind legs: reinforce attention → sitting up → rearing up → standing → walking. Each step is a closer approximation.

Instinctive drift: A conditioned animal may eventually revert to instinctive behaviour over the learned response — conditioning cannot fully override instinct.

Punishment — Why Reinforcement is Preferred

Doesn't teach correct behaviour
Punishment tells the organism what not to do, but not what to do instead. Must always pair with positive modelling of desired behaviour.
Models aggression
Physical punishment teaches the child that aggression is an effective strategy — they may imitate it in other situations.
Often ineffective
Child may suppress behaviour only when parent is present; returns when unsupervised.
Negative emotional effects
Via classical conditioning, the punisher (parent) can become a CS for fear and anxiety — damaging the relationship.

6.3.8 Cognition in Learning — Beyond Behaviourism

Köhler — Insight
Chimps paused to mentally plan before acting — they didn't trial-and-error; they achieved sudden insight. Shows purposive, goal-directed behaviour.
Tolman — Latent Learning
Rats learned maze structure without reinforcement, developing a cognitive map. When reinforcement was later introduced, they rapidly outperformed naïve rats. Learning occurred without observable behaviour.
Token Economy
Secondary reinforcers (tokens) exchangeable for primary reinforcers. Advantages: (1) works across groups simultaneously, (2) allows immediate reinforcement when primary reinforcer is impractical, (3) builds delay of gratification. Risk: continuous schedule → vulnerability to extinction.

6.4 — Observational Learning

Core Definition
Learning through observing and imitating the behaviour of others. Also called social learning or modelling. Bandura's framework. Departs from strict behaviourism on two counts: (1) learning without overt behaviour; (2) cognition plays a central role.

6.4.1 Bobo Doll Experiments (Bandura, 1965)

Children watched a film of a woman beating a Bobo doll in one of three conditions:

Film ConditionChild's Aggression Toward Bobo
Model RewardedHigh aggression — most imitation
No ConsequencesHigh aggression — equal to reward condition
Model PunishedLower aggression — most hesitation
Key Finding

When Bandura asked ALL children to demonstrate what they had learned (with no threat of punishment), all three groups showed equal knowledge of how to be aggressive. This demonstrates latent learning — performance ≠ learning. Children who saw punishment had learned but chose not to perform.

Seeing someone merely get away with behaviour is as effective at promoting imitation as seeing them rewarded.

6.4.2 Bandura's Four-Step Model of Modelling

1
Attention Observer must pay attention to the model. Children attend more to warm, nurturing, or powerful models; to televised models; and with age, to models similar to themselves.
2
Retention in Memory Observer must form and retain a cognitive representation of the modelled behaviour. Memory is the bridge between observation and later reproduction.
3
Reproduction Observer must be physically and mentally capable of reproducing the behaviour. A child cannot perfectly replicate a superhero flying, but may approximate it.
4
Motivation Observer must be motivated to execute the behaviour. Expected consequences (reward or punishment) influence whether the learned behaviour is actually performed.

Media & Children — Key Points

Screen Time Research
Children as young as 12 months model emotional reactions seen on TV. Research links screen time to poorer executive functioning in some areas. American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP) recommends avoiding screen time for children under 18 months. Video games can have both negative effects (aggression, sexist attitudes) and positive effects (teamwork, cognitive skills, exergaming for physical–cognitive health).
Two Key Departures from Behaviourism
1. Learning can occur without any overt change in behaviour (latent learning).
2. Cognitive processes — attention, memory, motivation — are central to learning.
