Overview

Core Definition
Learning is a process through which experience produces a lasting change in behaviour or mental processes. It may show up immediately in behaviour (riding a bike) or lie dormant until needed (knowing speeding is wrong but still speeding).

From the behavioural perspective, nurture — our environment and experiences — carries more weight than nature in shaping who we are and how we act. This is the foundation for understanding all four types of learning in this chapter.

Habituation — Brief Mention
The simplest form of learning: learning not to respond to a repeated, non-threatening stimulus. It helps us focus on what matters and ignore what doesn't. Example: tuning out a fan's hum after a while. Only briefly introduced in this seminar — not tested in depth.
Behaviourism vs. Cognitive Psychology — The Core Tension

Behaviourists (Pavlov, Watson, Skinner) say learning is a change in observable behaviour driven by external stimuli and consequences. They avoided discussing thoughts or feelings.

Cognitive psychologists (Tolman, Bandura, Köhler) say some learning must be explained by changes in internal mental processes — not just behaviour. You can learn something without ever showing it in your actions (latent learning), and you can learn by watching others without doing anything yourself (observational learning).

The debate — whether learning is defined by a change in behaviour or a change in understanding — continues today.

Classical Conditioning

Core Concept
A basic form of learning in which a stimulus that produces an innate reflex becomes associated with a previously neutral stimulus, which then acquires the power to elicit essentially the same response.

How It Was Discovered — Pavlov

Ivan Pavlov, a Russian physiologist studying digestion, noticed his dogs began salivating before food appeared — just from the sight of the food bowl or the sound of his footsteps. He realised the dogs had learned to associate these cues with food. He then systematically tested this by pairing a bell with food across many trials.

The key insight: an organism can be taught to respond to a completely neutral stimulus (one that originally meant nothing) as if it were the real thing — purely through repeated association.

The Three-Stage Process

1
Before Conditioning Food (UCS) naturally causes salivation (UCR) — no learning needed, it's a reflex. The bell (Neutral Stimulus) produces no salivation — it means nothing to the dog yet.
2
During Conditioning — Pairing Bell (NS) is sounded just before food (UCS) is presented, repeatedly. The dog salivates (UCR) because of the food. Over many trials, the dog begins to associate the bell with the food.
3
After Conditioning The bell alone (now CS) causes salivation (CR) — even without food. The neutral stimulus has lost its neutrality and now has the same power as the food to produce the response.
NS + UCS → UCR   [repeated]   →   CS → CR
Why timing matters (Contiguity): The NS must come just before the UCS — only a short time gap. If Pavlov rang the bell 2 hours before feeding, the dog would not connect the two events and conditioning would fail. Forward conditioning (NS before UCS) works best; backward (UCS before NS) works least.

Acquisition, Extinction & Spontaneous Recovery

These three phases describe the life cycle of a conditioned response and are often tested together (the seminar's graph on slide 10 illustrates all three).

PhaseWhat HappensKey Point
Acquisition CS and UCS are paired repeatedly. CR gradually builds in strength over trials. The learning phase — the association is being formed.
Extinction CS is presented repeatedly without UCS. CR weakens progressively and eventually ceases. Extinction suppresses the CR — it does NOT eliminate the underlying memory of the association.
Spontaneous Recovery After a rest period following extinction, the CS is presented again — CR reappears (though weaker). Proof that extinction only suppressed, not erased. Several extinction sessions may be needed to fully reduce the response.
Why This Matters — Real Life Example
A person who overcomes a fear of dogs through therapy (extinction) may suddenly feel afraid again when they encounter a dog after a long break — that is spontaneous recovery. The fear was never fully erased, only suppressed. This is why relapse is common and why multiple rounds of therapy may be needed.

Stimulus Generalisation & Discrimination

Once a CS is established, two opposite tendencies can emerge:

ConceptWhat HappensExample
Stimulus Generalisation The CR spreads to stimuli that are similar to the CS — even though those similar stimuli were never paired with the UCS. Dog salivates not just to the ringing bell (CS) but also to a doorbell or alarm clock. A person bitten by a dog (CS = dog → CR = fear) also fears wolves and foxes.
Stimulus Discrimination The organism learns to respond only to the specific CS, distinguishing it from similar stimuli that don't signal the UCS. Dog salivates to the ringing bell but not to the doorbell. A zoo reptile keeper fears king cobras but not all snakes.
Why Generalisation Happens

Generalisation is actually adaptive — it would be dangerous to only fear the exact dog that bit you. Extending the fear response to similar stimuli (other dogs, similar animals) provides broader protection. Discrimination is equally important: being able to distinguish between a harmless garden snake and a venomous cobra could save your life.

What Types of Responses Can Be Classically Conditioned?

Because CC starts with a reflex (an innate, automatic response to the UCS), the responses that get conditioned are typically:

  • Emotional responses — fear, happiness, disgust. E.g., feeling happy when you smell a perfume that reminds you of your mother; Little Albert's fear of white rats spreading to all furry objects.
  • Physiological responses — salivation, nausea. E.g., taste aversion: eating a food just before falling ill once can permanently condition nausea to that food — even years later.
Taste Aversion — A Special Case
Taste aversion is unique because: (1) it can occur after just one pairing (unlike most CC which requires many), and (2) the gap between eating the food (NS) and feeling sick (UCS) can be up to 7 hours in humans — far longer than normal CC allows. This is explained by biological preparedness: evolution has wired us to learn food–illness associations easily, because it helps us avoid poisonous substances. This is why coyotes can be trained to avoid sheep by making them sick from eating treated sheep carcasses.

Operant Conditioning

Core Concept
In operant conditioning, the consequences of behaviour — rewards and punishments — influence the probability that the behaviour will occur again. Unlike classical conditioning where the response is forced/reflexive, here the organism actively chooses to act on its environment.

Thorndike's Law of Effect (the foundation): Behaviours that lead to satisfying consequences are strengthened (more likely to recur). Behaviours that lead to discomforting consequences are weakened (less likely to recur). He demonstrated this with cats in puzzle boxes — cats learned through trial and error, and over time went straight to the behaviour that opened the box.

Skinner formalised this with the Skinner box and called it operant conditioning — emphasising that the organism operates on its environment to produce a consequence.

Behaviour → Consequence → Probability of Re-occurrence ↑ Reinforcement or ↓ Punishment

Types of Reinforcers

What — Primary vs. Secondary
TypeDescriptionExamples
Primary Directly and inherently reinforcing — fulfills a basic biological need or desire. No learning required for it to work. Food, water, warmth, sex
Secondary Reinforcing only because it has been learned to be associated with a primary reinforcer. Not reinforcing on its own initially. Money (leads to food/shelter), grades, praise, tokens. A dollar bill is just paper — what makes it reinforcing is what it can buy.
Is Attention a Primary or Secondary Reinforcer?

Attention is a secondary reinforcer. Primary reinforcers satisfy biological needs directly — food, water, warmth. Attention does not fit this because it is not inherently rewarding at birth.

Attention becomes reinforcing through learned association. From early childhood, attention from caregivers is repeatedly paired with primary reinforcers — being fed, held, kept warm. Over time, attention itself acquires reinforcing power. This is why a student like Ali may find a teacher's scolding rewarding — any attention, even negative, has been learned to be valuable.

This also explains why identifying the actual reinforcer behind a behaviour matters before designing any intervention. What looks like punishment from the outside may be functioning as reinforcement if it delivers something the person values — including attention.

How — Positive vs. Negative
⚠ Most confused concept in this chapter: "Positive" and "negative" here refer to adding (+) or removing (−) a stimulus — NOT to whether the experience is good or bad. Both positive AND negative reinforcement increase behaviour.
⚠ Critical Rule — Consequence Is Defined by Its Effect, Not Its Appearance
Always identify a consequence by what it does to behaviour — not by how it looks or feels. A scolding that increases misbehaviour is reinforcement, not punishment — because the child may find the attention rewarding. A reward that decreases behaviour is punishment. Ask: did the behaviour go up or down? That tells you whether it is reinforcement or punishment. Only then decide if it is positive or negative based on whether something was added or removed.

When — Continuous vs. Partial Reinforcement
TypeDescriptionBest forRisk
Continuous Every correct response is rewarded Early/new learning — helps the organism quickly learn what behaviour is desired Extinction happens fast when rewards stop. Omitting a reward may be misread as the behaviour being wrong. Can become boring over time.
Partial (Intermittent) Only some correct responses are rewarded, on a schedule Maintaining learnt behaviour long-term Slower initial acquisition — but far more resistant to extinction

Why is partial reinforcement more resistant to extinction? Because the organism is used to not always getting a reward, so when rewards stop, it doesn't immediately know the contingency has changed — it keeps trying. With continuous reinforcement, the absence of a reward is a clear signal that something has changed, triggering faster extinction.

The Four Reinforcement Schedules

Partial reinforcement comes in four types, organised by two dimensions: Ratio (based on number of responses) vs. Interval (based on time), and Fixed (predictable) vs. Variable (unpredictable).

ScheduleRuleResponse PatternExamples
Fixed Ratio (FR) Reward after a set number of responses High, steady rate. Brief pause after each reward (organism "knows" it just got one). Then resumes. Factory worker paid per 10 cartons completed; telemarketer paid per 30 calls made
Variable Ratio (VR) Reward after an unpredictable number of responses — varies around an average Highest and most sustained response rate. Most resistant to extinction. No post-reward pause because you never know when the next one is coming. Slot machines; telemarketer paid per 3 secured sales (sales rate varies); social media likes
Fixed Interval (FI) Reward for the first response after a set time has passed, regardless of how many responses were made "Scallop" pattern — low responding after reward, then burst of activity near the deadline as the time approaches. Weekly/monthly paychecks; telemarketers paid $10/hr; year-end bonus; studying hard before a scheduled exam
Variable Interval (VI) Reward for first response after a varying time interval — unpredictable Steady, moderate responding. Second highest rate to VR. Most unpredictable — organism must always be ready. Random visit from boss who praises; pop quizzes; checking phone for messages

Ratio schedules produce higher response rates than interval schedules because the organism controls how quickly it can earn rewards — work faster, earn sooner.

Shaping

What if you want to condition a behaviour that never occurs spontaneously? You use shaping — reinforcing successive approximations that gradually move toward the final desired behaviour.

Example: Training a dog to walk on its hind legs. You can't wait for it to do that spontaneously. Instead, reinforce attention → sitting up → rearing slightly → standing → taking first steps. Each closer approximation is rewarded until the full behaviour is achieved.

The same logic applies to children: a parent might start by reinforcing any studying behaviour, then only good grades on assignments, then only good grades on exams — progressively raising the standard.

Punishment — Why It's Often Ineffective

Punishment can reduce behaviour, but it comes with significant drawbacks that make reinforcement the preferred approach:

  1. Inconsistency problem — the unwanted behaviour disappears only while the threat of punishment is present. Remove the parent or authority figure, the behaviour returns.
  2. Lure of rewards outweighs punishment — when the immediate pleasure of a behaviour is strong enough (e.g., drugs vs. risk of imprisonment), punishment loses its deterrent effect.
  3. Triggers escape and aggression — the punished organism tries to avoid or fight back against the punisher, damaging the relationship.
  4. Inhibits learning desirable behaviour — punishment tells the organism what not to do, but not what to do instead. It can cause helplessness and depression. And crucially: unwanted behaviour that goes unpunished becomes self-reinforcing — the organism may learn it can "get away with it."
Helpful Conditioning Principles
Prefer reinforcement (positive and negative) as the main strategy.
If punishment is necessary, make it: Swift · Certain · Limited in duration/intensity · Logical · Use negative punishment over physical.
Always pair punishment with clear modelling of the desired behaviour and reinforcement of that behaviour.

Cognitive Learning

Core Concept
According to cognitive psychology, some forms of learning must be explained as changes in mental processes, rather than as changes in behaviour alone. Pure behaviourism is insufficient — it cannot explain learning that happens without any observable action or reinforcement.

The key challenge to behaviourism came from researchers who noticed animals (and humans) sometimes solving problems without trial-and-error, learning without reinforcement, and learning without doing anything at all.

1 — Cognitive Maps (Tolman)

Definition
A cognitive map is a mental representation (internal image) built up through experience and stored internally. Tolman originally described it in the context of physical environments (a rat's mental layout of a maze), but the concept extends to any internal representation — of procedures, workflows, social situations, or causal relationships. It is the stored product of learning, built even without reinforcement.

Tolman's Rat Maze Experiment: Two groups of rats explored a maze. Group A received food at the end — they learned quickly. Group B received no food — they appeared to learn nothing. On day 11, food was introduced for Group B. They immediately matched Group A's performance — navigating the maze as efficiently as if they'd been rewarded all along.

What this proves: Group B had been building a mental map of the maze the whole time, even without reinforcement. The learning was happening internally and silently — only revealed when motivation (food) was introduced. This is the essence of latent learning.

2 — Latent Learning (Tolman)

Definition
Learning that occurs without reinforcement and without any hint that learning has taken place — it remains hidden until conditions arise that make expression worthwhile, safe, or necessary. Tolman originally described this as knowledge revealed when reinforcement is introduced, but the concept extends further: latent learning can be revealed by any shift in conditions — a reward being added, a threat being removed, necessity arising, or opportunity presenting itself.
Why Latent Learning Challenges Behaviourism

Operant conditioning predicts: no reinforcement → no learning. Tolman showed this is wrong. The rats learned the maze's layout simply by exploring — their cognitive processes were active the whole time, building a mental map, even without a reward to drive them. This means learning can be a purely internal, cognitive process independent of observable behaviour or consequences.

Everyday example: You may have memorised your neighbourhood's layout just by walking through it — without ever deliberately trying to learn it, and without being rewarded. The knowledge only becomes apparent the first time someone asks you for directions.

Extended Concept — What Reveals Latent Learning

Latent learning is not only revealed by a tangible reward being introduced. It can be revealed by any condition that makes expression of knowledge worthwhile or safe:

  • Reward introduced — Tolman's rats; employees given a task with a bonus attached
  • Threat removed — children in Bobo doll experiment asked to demonstrate safely; knowledge was present but suppressed by fear of punishment
  • Necessity arises — a person who never consciously learned a route navigates it confidently when their usual path is blocked
  • Opportunity presents itself — a child who watched a sibling play a video game sits down and plays competently when the sibling is away

What matters is that the knowledge was always there — built silently through observation or exploration. The revealing condition is not the cause of learning; it is simply what makes the existing learning visible.

3 — Insight Learning (Köhler)

Definition
Problem solving that occurs by suddenly perceiving new forms or relationships — a "flash of insight" (also called discovery learning). The solution appears all at once after mental reflection, not through gradual trial-and-error.

Köhler's Chimps: Köhler placed a banana just out of reach outside a chimp's cage, with a stick inside. Rather than randomly trying things, the chimp studied the situation — and then suddenly picked up the stick and used it to pull the banana in. No trial-and-error; the solution came from mentally re-organising the problem. The chimp "saw" how the stick and banana were related before acting.

This shows that learning can be purposive and goal-directed — the chimp wasn't just responding to past reinforcement, it was using its mind to reason toward a solution. Behaviourism alone cannot explain this.

4 — Observational Learning / Social Learning (Bandura)

Definition
A type of learning where we observe, imitate, or model another person's behaviour — and learn from the consequences we see them experience. Also called social learning. No direct reinforcement of our own behaviour is needed.

Bandura argued that cognitive processes play a central role. We don't just mindlessly copy; we observe, encode, store, and then decide whether to perform the behaviour based on what we expect the consequences to be.

Bandura's Four Internal Processes

Observational learning is not passive copying. Four internal steps determine whether observed behaviour is learned and eventually performed:

1
Attention The observer must pay attention to the model's behaviour. Without attention, nothing is encoded. Children attend more to models who are warm, powerful, nurturing, or similar to themselves. Televised models are particularly effective at capturing attention.
2
Retention The observer must store a mental representation of the observed behaviour in memory. This cognitive representation bridges the gap between watching and later reproducing — sometimes days or weeks later.
3
Reproduction The observer must have the physical and cognitive ability to reproduce the behaviour. A child watching a gymnast may retain the memory but lack the physical capability to reproduce it precisely.
4
Motivation The observer must be motivated to perform the behaviour. Motivation can be external (a reward) or intrinsic (curiosity, opportunity, necessity). Even if the first three steps are complete, behaviour will not be performed without sufficient motivation.
Bandura's Bobo Doll Experiment

Children watched a film of a woman beating a Bobo doll. They were then left alone with the doll. Three conditions were used:

ConditionWhat Children SawBehaviour Toward Bobo
Model Rewarded Adult was praised for aggression High aggression — imitated directly
No Consequences Nothing happened to adult after aggression High aggression — equal to reward condition. Simply getting away with it is enough — absence of punishment functions as implicit approval.
Model Punished Adult was scolded for aggression Lower aggression — children hesitated, expecting similar punishment for themselves
Critical Finding — Learning ≠ Performance
When Bandura asked all children to show exactly what they had seen — with no threat of punishment — every group demonstrated equal knowledge of how to be aggressive. Children who had seen the model punished had learned the behaviour just as fully — they simply chose not to perform it.

Learning is the internal acquisition of knowledge. Performance is the external expression of that knowledge. They are not the same thing. Performance depends on perceived consequences — not on what has been learned.
Inhibited Performance vs. Latent Learning
The children who saw the model punished illustrate inhibited performance — their learning was fully present but suppressed by anticipated punishment. When the researcher invited them to demonstrate safely, the suppressing condition was removed and the knowledge was revealed.

This connects to latent learning: the knowledge was stored silently all along and only expressed when conditions changed. Crucially, the suppression is conditional, not permanent — should conditions arise where children sense they will not be punished, that stored knowledge of aggressive behaviour can surface at any time.
Implication for Violent Media

Most violent media does not consistently show perpetrators being punished — children are predominantly exposed to the reward and no-consequences conditions, both of which promote imitation. Even when punishment is shown, the learning still occurs and is stored as latent knowledge. Should conditions change — unsupervised settings, peer encouragement, anonymity — that stored knowledge can surface. Prevention at the point of encoding (regulating exposure) is more reliable than relying on suppression after the learning has already taken place. The less violent behaviour children observe and encode, the less there is available to be expressed when conditions become favourable.

Two Key Departures from Behaviourism

1. Learning can occur without any overt change in behaviour — children learned aggression without ever being aggressive themselves.

2. Cognitive processes are central — attention, retention, reproduction, and motivation all shape whether observed learning becomes action. Behaviourism cannot account for this internal mediation.

How the Four Cognitive Concepts Relate

All four concepts involve hidden internal processes — which is what makes them cognitive learning and why they collectively challenge behaviourism. But each describes a different aspect of that internal processing:

ConceptWhat it describesKey feature
Latent Learning The conditions of learning — occurs without reinforcement, revealed when conditions become favourable Knowledge is hidden until expression is safe, necessary, or rewarded
Cognitive Map The stored product of learning — the internal mental representation itself What latent and observational learning produce and store; broader than just physical environments
Insight Learning The restructuring of stored knowledge to solve a new problem Sudden "aha" — existing cognitive maps are reorganised; no trial-and-error needed
Observational Learning The source of learning — acquired by watching a model, not through direct experience Mediated by four internal steps: attention, retention, reproduction, motivation
The Thread Connecting All Four

Latent learning and observational learning often operate simultaneously — a person can learn by watching (observational) while that knowledge remains hidden until conditions allow expression (latent). In both cases, cognitive maps are being built as the stored product. And when a new problem requires connecting those stored maps in a new way, insight occurs. They are different facets of the same underlying cognitive system — separated by researchers for clarity, but deeply interconnected in practice.

Activity 1.7 — Applying Learning Principles

Train a young dog to press the bin pedal
Use shaping with positive reinforcement: reward the dog for sniffing the bin → reward for pawing near the pedal → reward only for touching the pedal → reward only for pressing it down. Successive approximations toward the final behaviour.
Train a 2-year-old to throw rubbish in the bin
Use shaping + positive reinforcement: praise and reward each step — walking toward bin, picking up rubbish, dropping it in. Keep instructions simple and consistent. Model the behaviour (observational learning) so the child sees what to do.
Stop a dog from chewing socks
Use positive punishment (aversive sound/spray when chewing occurs) combined with providing an acceptable chew toy and positively reinforcing chewing the toy. Punishment alone won't work — you must also redirect to the correct behaviour.
Stop a 5-year-old from chewing socks
Use negative punishment (remove a favourite activity when sock-chewing occurs). Tell them clearly why the privilege is being removed and what the correct behaviour is. Reinforce the desired behaviour (leaving socks alone) with praise.
