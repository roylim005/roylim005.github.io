10.1

Evaluating the World: Attitudes

Definition
Social Psychology — the study of how we think and behave in social situations.
Attitudes — evaluative beliefs we hold about people, places, and things (our liking or disliking).

How Attitudes Are Acquired (Learning Theory)

Classical Conditioning

Emotional/physiological responses attached to stimuli shape attitudes. E.g., a child's face paired with a smiley emoji → more positive feelings toward that child.

Operant Conditioning

Rewarded attitudes strengthen; punished attitudes weaken. E.g., friends applauding pro-green behaviour → stronger eco attitude.

Observational Learning

We model attitudes of those around us. Research shows our attitudes closely resemble our parents' and peers' attitudes.

Attitude–Behaviour Consistency

  • We often behave inconsistently with our attitudes, especially under social pressure.
  • Consistency is higher when: the attitude is strong & accessible, behaviour is specific to the attitude, and social pressure is low.

Attitude Change

Cognitive Consistency
We are motivated to avoid contradictions in our attitudes and between attitudes and behaviour.
Cognitive Dissonance Theory (Festinger, 1957)
Inconsistency among attitudes (or attitude vs. behaviour) creates an unpleasant state — dissonance. We reduce it by: (1) changing behaviour, (2) changing attitude, or (3) adding new beliefs.

Applied example: SBIP eating-disorder program — repeatedly criticising ultra-thin ideals created dissonance → women reduced endorsement of those ideals.

Persuasion & Routes to Attitude Change

Central Route

Careful, critical evaluation of argument logic. Leads to durable attitude change. Used when motivated & cognitively able.

Peripheral Route

Persuaded by superficial cues: communicator attractiveness, ad aesthetics, ease of navigation. Used when uninvolved or cognitively depleted.

Variable TypeKey Factors That Increase Persuasion
CommunicatorAttractive, credible, expert, not obviously trying to persuade (unless attractive)
MessageStrong logic (central route); two-sided arguments that address objections; ease of peripheral cues
AudiencePeripheral processing, mood, self-esteem, self-monitoring, intelligence — all interact in complex ways
10.2

Forming Impressions of Others

Impression Formation
How we understand and make judgments about others. We observe behaviour → assign a cause (attribution) → form an impression.

Attribution Types

Trait Attribution

Behaviour attributed to a person's internal traits, abilities, or characteristics.
"She yelled because she's aggressive."

Situational Attribution

Behaviour attributed to environmental/situational factors.
"She yelled because the server spilled coffee on her."

Attribution Biases (Heuristics)

Fundamental Attribution Error (FAE)
Over-attributing others' behaviour to traits while under-weighting situational factors.
Note: More common in individualistic cultures (Western) than collectivistic cultures (India, Japan). Education & mindfulness can reduce FAE.
Actor / Observer Bias
As actors (ourselves), we favour situational attributions for our own behaviour.
As observers, we favour trait attributions for others' behaviour.
Why? We can't see our own behaviour — attention is on the environment when we act; on the person when we observe.
Self-Serving Bias
We make trait attributions for successes ("I'm smart") and situational attributions for failures ("The exam was unfair").
Protects self-esteem. Universal across cultures. Reduced in major depressive disorder.
Downside: Excess self-serving bias → narcissism, relationship difficulties.
10.3

Prejudice: Why Can't We All Just Get Along?

Stereotype

A schema for a group of people stored in memory. The cognitive component of an attitude about a group. Helpful in general; problematic when applied rigidly.

Prejudice

A stereotype gone awry — biased, negative stereotype + negative emotion toward all members of a group, regardless of individual qualities.

Discrimination

The behavioural expression of a prejudice. E.g., refusing to hire a qualified person because of gender.

Modern Racism
Aversive Racism (Gaertner & Dovidio): Europeans who outwardly support equality may still feel unconscious negative emotions toward minorities — motivating subtle discrimination and avoidance. More likely under stress.
Stereotype Threat (Steele)
Fear that others will judge you by prejudicial stereotypes about your group → anxiety → impaired performance (a self-fulfilling prophecy).
Examples: Women on maths tasks when outnumbered by men; African Americans in academic settings.
Interventions: Self-affirmation programmes; reducing situational cues of stereotyping.

How Prejudice Develops

  • Classical conditioning: Seeing negative non-verbal cues toward a group → conditioned negative feeling.
  • Operant conditioning: Peers reinforcing or punishing prejudicial attitudes.
  • Observational learning: Modelling parents and authority figures (Jane Elliot classroom study).
  • In-group bias: We favour our own group; out-group members seen as homogenous (out-group homogeneity bias).
  • Realistic-conflict theory: Competition for resources fuels prejudice; minority out-groups become scapegoats.
Classic Study — Robber's Cave (Sherif, 1961)
Boys at summer camp split into Eagles & Rattlers → competition → strong prejudice & hostility. Resolution: superordinate goals (shared goals requiring cooperation) dissolved prejudice. Basis of the jigsaw classroom.

Reducing Prejudice — Optimal Contact Conditions

  • Groups need each other (mutual interdependence).
  • Common superordinate goal requiring joint effort.
  • Equal-status cooperation ("shoulder to shoulder").
  • Contact is hospitable, informal, and emotionally positive.
  • Sustained over time; norms promote harmony and respect.
10.4

Being Drawn to Others: The Nature of Attraction

Proximity & Mere Exposure Effect

The more we encounter someone, the more we tend to like them. Physical closeness → more exposure → greater attraction. Holds across cultures.

Similarity

"Birds of a feather flock together." We gravitate toward similar age, status, intelligence, race, religion, attitudes, and attractiveness. Reduces cognitive dissonance; boosts self-esteem.

Physical Attractiveness

Strongest predictor of a second date (Walster et al., 1966). Induces the halo effect — attractive people perceived as kinder, smarter, more sociable. Preference may be partly innate (2-month-old infants prefer attractive faces).

Matching Hypothesis

We tend to form relationships with people whose attractiveness level is comparable to our own. True for both romantic and friendship pairs.

The "Chemistry" of Love
Three emotional systems with distinct neurochemistry:
Sex drive → governed by oestrogens & androgens
Romantic attraction → ↑ dopamine & norepinephrine, ↓ serotonin
Attachment / companionate love → oxytocin, vasopressin, neuropeptides
10.5

Group Influence

Group Norms

Rules (explicit or unwritten) guiding group members' behaviour. Breaking norms risks ridicule, persuasion, or ostracism.

Cohesiveness

Degree to which members want to maintain group membership. High cohesiveness → high conformity pressure.

Conformity — Asch (1951)

Classic Study
Participants matched line lengths; confederates deliberately chose the wrong line. 74% conformed at least once. Conformity peaked with only 3 confederates. Individualistic cultures conform less; collectivistic cultures conform more (in face-to-face settings).
Normative Conformity

Change behaviour to fit the norm — no real attitude change. Motivated by desire to be liked.

Informational Conformity

Behaviour and attitude change — genuinely persuaded by the majority. More likely when uncertain or lacking confidence.

Deindividuation (Stanford Prison Experiment — Zimbardo, 1971)
When people are in roles, wear uniforms, or are anonymous, behaviour is controlled more by external situational norms than by internal values. Guards became abusive; prisoners became submissive — within days. Experiment cancelled early.

Group Performance

Social Facilitation

Presence of others enhances performance on familiar/easy tasks; impairs it on complex or unfamiliar tasks (e.g., public speaking).

Social Loafing

Group members work less hard when individual effort is not evaluated. Reduced by rewarding individual contribution and fostering prosocial traits.

Groupthink (Janis, 1982)

Group fixates on one decision without examining alternatives. Promoted by: isolation, high cohesiveness, dictatorial leadership, stress. → Poor decisions.

Requests & Demands: Compliance and Obedience

Compliance Techniques

TechniqueHow It WorksWhy It Works
Foot-in-the-Door Small request first → then larger request Self-image shifts to "I'm someone who complies"; avoiding dissonance with new self-image
Door-in-the-Face Very large request first (rejected) → smaller target request Perceptual contrast; norm of reciprocity (they conceded, so we concede); guilt reduction
Low-Balling Agree to a deal → terms change unfavourably Commitment to original agreement; perceptual contrast; don't want to break promise
That's-Not-All Original offer sweetened with extras Norm of reciprocity — seller does something nice, buyer feels obligated to say yes

Obedience — Milgram (1963)

Classic Study
Participants ("teachers") instructed to deliver shocks up to 450 V to a "learner" (confederate). Authority figure (experimenter in lab coat) prodded them calmly to continue.

Result: 65% of participants delivered the maximum 450 V. No participant refused before 300 V. Replicated with women, different cities, other Western cultures.

Factors That Increased Obedience

  • Authority figure present — relieves personal responsibility; intimidates.
  • Physical proximity of authority — obedience dropped from 65% → 27.5% when experimenter gave orders by phone.
  • Incremental escalation — foot-in-the-door logic; each small step makes the next harder to refuse ("slippery slope").
  • Psychological distance from victim — couldn't see the "learner"; holding victim's hand → only 30% went to max.
  • Time pressure — orders began immediately, leaving little time to reflect.
  • Low sense of personal responsibility — those who disobeyed expressed stronger personal responsibility and concern for the learner.
10.7

Aggression: Hurting Others

Definition
An action intended to cause harm to another person who does not want to be harmed.
Instrumental Aggression

Aimed at achieving a goal (e.g., hitting a sibling to grab a toy).

Hostile Aggression

Motivated solely by the desire to hurt (e.g., bullying for its own sake).

Biological Explanations

  • Testosterone: Weak correlation with aggression in males; not consistently causal. Physical size may be the mediating variable.
  • Serotonin: Low levels associated with difficulty controlling aggressive impulses (and self-harm/suicide). SSRIs increase empathy and reduce aggression in some studies.
  • Childhood abuse & brain damage: Abuse → structural abnormalities in amygdala, frontal lobe, hippocampus → increased aggression risk.

Learning Explanations

  • Observational learning (Bandura): Bobo doll study — children mimic aggressive models. ~70% of prime-time TV contains violence.
  • Cognitive neoassociation theory: Cues (e.g., guns) become associated in memory with aggression → seeing those cues primes aggressive thoughts.
  • Hostile attribution bias: Maltreated children perceive others as hostile → more easily angered → more aggression.
Frustration–Aggression Hypothesis (Dollard et al., 1939)
Frustration activates a motive to harm. Likely directed at the perceived source of frustration. High-stress situations (e.g., poverty) with poor coping skills → greatest risk of abuse.
10.8

Choosing to Help Others: Prosocial Behaviour

Prosocial Behaviour

Behaviour that helps others. May include personal benefit.

Altruism

Helping others without considering personal benefit.

Kitty Genovese Case (1964) — Context
Murder reportedly witnessed by 38 neighbours, none of whom helped in time. This apparent apathy prompted Latané & Darley to study helping behaviour scientifically.

Latané & Darley's 5 Steps to Helping

  • 1. Notice that something unusual is happening.
  • 2. Interpret it correctly as an emergency requiring help.
  • 3. Feel responsible to intervene.
  • 4. Know how to help.
  • 5. Implement the helping strategy.
Bystander Effect (Latané & Darley)
As the number of bystanders increases, the probability any one person will help decreases.
Diffusion of Responsibility

Each bystander shares responsibility equally → each individual bears less. Fix: Single out one bystander by name/description to assign full responsibility.

Pluralistic Ignorance

Seeing others do nothing → each person concludes there is no emergency → nobody acts.
