What is a Mental Health Disorder?

A mental health disorder is a dysfunction in thinking, emotions, and/or social behaviour that impairs functioning and is not culturally expected.

There is no sharp line between normal and abnormal behaviour — it is a judgment call based on degree and frequency. A key practical test: does it affect your daily functioning? If yes → seek help.

1 · Not Typical / Culturally Expected
  • Behaviour is statistically rare compared to most people
  • Violates social and cultural norms
  • ⚠️ Norms vary widely across cultures and across time
  • Judging on this alone is problematic
2 · Distress
  • Causes significant personal pain to the individual
  • Or causes distress to others (e.g. chronic lying, violence)
  • Often what drives a person to seek treatment
3 · Dysfunction
  • Interferes with ability to function — work, school, relationships
  • The more difficulty functioning, the more likely it meets criteria
  • Most important practical indicator
Key exam point: All three indicators (the "3Ds") are used together. Judgement is needed when 2 or more are present. Degree and frequency matter — a behaviour that is mild and rare is less likely to meet disorder criteria than one that is severe and persistent.

Models of Mental Health Disorder

Demon Model
  • Behaviour is the work of Satan / evil spirits
  • Historical (pre-scientific) view
  • Treatment: exorcism / punishment
Medical Model (Biological)
  • MHD viewed as a disease — like any physical illness
  • Caused by: genetics, chemical imbalance, brain dysfunction
  • Treatment: medication, surgery
Weaknesses: "Doctor knows best" → patient becomes passive · may lead to unnecessary drug therapy · does not encourage good coping skills
Biopsychosocial Model
  • Integrates all perspectives
  • No single cause — it is a combination of:
  • Biological (brain, genes, chemistry)
  • Psychological (thoughts, emotions, learning)
  • Social/cultural (environment, gender, poverty)

This is the currently accepted approach. Most disorders cannot be explained by one factor alone.

Psychological Model — 4 Perspectives

Psychoanalytic
  • Present problems caused by unresolved conflicts in the past
  • Tied to early relationships and childhood trauma
  • Memories are buried (repressed) in the unconscious mind
  • Goal of therapy: make the unconscious conscious
Social Learning
  • Abnormal behaviour is learned, just as normal behaviour is
  • Through associations, reinforcements, and punishments (classical & operant conditioning)
  • Observational learning also plays a role
Cognitive
  • Focuses on internal processes: expectations, biases, thinking errors
  • Cognitive errors (distortions) result in maladaptive behaviour
  • Negative automatic thinking patterns maintain disorders
Humanistic
  • Disorder stems from incongruence between ideal self and actual self
  • Distorted feeling of being unable to achieve true potential
  • Failure to fulfil basic needs blocks self-actualisation
Sociocultural Approach

Takes into account the social and cultural factors that shape behaviour: age, race, gender, social roles, socioeconomic status, poverty, discrimination, and environmental stressors. Internal biological and psychological processes can only be fully understood in the context of society and culture.

📋

Classifying Mental Disorders — The DSM

The DSM-V-TR (2022) — Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders — is the most widely used system. It classifies disorders by their mental and behavioural symptoms.
Why classify?
  • When a problem is identified and classified, appropriate treatment can be given
  • Provides a common language for clinicians and researchers
  • Helps study possible underlying causes
  • Needed for insurance, resource allocation
Limitations
  • Diagnosis can be biased by culture, race, or gender
  • Mental illness may be influenced by social and cultural variables
  • Diagnosis can carry stigma
  • The suffering of the afflicted is real regardless of debate
Indicators of abnormality reminder: No sharp line between normal and abnormal. Judgment call when 2+ indicators present. Degree & frequency determine clinical significance. The key question: does it affect daily functioning?

Anxiety, OCD & Trauma-Related Disorders

Anxiety disorders differ from everyday worry in their focus, duration, intensity, and degree of functional impairment. The key is that anxiety is out of proportion and significantly disrupts daily life.

1

Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD)

Core feature: Feel anxious most of the time about many different things — major and minor — without a specific known cause.

  • Persistent and pervasive feelings of anxiety, without a clear external trigger
  • Worry is difficult to control
  • Symptoms: restlessness, easy fatigue, difficulty concentrating, irritability, muscle tension, sleep problems
  • Example worry: "Will my children get good jobs? Will they get partners? Will they grow up to be pretty?"
  • Must be present more days than not for 6 months
  • Women have higher rates than men
2

Panic Disorder & Agoraphobia

Core feature: Abrupt (peaks within 10 minutes) episodes of intense fear/discomfort with no connection to present events — sometimes called "fear of fear."

Panic Disorder
  • Sudden overwhelming panic — heart pounds, ringing in ears, difficulty breathing, numbness
  • Person stays away from triggers to avoid another attack
  • No identifiable specific cause
Agoraphobia
  • Fear of places — malls, cinemas, grocery stores
  • Avoidance of places where escape may be difficult if panic occurs
  • Can lead to being housebound
3

Specific Phobia & Social Anxiety Disorder

Specific Phobia
  • Irrational fear of a specific object or situation (not just extreme fear)
  • Person recognises the fear is unreasonable but cannot stop it
  • Example: pilot who panics going above the 10th floor of a building
  • Examples: patrol car, trolley bag, feathers, spiders
  • Key words: Persistent, Irrational
  • Markedly interferes with ability to function
Social Anxiety Disorder (Social Phobia)
  • Irrational, persistent fear of being negatively evaluated by others
  • Fear of public speaking, eating in public, meeting new people
  • Extreme fear of rejection, embarrassment, or humiliation
4

Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD) & Hoarding

OCD
  • Obsessions: involuntary, intrusive, nonsensical thoughts/images/urges that one tries to resist or eliminate
  • Most common: contamination & aggression thoughts
  • Compulsions: actions performed to suppress thoughts and provide relief
  • Most common: cleaning/washing or checking rituals
  • Compulsions can interfere significantly with everyday living
  • Associated with meticulous and perfectionist personality
Hoarding Disorder
  • Persistent difficulty discarding possessions even when of little value
  • Having to discard possessions causes significant distress and anxiety
  • Living space becomes cluttered and unusable
5

Posttraumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD)

Develops after exposure to a terrifying event (assault, disaster, combat, sudden death of a loved one).

Traumatic Event
→
Initial Reactions
→
Intrusive Memories
→
Avoidance / Numbing
→
Mood / Cognition Change
→
Heightened Arousal
  • Intrusive memories: flashbacks, nightmares, distressing thoughts triggered by reminders
  • Avoidance/Numbing: avoid situations/thoughts associated with trauma; emotional withdrawal
  • Hypervigilance: always on guard, difficulty sleeping and concentrating
  • Anniversaries of trauma can trigger symptoms

Somatic & Dissociative Disorders

Somatic Symptom & Related Disorders

Physical complaints for which there is no apparent physical cause — the key idea is Anxiety → Physical Symptoms. The pain felt is real; the somatic symptoms are neither intentionally produced nor faked.

Somatic Symptom Disorder
  • Psychological problems appearing as bodily symptoms or physical complaints
  • Cannot be explained in terms of a physical disease
  • The pain "felt" can be very real
  • Symptoms appear unconscious to the patient — not intentional
Illness Anxiety Disorder
  • Excessive concern about having a serious medical illness
  • Frequently seeks medical reassurance even in the absence of evidence
  • Convinced of a serious illness — not just one or two symptoms
  • May refuse to see doctors, or doctor-shop
Conversion Disorder
  • Emotional distress expressed through physical symptoms / malfunctioning
  • Often involves sensory-motor loss: blindness, paralysis, inability to speak
  • No organic pathology — "all in the mind"
  • Stressor → Loss of sensation (speech, mobility, sight…)
  • Patient may repress the stressor or be unaware of its impact

Dissociative Disorders

"Fragmentation" of the personality. The mind splits off uncomfortable feelings or memories and loses them to conscious recall. Typically linked to severe stress or trauma.

1

Dissociative Amnesia

  • Stressful event → selective memory loss
  • Immediate and remote memory remain intact
  • Memory of a specific traumatic period is lost
2

Dissociative Fugue

  • Dissociative amnesia plus "flight" from home, family, and job
  • Person may wander and assume a new identity
  • Can't recall past life or identity
3

Depersonalisation Disorder

  • Sensation of mind and body having separated
  • Person feels like an external observer of their own body
  • Like being in a dream / out-of-body experience
  • Associated with OCD
4

Dissociative Identity Disorder (DID)

  • Individual displays multiple identities (alters)
  • A dominant self emerges to protect against trauma
  • Each alter contrasts with the original self
  • Each operates within its own consciousness
  • Alters emerge suddenly under stress
  • Almost all patients have documented severe childhood abuse

Mood Disorders

Mood disorders are defined and diagnosed by the occurrence of one or more mood episodes, or periods of abnormal happiness or sadness. Disturbance in mood is their prominent feature.
Major Depression (Unipolar)

Form of depression that does not alternate with mania. Consistently inappropriate to the situation. Accounts for a large share of mental hospital admissions.

  • Feel worthless — exaggerated guilt and self-blame
  • Lack appetite — significant weight change
  • Withdrawal — loss of interest in usual activities
  • Sleeping disorder — too much or too little
  • Agitated or lethargic motor activity
  • Suicidal thoughts
  • Also includes Seasonal Affective Disorder (SAD) — linked to deprivation of sunlight; often shows opposite symptoms (increased appetite, weight gain, more sleep)
Who is more vulnerable? Women twice as likely as men · Adolescent females 13–15 especially at risk · Lower socioeconomic status · People with a history of adverse life events
Bipolar Disorder

Extremes of mood, from mania to depression. A "roller coaster of mood" — also called manic-depressive disorder. The mania and depression typically alternate, though in rare cases they may coexist.

  • Manic episode: Happy, confident, energetic, hyperactive, talkative, decreased need for sleep, inflated self-esteem, risky behaviours
  • Depressive episode: Not interested, no energy, withdrawal — same as major depression symptoms
  • Both episodes must occur for a bipolar diagnosis
  • Men and women are equally likely to be diagnosed
  • Onset typically in late adolescence / early adulthood

Mood Spectrum

MANIC
EUTHYMIC (normal)
DEPRESSED

Bipolar swings between Manic & Depressed poles  ·  Unipolar (Major) Depression stays below the euthymic line

Schizophrenia

Schizophrenia has major disturbances in thought, emotion, and behaviour. It is a psychotic disorder — the person is out of touch with reality, forms their own reality, and is unable to distinguish real from unreal.
❌ Myths
  • Multiple personality (that's DID — a different disorder)
  • Dangerous and violent by nature
  • Result of bad parenting
✓ Facts
  • Psychotic split from reality
  • Unable to distinguish real from unreal
  • May hallucinate or have delusional ideas
  • May be apathetic, losing interest in things
  • Unity of mind is broken — evident in their language
  • Sufferer is often unaware of their condition

Symptoms of Schizophrenia

✦ POSITIVE SYMPTOMS — "they have what others do not"
  • Delusions: mind feels controlled from outside; thoughts spoken out loud; ideas loosely associated
  • Hallucinations: both pleasant and unpleasant; responsive to voices; voices feel real and have power over them. Auditory hallucinations most common.
  • Disorganised speech (loose associations / "word salad")
  • Disordered/catatonic behaviour
✦ NEGATIVE SYMPTOMS — "they lack what others have"
  • Loss of energy, motivation and self-care
  • Withdrawal — occupied with internal thoughts
  • Emotional changes / blunted affect (flat emotions, immobile facial expression)
  • Poverty of speech (alogia) — brief, empty replies
  • Emotional/social withdrawal, apathy
Key distinction: Positive symptoms = something added/distorted (delusions, hallucinations). Negative symptoms = something absent/reduced (emotion, speech, motivation). People with predominantly positive symptoms tend to respond better to medication.

Causes (Biopsychosocial)

Biological
  • Strong genetic component — family, twin & adoption studies show high heritability
  • Identical twin: ~48% chance if other twin has it
  • Neurotransmitters: excess dopamine (positive symptoms); also glutamate dysfunction
  • Enlarged brain ventricles (reduce overall brain size)
  • Frontal and temporal lobe dysfunction
Psychological / Developmental
  • Prenatal factors: birth complications, maternal stress, prenatal viruses
  • Brain synapses are trimmed in adolescence — hypothesis is that too many are trimmed in schizophrenia
  • Genes interact with prenatal environment
Social / Environmental
  • Quality of family communications can influence onset in high-risk individuals
  • Critical, hostile families = higher susceptibility
  • Chronic stress from low-income lifestyle can trigger relapses
  • Family/environment do not cause schizophrenia but affect susceptibility
