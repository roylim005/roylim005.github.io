Classical Conditioning

Core Idea: A neutral stimulus becomes a conditioned stimulus by being repeatedly paired with an unconditioned stimulus ‚Äî learning through association.

The Conditioning Process (Pavlov's Dogs)

BEFORE CONDITIONING
Bell (NS) ‚Üí No response
Food (US) ‚Üí Salivation (UR)
‚Üì Repeated Pairings
DURING CONDITIONING
Bell (NS) + Food (US) ‚Üí Salivation (UR)
‚Üì After enough pairings
AFTER CONDITIONING
Bell (CS) ‚Üí Salivation (CR)
‚Üì What happens next?
üîä Stimulus Generalization
Similar bells also cause CR
üéØ Stimulus Discrimination
Only THIS bell causes CR
üìâ Extinction
CS presented without US ‚Üí CR fades
üîÑ Counterconditioning
CS paired with new US ‚Üí new CR

Key Conditions for Classical Conditioning

‚è±Ô∏è Contiguity
NS/CS and US must be close together in time. Brief delay is best. Too long a gap breaks the association.
üîó Contingency
NS/CS must reliably predict the US. If CS sometimes appears without US, conditioning weakens.
üåø Taste Aversion
Food (CS) paired with illness/nausea (US) ‚Üí food becomes CS for nausea (CR). Can occur after ONE pairing. Even hours-long delay can work!
Mnemonic for Terms: NS ‚Üí CS (neutral becomes conditioned) | UR ‚Üí CR (same response, different name)
"The stimulus CHANGES name; the response gets the 'C' label after learning."

Operant Conditioning

Core Idea: Behavior is shaped by its consequences. Reinforcement increases behavior; punishment decreases behavior. The key question: are you ADDING or REMOVING something?

The Four Consequences

‚úÖ POSITIVE REINFORCEMENT
ADD something desirable ‚Üí behavior INCREASES
Give child candy for tidying room ‚Üí tidying increases
üîµ NEGATIVE REINFORCEMENT
REMOVE something undesirable ‚Üí behavior INCREASES
Buckle seatbelt ‚Üí annoying beeping stops ‚Üí buckling increases
üö´ POSITIVE PUNISHMENT
ADD something undesirable ‚Üí behavior DECREASES
Speed ‚Üí get speeding ticket ‚Üí speeding decreases
‚ö†Ô∏è NEGATIVE PUNISHMENT
REMOVE something desirable ‚Üí behavior DECREASES
Teen breaks curfew ‚Üí phone taken away ‚Üí curfew-breaking decreases
‚ö†Ô∏è Critical Distinction: Negative Reinforcement ‚â† Punishment!
Negative Reinforcement INCREASES behavior by removing something BAD.
Both types of Reinforcement INCREASE behavior. Both types of Punishment DECREASE behavior.

Key Operant Concepts

ü™ú Shaping
Reinforcing successive approximations toward a desired final behavior. Used to teach novel behaviors that the subject can't perform spontaneously. Example: Teaching a dog to roll over step by step.
üèÖ Primary vs. Secondary Reinforcers
Primary: Inherently satisfying (food, water, warmth, sex)

Secondary: Learned value through association with primary (money, praise, grades, tokens)
ü™ô Token Economy
System using tokens (secondary reinforcers) exchangeable for primary reinforcers. Also teaches delay of gratification ‚Äî waiting for a larger, later reward.
üêæ Instinctive Drift
Conditioned behaviors gradually replaced by instinctive behaviors of the species. Shows limits of operant conditioning ‚Äî biology can override learning.

Schedules of Reinforcement

Key Principle: HOW you deliver reinforcement shapes the pattern and durability of behavior. Variable schedules create the most persistent behavior.
Continuous
Pattern: Every response reinforced
Response rate: Moderate
Extinction: FASTEST üíÄ
Best for: Teaching NEW behaviors
Example: Every time you put money in, gumball comes out
Fixed Ratio (FR)
Pattern: After fixed # of responses
Response rate: High, with pause after reward
Extinction: Moderate
Example: FR5 = reward after every 5 responses. Piece-rate pay.
‚≠ê Variable Ratio (VR)
Pattern: After unpredictable # of responses
Response rate: HIGHEST üöÄ
Extinction: SLOWEST (most resistant)
Example: Slot machines, gambling, fishing
Fixed Interval (FI)
Pattern: First response after fixed TIME
Response rate: Low, scallop pattern (burst before deadline)
Example: Weekly paycheck, studying before exam
Variable Interval (VI)
Pattern: First response after unpredictable TIME
Response rate: Moderate, very steady
Extinction: High resistance
Example: Checking email, social media, pop quizzes

Observational Learning (Bandura)

Core Idea: Learning can occur by watching others ‚Äî no direct experience or reinforcement needed. Cognition matters in learning.

Bobo Doll Experiment

Children watch adult model punch, kick, yell at Bobo doll
‚Üì
Children placed in room with Bobo doll (slightly frustrated first)
‚Üì
Children imitate the same aggressive behaviors, even inventing new ones
‚Üì Key finding
Learning happened through observation alone
No direct reinforcement required
Learning can be latent ‚Äî stored and expressed later

Bandura's 4 Conditions for Observational Learning

üëÅÔ∏è 1. Attention
Must notice and focus on the model's behavior. We pay more attention to models who are similar to us, attractive, or high status.
üß† 2. Retention
Must remember the observed behavior. Memory, mental imagery, and rehearsal all help retain what we've observed.
üí™ 3. Reproduction
Must be physically capable of performing the behavior. Watching a gymnast doesn't mean you can do it ‚Äî physical ability matters.
üéØ 4. Motivation
Must want to perform the behavior. Vicarious reinforcement (seeing others rewarded) increases motivation. Vicarious punishment decreases it.
