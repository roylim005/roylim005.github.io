01

What is Personality? Both

Core Definition — from Slides

Personality is the quality that causes consistency in behaviour under different situations and across time. It is a person's general style of interacting with the world — the essence of who the person is.

Even as physical appearance changes, personality tends to remain stable. The Study Unit uses the Phineas Gage case to open the personality chapter: in 1848, an iron rod passed through the frontal lobes of his brain. His memory and reasoning remained intact, but his personality transformed completely — from even-tempered and conscientious to hostile and impulsive. This revealed the link between the prefrontal cortex and personality.

The slides also ask: "I am cheerful, I like to socialise, I am an extrovert — 100%?" The point is that personality traits describe general tendencies, not absolute rules. Nobody expresses any trait 100% of the time in all contexts.

Key insight from slides

Psychologists have proposed several theoretical explanations for personality — none has the whole truth. Each theory explains the person from a different angle, like different people describing different parts of the same elephant. Each perspective contributes something the others cannot.

3.2 Allport's Three Types of Traits Study Unit

Gordon Allport (1961) identified three types of traits. A common error is listing only two — all three must be known.

Cardinal Traits
A single, all-important trait that dominates every area of a person's life. Very few people have a cardinal trait.

Example: Abraham Lincoln — honesty; Napoleon — ambition. The trait is so pervasive it defines the person across all contexts without exception.
Central Traits
A combination of 5–10 traits that accounts for the uniqueness of a person's personality. Most people are described through central traits.

Examples: confident, warm, sociable, cautious, optimistic, passionate, serious, reliable.
Secondary Traits
Tendencies that appear only in certain situations — less consistent and more context-specific than central traits.

Example: a generally calm person who becomes aggressive only when frustrated in competitive situations.
Why are cardinal traits rare?

It is unusual for one tendency to override context in every area of life. Most people moderate behaviour by situation — a cardinal trait does not allow this moderation at all. That pervasiveness is what makes it rare.

3.3 Cattell's 16 Personality Factors (16PF) Study Unit

Raymond Cattell (1946) measured personality along 16 trait dimensions (Study Unit Table 3.2). The Big Five simplifies this into five broader factors. Nature vs Nurture in Personality Study Unit

Learning Outcome

The Study Unit explicitly states this as a learning outcome and directs students to read the textbook on genetic contributions to personality and environmental influence on personality traits.

4.1 The Nature Argument — Genetic Contributions

Twin studies
Identical twins reared apart show remarkable personality similarities when reunited. More similarities between identical twins than fraternal twins — supports a genetic component. Famous example: the "Jim twins" — both drove Chevrolets, smoked the same brand, worked as deputies, shared the same hobbies despite separate upbringings.
Adoption studies
Adopted children show more personality similarities to their biological parents (genes, no shared environment) than to adoptive parents (environment, no shared genes) — suggesting personality traits are moderately heritable.
Temperament
Newborns show differences in temperament strongly linked to genes and stable over time — suggesting some personality dispositions are present from birth.
Neuroscience
Serotonin transporter gene is linked to anxiety-related traits. Dopamine receptor gene is linked to novelty-seeking. Specific genes influence neurotransmitter functioning that shapes personality dimensions.

4.2 The Nurture Argument — Environmental Influence

Person-situation interaction
Even stable traits behave differently depending on context. The same trait can produce different behaviours in different environments — showing that environment shapes how traits are expressed.
Age & culture
Gradual personality change occurs across adulthood. People generally become more conscientious, agreeable, and emotionally stable with age. Culture shapes which traits are valued and how they are expressed.
Social learning
Bandura showed personality is shaped through observational learning — watching others and outcomes of their behaviour. Personality patterns can be acquired by modelling, not just genes.
Parenting
Rogers showed that conditional vs unconditional positive regard shapes the self-concept and personality trajectory throughout childhood.
Conclusion — Interaction Position

Neither nature nor nurture alone determines personality. Both contribute, and the real story is in their interaction: multiple genes working simultaneously together with multiple environmental and developmental events produce personality.

Freud's Psychodynamic Theory Both

Core idea (from slides): Nothing we do as adults is by chance. The unconscious is the key to personality. Personality forms during the first few years of life, rooted in unresolved conflicts of early childhood.

5.1 The Three Structures — Iceberg Model Both

Conscious level — visible
EGO
Reality Principle. Conscious, rational, regulating. Negotiates between id demands and superego standards. What others see in our behaviour. Aim: safety, compromise, delayed gratification.
— waterline — below consciousness —
SUPEREGO — Preconscious level
Moral/Idealistic Principle. The judge. Our moral centre, conscience, and values. Develops around age 5 through identification with the same-sex parent. Learned from parents and culture. Represents right and wrong. Aim: perfection.
ID — Unconscious level
Pleasure Principle. Present from birth. Entirely unconscious. Seeks immediate gratification and avoids pain. Drives sexual and aggressive impulses. Aim: immediate gratification at all costs.
When they conflict — from slides

Excessive conflict between id and superego → dysfunction → creates anxiety. The ego manages this anxiety through defence mechanisms. A Freudian slip is when the id's impulse breaks through before the ego can screen it — the true unconscious feeling "slips out."

Humanistic Theory Study Unit

Core idea (from slides): Personality is driven by needs to adapt, learn, grow and excel — NOT by unconscious conflicts. We have enormous potential, possibility, and choice (free will). Each person has their own unique subjective experience of reality.

6.1 Carl Rogers — Self Theory

Self-concept
An individual's awareness of a continuous identity. Shapes behaviour by acting as a self-monitor against an inner standard. If self-concept is good, we try to live up to our ideal standard. If bad, we behave and feel badly.
Self-actualisation
The master motive — the drive to fulfil one's potential and become one's best self. Personality is driven by the need to actualise, not by unconscious conflict.
Unconditional positive regard
Acceptance and love with no strings attached. Essential for healthy personality. Allows the child to keep their own inner valuing system and develop an authentic self-concept grounded in who they actually are.
Conditional positive regard
Love/acceptance contingent on meeting standards. Causes the person to base their self-concept on others' judgements rather than their own inner values. Over time they stop listening to their own organismic valuing process.
Incongruence
The gap between the actual self and the ideal self-concept. When large, causes psychological discomfort and emptiness despite apparent external success — because the person is not living as who they truly are.

6.2 Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs

Lower-order needs must be substantially satisfied before higher-level needs become motivating. Maslow estimated only 1 in 10 people operates from self-actualisation needs.

  • Physiological — hunger, thirst (bottom of hierarchy)
  • Safety — security, freedom from harm
  • Belongingness & Love — affiliation, acceptance
  • Esteem — achievement, recognition, competence
  • Cognitive — to know, understand, explore
  • Aesthetic — symmetry, order, beauty
  • Self-actualisation — fulfil potential, realise self (top)
Limitation

The hierarchy does not explain why some people forgo lower needs for higher ones (e.g., Nelson Mandela choosing imprisonment over silence). Culture also heavily influences which needs are prioritised.

Social Cognitive Theory Study Unit

Core idea: Personality is NOT determined by unconscious motives. It is shaped by social learning through observation AND by patterns of thinking about the environment. Problems arise from inappropriate thoughts or faulty reasoning.

7.1 Bandura — Reciprocal Determinism & Self-Efficacy

Personality is the product of three mutually interacting forces:

Environment
⇌
Behaviour
⇌
Cognition
(thoughts, beliefs, goals)

Each force influences the other two reciprocally. We choose environments that shape our thinking; thinking guides behaviour; behaviour changes the environment. Personality forms through this ongoing loop.

Self-Efficacy

One's expectation of success in a given situation. High self-efficacy → approach tasks confidently → better outcomes. Low self-efficacy → likely to give up or not try → poor outcomes → reinforces belief that one cannot succeed. Self-efficacy can differ across different domains of life.

Personality Assessment Study Unit

8.1 Reliability & Validity

Reliability
The ability of a test to produce consistent results over repeated testing. Since personality is stable, results should not change dramatically. Tested via test-retest method (same test given twice) or split-half method (test divided into two halves; scores compared).
Validity
The ability of a test to measure what it claims to measure. Types: content validity (measures intended knowledge), predictive validity (predicts future achievements), face validity (appropriate by inspection), construct validity (measures the specific psychological quality designed).
Cultural Bias Concern

Dana (1993) questioned the validity of Western assessment tests translated into other languages. Tests may reflect Western values and fail to accurately measure personality in other cultural contexts — especially tools assessing psychopathology.

8.2 Key Assessment Tools

ToolTypeKey Details
MMPI-2 Self-report inventory Minnesota Multiphasic Personality Inventory. Clinical tool identifying problem areas of personality. Contains validity scales to assess truthfulness of responses. Good validity — identifies depressed or psychotic persons.
Myers-Briggs (MBTI) Type indicator 4 dimensions: Introversion-Extraversion, Thinking-Feeling, Sensation-Intuition, Judgement-Perception. Criticism: may not identify truly distinct types — people may have different degrees of a trait rather than a fixed type (Furnham et al., 2003).
NEO PI-R Self-report inventory Measures the Big Five (OCEAN) personality traits. Places a person on the continuum of each of the five dimensions.
Cattell's 16PF Self-report inventory Measures personality along 16 trait dimensions. More detailed but harder to use in research than the Big Five.
Rorschach Inkblot Projective test Patient describes what they see in 10 ambiguous inkblots. Assumes patients project unconscious conflicts onto images — revealing repressed thoughts. Rooted in Freud's defence mechanism of projection. Bypasses conscious self-filtering.
TAT Projective test Thematic Apperception Test. Patient shown ambiguous pictures and asked to tell a story. Responses coded for consistent themes, emotions, or issues. Also rooted in psychodynamic theory — reveals unconscious preoccupations. Both TAT and Rorschach access information a direct interview would not reveal.
Why use multiple assessment methods?

Each method accesses a different layer: self-report captures what people are aware of/willing to disclose; projective tests bypass conscious filtering; behavioural observation captures real-world patterns. Convergence across methods increases diagnostic confidence and reduces the risk of any single method's blind spots.
