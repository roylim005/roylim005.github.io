13.1

What Is a Mental Health Disorder?

A dysfunction in cognition, emotions, and/or social behaviour that impairs functioning and is not culturally expected.

Core Definition A mental health disorder = dysfunction in thinking / emotions / social behaviour that (1) impairs functioning AND (2) is not culturally expected.

Three Diagnostic Criteria

  1. Not typical or culturally expected — behaviour occurs far less/more often than most people, or violates social/cultural norms. Norms vary by culture, region, and era.
  2. Distress — causes great personal distress to the individual or those around them.
  3. Dysfunction — interferes with ability to hold a job, attend school, or form close relationships.

13.1.2 · Explanatory Models

Biological

  • Genetics
  • Neurotransmitter imbalance
  • Hormone dysfunction
  • Brain structure abnormalities
  • Also called the medical model

Psychological

  • Psychoanalytic: unresolved unconscious conflicts
  • Social learning: conditioning & modelling
  • Cognitive: thoughts, expectations, assumptions
  • Humanistic: distorted self-perception

Sociocultural

  • Age, race, gender, social roles
  • Socioeconomic status
  • Poverty & discrimination
  • Environmental stressors
Biopsychosocial Model: No single theory is sufficient. Most disorders result from a combination of biological, psychological, and social factors working together like puzzle pieces.
13.2

The DSM Model

The Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (DSM-5, 2013) lists specific criteria for diagnosing nearly 300 disorders across 20 major categories.

Strengths

  • Common language for clinicians & researchers
  • Atheoretical — does not speculate on causes
  • Reliability & validity improved with each revision
  • Enables systematic study of disorders

Criticisms

  • Clinician subjectivity & personal bias
  • Comorbidity: symptoms overlap multiple disorders
  • Gender, race, culture biases can skew diagnosis
  • Labelling risk: self-fulfilling prophecy & stigma
  • Personality disorder reliability remains low
Comorbidity — when a person meets the criteria for more than one disorder simultaneously. Common across most categories.
13.3

Anxiety, OCD & Trauma-Related Disorders

Excessive anxiety is characterised by intensity, duration, frequency, and/or irrationality out of proportion to the actual threat.

Four Components of Excessive Anxiety

ComponentExamples
PhysicalElevated heart rate, muscle tension, sweating, dizziness, dry mouth (sympathetic NS activation / fight-or-flight)
CognitiveWorrying, exaggerating danger, paranoia, loss-of-control fears
EmotionalDread, terror, panic, irritability, restlessness
BehaviouralEscape/flight, aggression, freezing, future avoidance

Specific Disorders

GAD

Generalized Anxiety Disorder

Chronic, excessive worry across nearly all situations. Symptoms: fatigue, restlessness, difficulty concentrating/sleeping. Prevalence: ~2–4% per year.

Panic Disorder

Panic Disorder

Recurrent panic attacks + fear of having more attacks that impairs functioning. Lifetime: 1–5%. 2–4× more common in women. Often develops in late adolescence.

Agoraphobia

Agoraphobia

Fear of places where escape is difficult or help unavailable. Often develops secondary to panic disorder. ≥75% diagnosed are women.

Specific Phobia

Specific Phobia

Persistent, irrational fear of a specific object/situation causing severe anxiety or panic. Worldwide prevalence ~7.4%. Begins in childhood; more common in females.

Social Anxiety

Social Anxiety Disorder

Irrational, persistent fear of negative evaluation in social situations. Lifetime: 1–12% US adults. Develops in early childhood or adolescence.

OCD

Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder

Obsessions: recurrent intrusive thoughts. Compulsions: repetitive behaviours performed to reduce anxiety. Lifetime: 1–3%. Compulsions provide only temporary relief.

Hoarding

Hoarding Disorder

Persistent difficulty discarding possessions despite little value; causes significant distress. Prevalence: 2–5%; roughly equal in males and females.

PTSD

Posttraumatic Stress Disorder

Develops after a traumatic event. Key features: re-experiencing (flashbacks, nightmares), persistent avoidance, emotional numbing, hypervigilance. ~5% US adults per year.

13.3.3 · Biopsychosocial Explanations

Biological

  • Neurotransmitters: serotonin, norepinephrine, GABA
  • Genetic predisposition (especially panic & OCD)
  • Overactive amygdala (GAD, PTSD)
  • Cingulate cortex abnormalities (panic, OCD, SAD)
  • Prefrontal–striatum pathways (OCD)

Psychological

  • Classical conditioning of phobias (Little Albert)
  • Conditioning + generalisation → panic
  • Observational learning of fears
  • Negative reinforcement maintains compulsions
  • Cognitive: perceiving situations as uncontrollable/dangerous
  • Catastrophic misinterpretation of bodily cues (panic)

Sociocultural

  • Rapid social change, war, pandemics ↑ anxiety
  • Adverse childhood experiences
  • Social support buffers PTSD risk
  • Women diagnosed at higher rates
  • White Americans at higher lifetime risk than other ethnic groups
13.4

Dissociative & Somatic Symptom Disorders

Relatively rare, but frequently of great interest. Both categories involve a disconnect — from the self (dissociative) or in physical experience (somatic).

  • Two or more separate identities ("alters") within one individual; each has distinct perceptions, mannerisms, speech.
  • Frequent blackouts and amnesia; self-mutilating behaviour common.
  • Estimated ~1% of general population.
  • Almost all report chronic, severe childhood physical/sexual abuse — dissociation as a coping mechanism.
  • Controversy: validity debated; cases may represent an extreme form of PTSD. A dissociative-PTSD subtype is included in DSM-5.
  • Mild dissociation is common (e.g., highway hypnosis, missing parts of conversations).
  • Extreme dissociation linked to severe stress or trauma history.
  • Highly susceptible to hypnosis; disrupted sleep–wake cycle increases vulnerability.
  • Person is convinced they have a serious illness despite no medical evidence.
  • May seek multiple physician opinions or, alternatively, avoid medical care entirely.
  • Often has family history of depression/anxiety; possibly related to panic disorder and OCD.
  • Prevalence: 1–5% of general population.
  • Somatic symptom disorders involve real physical symptoms with no apparent physical cause — psychological distress is assumed to underlie them.
13.5

Depressive & Bipolar Disorders

A significant, persistent change in emotional state — either towards depression or extreme elation (mania) — that impairs functioning.

Depressive

Major Depressive Disorder

Depressed mood or loss of interest plus ≥4 symptoms for ≥2 weeks. Symptoms: sleep/appetite/motor changes, fatigue, poor concentration, worthlessness, suicidal thoughts. ~7% per year.

Depressive

Persistent Depressive Disorder

Less severe but more chronic: depressed mood + ≥2 symptoms for ≥2 years. Often begins in childhood/adolescence. ~1.7% per year.

Bipolar

Bipolar Disorder

Shifts between depressive episodes and mania — elevated mood, increased energy, high self-esteem, decreased sleep, impulsivity. Lifetime: ~2.4%. Onset: late adolescence.

Bipolar

Cyclothymic Disorder

Chronic, milder form of bipolar: alternating mild mania and moderate depression for ≥2 years. Functions reasonably well during mild mania.

Individual Variations: Women are twice as likely as men to be diagnosed with depression. Adolescent females 13–15 are particularly vulnerable. African, Asian, and Hispanic Americans have lower lifetime rates than White and Native Americans.

Biopsychosocial Explanations

Biological

  • Genetics: especially strong for bipolar disorder (twin studies)
  • Neurotransmitters: serotonin, norepinephrine (depression); dopamine, glutamate (mania)
  • Stress hormones (HPA axis activation) inhibit mood-related neurotransmitters
  • Prefrontal cortex & limbic system (amygdala, hippocampus) abnormalities
  • Overactive subgenual cingulate (Brodmann's Area 25)

Psychological

  • Early adverse life events → self-blame, self-hatred (psychoanalytic)
  • Learned helplessness: belief that outcomes are uncontrollable
  • Ruminative coping style: focusing repetitively on depressed feelings
  • Cognitive distortions (Aaron Beck): negative automatic thinking
  • Depressive attributions: failures attributed to internal, global, stable causes

Sociocultural

  • Lower social status & adverse neighbourhoods
  • Major stressful life events especially in genetically predisposed
  • Gender: women at disadvantage — less power, more discrimination, sexual abuse, poverty
  • Traditional gender roles ↑ helplessness in women
  • Men less likely to seek help; express depression as irritability/substance use
13.6

Schizophrenia

A chronic, disabling disorder (~1% worldwide) involving disintegration of one's personality — the disconnection among cognitive, emotional, perceptual, and motor elements, resulting in loss of contact with reality.

≠ DID: Dissociative Identity Disorder = multiple intact identities. Schizophrenia = one identity that is no longer intact or coherent.

Symptoms

Positive Symptoms

  • Delusions — false beliefs (persecutory, grandiose, reference, thought control)
  • Hallucinations — most commonly auditory ("hearing voices")
  • Disorganised speech — loose associations, "word salad"
  • Disorganised/catatonic behaviour — repetitive acts, catatonic stupor or excitement

Negative Symptoms (~25%)

  • Blunted affect — lack of emotional expression; flat tone
  • Alogia — poverty of speech; brief, empty replies
  • Avolition — inability to follow through on plans; apathy

Positive symptoms → less severe course, better medication response. Negative symptoms → poorer prognosis.

Biopsychosocial Explanations

Biological

  • Strong genetic heritability: identical twins ~48%, fraternal ~17%
  • Dopamine hypothesis (phenothiazines reduce dopamine → reduce positive symptoms)
  • Glutamate dysfunction (PCP/ketamine → schizophrenia-like symptoms)
  • Enlarged ventricles (reduces overall brain size)
  • Frontal & temporal lobe dysfunction
  • Advanced paternal age linked to mutations

Prenatal & Developmental

  • Birth complications, maternal diabetes, low birth weight
  • Prenatal maternal stress / viral exposure
  • Hypothesis: genetic predisposition + prenatal agents alter brain development; excess synaptic pruning in adolescence triggers onset

Family & Environment

  • Critical, hostile, poor-communicating families ↑ risk in genetically vulnerable individuals
  • Chronic stress from low-income lifestyle ↑ relapse
  • More prevalent in lower socioeconomic groups
  • Recovery related to economic/social support and medication adherence
13.7

Personality Disorders

Long-standing patterns of malfunctioning — thoughts, emotions, and behaviour causing persistent distress and functional impairment. Estimated ~10% of US adults meet criteria for at least one. DSM-5 lists 10 disorders.

Cluster B

Antisocial Personality Disorder

Impulsive; disregards others' rights without remorse or guilt. Irresponsible, blames others, lies and manipulates. 3–5× more common in men. Linked to genetics, low serotonin, impulsivity-control brain deficits, low nervous system arousal, and conflict-filled childhoods.

Cluster B

Borderline Personality Disorder (BPD)

Instability in moods, relationships, self-image, and behaviour. Intense bouts of anger/depression/anxiety; extreme insecurity; interpersonal cycles of idealisation → anger. Self-injury and suicide attempts common. Prevalence: 2–6%; slightly more common in young women.

  • Biological: Low serotonin → impulsivity; abnormal brain functioning → emotion dysregulation.
  • Psychological/Social: High rates of reported childhood sexual abuse and adverse life events; impedes attachment, identity development, and emotional regulation.
  • Biological: Genetic influence (twin, adoption, family studies); low serotonin; brain deficits in impulsivity and decision-making areas; elevated testosterone; low nervous system arousal.
  • Psychological/Social: Neglectful, inconsistent, hostile parenting → mistrustful, aggressive stance toward others.
