1What Is Stress?

Stress is the psychological and bodily response to a stimulus (stressor) that you perceive as challenging or threatening — it alters your body's equilibrium (homeostasis). Crucially, stress is subjective: the same event can be stressful for one person and not for another. As the slides put it, "it's not the problem that's the problem, but how we see the problem."

The stress response has three dimensions: (1) stressors we encounter, (2) physiological & behavioural reactions, and (3) coping — successfully or not.

Key Insight from the Slides

Our response to a stressor depends on our perception of it, not just the stressor itself — stressors are "in the eyes of the beholder." This is why cognitive appraisal matters so much.

Internal Conflicts as Stressors

Conflicts — having to choose between competing needs — are also a source of stress. The study unit identifies four types:

Approach–Approach

Two appealing options

Least stressful. E.g., Paris or Rome for holiday?

Avoidance–Avoidance

Two unpleasant options

More stressful — people often freeze. E.g., mop floor or do dishes?

Approach–Avoidance

One option, mixed feelings

Drawn to it but also repelled. E.g., save your pet from a burning building?

Multiple Conflicts

Several options, each with pros & cons

Highest complexity. E.g., choosing which university to attend

2The Stress Response — General Adaptation Syndrome (GAS)

Proposed by Selye, the GAS describes the body's physical response pattern to any serious, chronic stressor — mentally, physically, and emotionally. It has three stages:

① Alarm Reaction

The body mobilises resources — "fight or flight."

Hypothalamus activates the sympathetic nervous system → adrenal glands release adrenaline & cortisol → heart rate ↑, breathing ↑, blood pressure ↑, pupils dilate, muscles on alert, liver releases stored glucose, sweating to cool the body.

② Resistance Stage

Body seems to adapt to the stressor. Reactions are less intense than the alarm stage but the body remains on high alert, continuing to expend energy reserves.

③ Exhaustion Stage

Body depletes its resources. Prolonged high stress hormones damage the heart, weaken the immune system → illness/death if unresolved. Leads to heart disease, stroke, high blood pressure, mental disorders.

Stress & Illness

Psychological states cause physical illness: Stressor → Stress → Illness. The fight-or-flight response is hard-wired and designed to protect from bodily harm. But when stress hormones flood the body for non-dangerous events, they wear out the body's reserves — weakening the immune system and causing long-term health problems (heart disease, ulcers, stroke, high blood pressure, mental disorders).

Long-Term Effects of Stress

Chronic stress negatively affects: finances, relationships, children, career, and study. The stress effects span five domains:

Cognitive Appraisal — The Key Filter
How Cognitive Appraisal Works

Before a stressor produces stress, it passes through cognitive appraisal — your interpretation of the event. A negative appraisal triggers emotional, physiological, and behavioural stress responses. A positive appraisal results in no stress.

Example: Surprise quiz → "I can't do this" (stress) vs. "A chance to show what I know" (no stress).

The ABC model from CBT captures this: Activating Event → Belief (interpretation) → Consequences (behaviour/emotion). The goal is to recognise and challenge the unhelpful belief.

4Moderators of Stress

These personality characteristics reduce the impact of stressors on the individual's stress level:

1. Personality Type (A / B / C / D)

Your characteristic stress-response style shapes how strongly you experience stressors. See the Type A/B/C/D breakdown below.

2. Locus of Control

Internal: Believe your actions shape outcomes → feel in control → perceive stressors as manageable → happier, longer lifespan, better immune system.

External: Believe outside forces decide outcomes → more susceptible to stress, depression, health problems. Perceived lack of control can lead to learned helplessness.

3. Hardiness

Considered the most effective moderator — and it can be learned.

A mental quality of resistance to stress built on three Cs:
• Challenge — see stressors as opportunities
• Commitment — stay committed to self, work, values
• Control — believe your actions influence outcomes

Learned Helplessness

When people have no perceived control over aversive situations, they develop a passive response — they stop trying, even when escape is possible. Originally demonstrated with dogs by Seligman & Maier. In humans: leads to failure to persist, poor grades, depression. Can occur in elderly people not given choices over daily activities. Contrast with the hardy personality — opposite ends of the spectrum.

5Coping Strategies

Coping refers to the techniques people use to handle stress or their reactions to it. The two main types:

Problem-Focused Coping

An active, task-oriented approach aimed at eliminating or reducing the source of stress. Best when you believe your actions can affect the stressor.

  • Identifying & monitoring stressors
  • Identifying stress antecedents
  • Acquiring new skills
  • Setting new goals
  • Time management
  • Assertiveness training
  • Problem-solving
Emotion-Focused Coping

Aimed at managing your emotional reaction to stress. Used when the stressor itself cannot be changed — requires acceptance of the situation.

  • Distraction (e.g., running)
  • Disclosure — talking or writing about feelings
  • Cognitive reappraisal (see below)
  • Relaxation / meditation
  • Indulging (comfort food) — can be maladaptive
  • Suppressing negative thoughts — often maladaptive
  • Defense mechanisms (Freud) — unconscious, automatic
Cognitive Reappraisal (2.1 in slides)
Emotion-Focused — most useful for chronic stress

Reappraising the stressor — seeing the situation in a more positive light. This is the cornerstone of Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT). It is an active and conscious process of changing your interpretation of the stressful event to reduce negative feelings.

E.g., "I've lost my job" → reframe as "I have more time with family and can learn new skills."

Self-Disclosure (Study Unit 3.4)

Pennebaker (1993) found that writing about upsetting/traumatic experiences (20 mins/day for 4 days) led to fewer health clinic visits and more positive mood at year-end compared to a control group. The act of writing helps the writer work through and come to terms with the experience, reducing the negative effects of stress on the immune system.

This is a form of emotion-focused coping — "venting" by putting stress into words.

6Positive Lifestyle Choices

These act on both sides of the stress model — as moderators and as coping strategies:

1

Exercise

Reduces depression, anxiety, and negative emotions from stress; raises energy, releases serotonin/endorphins

2

Progressive Relaxation Training

Alternately tense then relax muscle groups; builds body awareness and ability to relax on command

3

Social Support

Strong relationships buffer stress effects; even giving support to others helps

4

Be Spiritual

Having purpose, self-awareness, and connectedness is associated with longer lifespan and better health

5

Be Optimistic

Expecting good outcomes promotes resilience; optimists show lower cortisol and better immune function

6

Time Management

Keeping a planner; treating all commitments (work, study, play) as serious

Key Research — Friedman & Rosenman (1974)

Originally proposed Type A doubles heart disease risk. Later research refined this: it is specifically anger, mistrust, and hostility within Type A — not all Type A traits — that elevate cardiovascular risk. Type A people without hostility are no more at risk than Type B.
