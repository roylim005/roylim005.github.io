THINKING & INTELLIGENCE

  • Creativity: Novel solutions
  • Incubation: Unconscious processing
Obstacles
  • Functional Fixedness: Can't see alternative uses
  • Mental Set: Stuck with familiar approach
Reasoning Types
  • Deductive: General → Specific (certain)
  • Inductive: Specific → General (probable)
  • Dialectical: Reconciling contradictions
Decision Making
  • Framing: How choices are presented
  • Availability: Judge by ease of recall
  • Representativeness: Judge by similarity
  • Affective: Emotion-based decisions
Language Development
  • Cooing: Early vowel sounds
  • Babbling: Consonant-vowel combos
  • Morphemes: Smallest meaningful units
  • Telegraphic: 2-word phrases (~age 2)
  • Grammar: Language rules
Intelligence Components
  • g (general): Single factor theory
  • Crystallized: Accumulated knowledge
  • Fluid: Processing speed/efficiency
  • Multiple: Separate intelligences
  • Emotional: Managing emotions

Types of Reasoning

DEDUCTIVE Reasoning
Direction: General → Specific
Certainty: If premises true, conclusion MUST be true

Example:
• All mammals have lungs (general)
• Dogs are mammals
• Therefore, dogs have lungs (specific)

✓ Logically valid = certain conclusion
INDUCTIVE Reasoning
Direction: Specific → General
Certainty: Probabilistic (can be wrong)

Example:
• This swan is white (specific)
• That swan is white (specific)
• Therefore, all swans are white (general)

⚠ Can be disproven (black swans exist!)
DIALECTICAL Reasoning
Direction: Thesis ↔ Antithesis → Synthesis
Purpose: Reconcile contradictions

Example:
• Tradition provides stability (thesis)
• Innovation drives progress (antithesis)
• Balanced approach honors both (synthesis)

🔄 Accept or integrate opposing ideas

Decision-Making Heuristics

Availability Heuristic
Judge likelihood by ease of recall

Example: After seeing news about plane crashes, you think flying is more dangerous than it actually is (car accidents are more common but less memorable)
Representativeness Heuristic
Judge by similarity to prototype

Example: Assuming someone wearing glasses and reading is a professor rather than a truck driver (ignoring base rates - there are more truck drivers)
Affective Heuristic
Decisions based on emotional response

Example: Avoiding nuclear power because it "feels" dangerous, despite statistical safety data

Major Intelligence Theories

Spearman's g (General Intelligence)
Charles Spearman
Core Idea: Single underlying mental ability

• Intelligence is ONE unified factor
• Good at one thing = tend to be good at others
• Measured by overall IQ score
Crystallized vs. Fluid Intelligence
Raymond Cattell
Two types of intelligence:

Crystallized:
• Accumulated KNOWLEDGE
• Vocabulary, facts, skills
• ↗ Increases with age

Fluid:
• Processing SPEED
• Learning new things quickly
• ↘ Peaks young, declines with age
Triarchic Theory
Robert Sternberg
Three independent intelligences:

1. Analytical: Academic problem-solving
2. Creative: Novel situations, innovation
3. Practical: Street smarts, everyday problems

You can be high in one and low in others!
Multiple Intelligences
Howard Gardner
8+ separate intelligences:

• Linguistic (words)
• Logical-mathematical (numbers)
• Spatial (visualization)
• Musical (rhythm, sound)
• Bodily-kinesthetic (movement)
• Interpersonal (understanding others)
• Intrapersonal (self-awareness)
• Naturalistic (nature patterns)
Emotional Intelligence
Daniel Goleman
Emotions matter for success!

Five components:
• Self-awareness
• Self-regulation
• Motivation
• Empathy
• Social skills

May predict success better than IQ
Nature vs. Nurture in Intelligence:
NOT either/or! Intelligence results from interaction of heredity AND environment.

Flynn Effect: Average IQ scores have increased over past century → suggests environment plays major role

Critical Distinctions

Algorithm vs Heuristic
Step-by-step procedure ⚔️ Mental shortcut
✓ Always finds solution ⚔️ ⚠ May or may not work
Slow, effortful ⚔️ Fast, efficient
For well-structured problems ⚔️ For ill-structured problems
Example: Try every locker combo ⚔️ Example: "Try numbers that mean something to me"
Prototype vs Exemplar
Average/ideal representation ⚔️ Specific remembered instance
Mental "composite" ⚔️ Actual experienced member
One per category ⚔️ Multiple stored examples
Example: Mental image of "average bird" ⚔️ Example: Your pet parakeet, robins you've seen
Crystallized Intelligence vs Fluid Intelligence
What you KNOW ⚔️ How fast you LEARN
Accumulated knowledge ⚔️ Processing speed
↗ Increases with age ⚔️ ↘ Peaks young, declines
Vocabulary, facts, skills ⚔️ Solving novel problems
Example: Knowing state capitals ⚔️ Example: Quickly learning new software
Deductive Reasoning vs Inductive Reasoning
General → Specific ⚔️ Specific → General
✓ Certain if premises true ⚔️ ⚠ Probabilistic (can be wrong)
Logically valid ⚔️ Based on observation
Example: All mammals breathe air → Whales are mammals → Whales breathe air ⚔️ Example: I've seen 100 white swans → All swans are white
Biolinguistic Theory vs Usage-Based Theory
Language is INNATE ⚔️ Language is LEARNED
Born with LAD (Language Acquisition Device) ⚔️ Develops through social interaction
Evolutionary/genetic ⚔️ Cognitive abilities + experience
"Pre-programmed" for language ⚔️ Language emerges from use
Memory Tip:
Think of these as SPECTRUMS, not absolute categories. Many concepts exist on a continuum!

Exam Strategy: Know the DIFFERENCES and be able to give EXAMPLES that distinguish them.
