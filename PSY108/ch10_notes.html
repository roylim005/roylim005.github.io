01

Attribution Theory & Biases

Attribution theory explains how we assign cause to behaviour — our own and others'. The seminar frames this as the tension between dispositional (personal) and situational (environmental) explanations. We often underestimate how powerful social context is.

The Two Types of Attribution
Trait / Dispositional Attribution
Behaviour is caused by the person's internal characteristics — their personality, ability, genes, or morality.

Example: "She yelled because she's an aggressive person."
VS
Situational Attribution
Behaviour is caused by external/environmental factors beyond the person's control.

Example: "She yelled because the server spilled coffee on her."
The Three Key Attribution Biases
Fundamental Attribution Error (FAE)
When judging others, we tend to overemphasise personal traits and underestimate situational forces — even when the situation is largely responsible. This is the most common attribution error.

Why it happens: Our attention naturally focuses on the person, not the background context. We are also less aware of the situational pressures others face.

Cultural note: More common in individualistic cultures (Western) where individual behaviour is emphasised. Less common in collectivistic cultures (e.g., Japan, India) where group context matters more.

Remedy — Attributional Charity: Consciously look for a situational explanation for unusual or unexpected behaviour before attributing it to personality. This is a good habit to practise.
Actor / Observer Bias
We make different attributions for the same type of behaviour depending on whether we are the actor or the observer.

As an actor (ourselves): We favour situational explanations for our behaviour — we're aware of the context and circumstances we're in.
As an observer (watching others): We favour trait explanations — we focus on the person, not their situation.

Example: If you shout at someone, it's because you had a terrible day (situation). If a stranger shouts, they must be an aggressive person (trait).

Why: As actors, we cannot see ourselves — our attention is outward on the environment. As observers, our attention is on the other person.
Self-Serving Bias
We attribute our successes to internal traits ("I'm talented / I worked hard") and our failures to external, situational factors ("The test was unfair / I was unlucky").

Purpose: Protects our self-esteem. Almost universal across cultures and age groups.

Example in slides: Success → "I'm talented, high IQ." Failure → "Bad leadership, poor facilities."

Downside: Taken too far, it prevents us from learning from failures and can damage relationships. Closely linked to narcissism.
⚡ Exam Watch
The formative quiz (Q14) confirms: FAE = believing personal traits rather than situations primarily determine behaviour. Q9 confirms social psychology's key insight = the social situation is the key determinant of behaviour, not personality alone.
02

Cognitive Dissonance

Cognitive Dissonance Theory — Festinger (1957)
When a person's behaviour conflicts with their attitudes or values, an uncomfortable state of psychological tension arises — dissonance. Because this tension is unpleasant, the person is motivated to resolve it.
Classic Example (from slides)
Smoking Scenario
Belief: "Smoking cigarettes is unhealthy."
Behaviour: "I smoke cigarettes."
→ Creates inner tension (dissonance)

Three ways to resolve it:
1. Change behaviour → Stop smoking
2. Change attitude/belief → "The research on smoking is not conclusive"
3. Add new beliefs → "I smoke less than most people; it's fine"
Real-Life Application (from SU2)

A woman in an abusive relationship experiences dissonance between knowing the relationship is harmful and staying in it. She can resolve it by: (1) leaving the relationship (behaviour change), or (2) convincing herself she must stay for her children (self-justification / attitude change). This illustrates how dissonance can sometimes keep people in harmful situations.

⚡ Exam Watch
Q13 in formative assessment: cognitive dissonance motivates people to make unpleasant experiences seem more rational — we rationalise to reduce the discomfort of inconsistency.
03

Prejudice & Discrimination

Stereotype

A generalised knowledge schema about a group of people stored in memory. The cognitive building block — what we believe about a group. Not inherently negative, but becomes problematic when rigidly applied.

Prejudice

A negative attitude toward an individual based solely on their group membership — dislike or fear. This is an attitude. Involves both the stereotype (cognitive) and a negative emotional reaction.

Discrimination

A negative action taken against an individual because of their group membership — avoiding, controlling, bullying, dominating, or eliminating. Discrimination is the behavioural expression of prejudice.

What Breeds Prejudice? (Seminar slides)
In-Group Bias

We favour members of our own group. We derive self-esteem from group membership and therefore perceive our in-group as superior. Out-group members are often seen as homogeneous ("they're all the same").

Stereotyping

Applying a rigid, generalised schema to all members of an out-group — ignoring individual differences. When stereotypes contain biased/negative information, they become the cognitive foundation of prejudice.

Scapegoating

Blaming out-group members for one's own group's problems — especially when resources are scarce or under threat. Rooted in realistic-conflict theory: competition for resources fuels inter-group hostility.

Jane Elliott's Classroom Experiment (1968)
Classic Study — How Prejudice Is Learned
A third-grade teacher divided her class into "superior" blue-eyed and "inferior" brown-eyed students. Within hours, blue-eyed students became bossy and cruel; brown-eyed students began to perform worse and feel inferior.

Key lesson: Prejudice can be rapidly created from the most trivial differences. Situational labelling is enough to produce discrimination. Social feedback shapes how we see ourselves and behave.
Pygmalion Effect / Self-Fulfilling Prophecy (Rosenthal & Jacobson, 1968)
Pygmalion Effect
Rosenthal and Jacobson randomly labelled some students as academically superior. Teachers — believing this — gave those students more attention, support, and praise. The students then actually performed better. The teachers' expectations became a self-fulfilling prophecy.

Link to social psychology: Both the Elliott and Rosenthal studies show that social feedback — the expectations and labels others place on us — powerfully shapes our own self-perception and behaviour. Situational forces include not just objective facts, but also subjective realities others create for us.
Reducing Prejudice — Jigsaw Classroom (Aronson & Gonzales, 1988)

Students from diverse groups were each made responsible for a different piece of a shared project — making them interdependent. As they cooperated, focus shifted from differences to tasks. Result: reduced prejudice, improved self-esteem and academic performance. Core principle: cooperation, not competition, reduces prejudice.

⚡ Exam Watch
Q15: Prejudice increases social distance. As social distance increases, probability of prejudice increases. The in-group does NOT decrease social distance toward newcomers.

Conformity — Asch & Sherif

Conformity
The tendency for people to adopt the behaviour and opinions of group members, even on objective facts. Social pressure can override one's own perceptions and judgement.
Asch's Line Study (1956) — Normative Influence
Classic Study
Participants matched line lengths alongside confederates who unanimously gave the wrong answer. Nearly one-third of participants conformed to the obviously incorrect majority judgment to avoid appearing "different."

What this demonstrates: The desire to be accepted, approved, and liked can overcome the desire to trust one's own eyes and brain.
Sherif's Norm Formation Study — Informational Influence
Classic Study
Used an ambiguous perceptual task (autokinetic effect). In groups, individual estimates converged into a shared group norm — and this norm was transmitted to the next generation of participants who never met the original group.

What this demonstrates: When the situation is ambiguous, we look to others for the correct interpretation of reality. The group becomes our source of information.
Two Types of Conformity
Normative Influence (Asch)
Conform to be accepted / liked, or to avoid rejection and punishment.
Behaviour changes but no real attitude change — you know you're going along.

Driven by: Desire to fit in. Fear of being ostracised.
VS
Informational Influence (Sherif)
Conform because you're genuinely persuaded the group is correct. Both behaviour AND attitude change.
Transmitted to next generation — becomes a cultural norm.

Driven by: Desire to be correct.
Conditions That Promote Conformity
  • Task is difficult or ambiguous — uncertainty makes us look to others for the right answer.
  • Group members are seen as competent — we trust their judgement more.
  • Responses are public — we don't want to be visibly different.
  • Group majority is unanimous — no ally to validate our own view.
  • High need for structure/certainty or low self-confidence in the individual.
  • Culture emphasises group cooperation — collectivistic cultures show higher in-person conformity.
⚡ Exam Watch
Q10: People are most likely to conform when a judgment task is difficult or ambiguous. Having even one ally (partner) against the group dramatically reduces conformity.
05

Groupthink

Groupthink (Janis, 1982)
Members of a group attempt to conform their opinions to what they believe is the group consensus. The drive for unanimity overrides realistic appraisal of alternatives. This can lead a group to take actions that individual members would normally consider unwise.
Conditions That Promote Groupthink
  • Directive (dictatorial) leadership — members fear disagreeing with the leader.
  • High group cohesiveness — members value belonging and avoid rocking the boat.
  • Isolation of the group — cannot access outside information or dissenting views.
  • Lack of systematic decision procedures — no structured method to examine alternatives.
  • Homogeneity of members' backgrounds and ideology — no diversity of perspective.
  • High external stress with low hope of a better solution than the leader's.
Effects of Groupthink
  • Group examines fewer alternatives — options are narrowed prematurely.
  • Selective gathering of information — only evidence supporting the consensus is considered.
  • Pressure to conform within the group — dissent is suppressed.
  • Members withhold criticism — ideas go unchallenged.
  • Collective rationalisation — the group convinces itself its decision is correct.
⚡ Exam Watch
Groupthink is driven by situational conditions (isolation, leadership style, cohesion), not individual stupidity. The antidote is actively encouraging thoughtful critique and including outside perspectives.

Obedience — Milgram & Zimbardo

Milgram's Obedience Experiments (1963–1974)
Classic Study — Setup
Participants ("teachers") were instructed to deliver electric shocks (15V–450V) to a "learner" (confederate, not actually shocked) whenever the learner made an error, under the calm insistence of an experimenter in a lab coat.

Key finding: 65% of participants delivered the maximum 450V shock — despite believing the learner was in pain or unconscious. No participant refused before the 300V mark.
What the Milgram Variations Showed

Milgram ran 16 variations. Key findings from the variation chart (in seminar slides):

ConditionEffect on Obedience
Two authorities give contradictory commands (#3)Obedience drops sharply — authority conflict removes clear directive
Remote authority (phone) (#7)Lower obedience — physical distance from authority reduces pressure
Women as subjects (#13)Similar obedience rates — not a gender difference
A peer administers the shock (#16)Highest obedience (~92%) — responsibility shifts even more to another person
Touch proximity — hand on shock plate (#8)Lower obedience — psychological distance reduced; reality of harm more vivid
Factors That Increase Obedience (Textbook)
  • Authority figure present — relieves personal responsibility; "I was just following orders."
  • Incremental escalation — foot-in-the-door logic; each small step makes the next harder to refuse (slippery slope).
  • Psychological distance from victim — not seeing/hearing the victim makes it easier to dissociate from consequences.
  • Time pressure — orders began immediately; no time to reflect on what was happening.
  • Institutional context — Yale University setting lent authority legitimacy.
Milgram's Conclusion
Obedience was driven by situational factors (norms, rules, the power of authority), NOT dispositional factors. There were no personality trait differences or psychological abnormalities in those who obeyed. Anyone has the capacity to obey destructive commands if the situation is powerful enough. The key takeaway: the power of the situation.
Zimbardo's Stanford Prison Experiment (1971)
Classic Study — Setup
Healthy college students were randomly assigned to play prison guards or prisoners in a mock prison. Within days, guards became abusive and sadistic; prisoners became docile and depressed. The experiment was meant to last 2 weeks but was terminated after 6 days.

Key conclusion: Situational forces — not character — drove behaviour. When people adopt social roles and lose their individual identity (deindividuation), external norms control behaviour more than internal values.

Broader lesson: Even bright, healthy people can rapidly take on the character of their assigned social role. Social situations can bring out the best or worst in human nature.
⚡ Exam Watch
Q11: Obedience is least likely when authority gives orders by telephone — physical distance from authority greatly reduces compliance. Q16: The Stanford Prison Experiment shows how quickly situational forces affect personality and behaviour.

Bystander Effect

Bystander Effect (Darley & Latané, 1968)
As the number of bystanders increases in an emergency, the probability that any one individual will help decreases. Counter-intuitively, having more people around makes help less likely — not more.
Classic Study — Darley & Latané (1968)
Participants heard what sounded like another student having a seizure via intercom. Whether they sought help depended on how many other bystanders they believed were listening. The graph (in seminar slides) shows: 2-person groups → nearly 100% helped; 6-person groups → only ~65% helped, and more slowly.

Key finding: More bystanders = fewer and slower responses. Participants assumed others would handle it.
Two Explanations
Diffusion of Responsibility

When multiple people are present, the sense of personal responsibility is shared and diluted. Each person feels only a fraction of the obligation to act. If you're alone, you bear 100% of the responsibility.


Practical fix: If you need help, single out one person by name or description ("You in the red shirt — call an ambulance!"). This eliminates diffusion and places full responsibility on one individual.

Pluralistic Ignorance

People look to others' reactions to decide whether an emergency exists. When everyone sees others doing nothing, each person concludes there is no real emergency — so nobody acts, even when everyone privately suspects something is wrong.

Interpersonal Attraction

The SU2 and seminar slides identify four main variables that affect attraction. These are tested at the awareness level — know what each factor is and why it matters.

1. Proximity

Frequent sightings best predict close relationships. The more physically close we are to someone in daily life, the more exposure we have to them, and the more we tend to like them (mere exposure effect). True even in randomly assigned housing.

2. Similarity

"Birds of a feather flock together." We gravitate toward people who are similar in age, values, attitudes, intelligence, and background. Similarity is more reliably predictive than "opposites attract." It also reduces cognitive dissonance.

3. Physical Attractiveness

Strong predictor of initial attraction. Attractive people benefit from the halo effect — assumed to be kinder, smarter, more sociable. Men weight physical attractiveness more heavily than women, who tend to prioritise psychological traits and social status.

4. "Chemistry"

The neurochemical basis of attraction. Three systems: sex drive (oestrogens/androgens), romantic attraction (↑ dopamine & norepinephrine, ↓ serotonin), and attachment (oxytocin, vasopressin). Each governed by different chemicals.
