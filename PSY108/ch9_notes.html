OVERVIEW

Developmental Psychology

Developmental psychology studies growth, change, and consistency across the lifespan from four perspectives: physical, emotional, cognitive, and sociocultural.

Empiricists (e.g. Locke)
  • We arrive as blank slates (tabula rasa)
  • All development comes from experience and environment
  • Nurture is primary
Nativists (e.g. Rousseau)
  • What we bring at birth matters most
  • Development driven by inborn traits
  • Nature is primary

Today's view — interaction, not either/or: Heredity and experience interact throughout the lifespan. Even inherited tendencies can be modified by environment. Shy baby monkeys placed with nurturant foster mothers showed reduced shyness — demonstrating that both nature and nurture shape even inborn traits (Suomi et al., 1985).

The lifespan is studied in overlapping stages: Prenatal → Neonatal (birth–1 month) → Infancy (1–18 months) → Childhood → Adolescence → Adulthood.

INFANCY

Prenatal, Neonatal & Infancy

Prenatal Factors & Teratogens

Physical development in the womb can be disrupted by teratogens — any environmental agent that harms the developing organism. These include infectious agents (rubella, malaria), prescription and OTC drugs, caffeine, alcohol, and smoking.

Most vulnerable period: The embryonic stage (weeks 3–8) when major organs form. Most miscarriages and genetic defects surface here. A woman may not yet know she is pregnant, making unknowing exposure a real risk.

Down syndrome — extra 21st chromosome; detectable prenatally. Associated with distinct facial features, heart defects, and varying degrees of intellectual disability.

Neural Development in Infancy

Infancy is the most fertile time for language and emotional (EQ) development due to sensitive periods — windows when the brain needs sufficient stimulation for normal development. Missing these windows makes skills like new language acquisition much harder later (hence why adults struggle to learn new languages compared to children).

Synaptic pruning — experience and exposure determine which neural connections are strengthened and which are discarded. By around age 11, the ultimate mass of the brain is largely established. This is a nurture process: what you experience shapes your brain's architecture.

Neonatal Reflexes

At birth, newborns are tested for reflexes to confirm normal neurological development. These automatic, inborn responses also form the foundation for later voluntary skills.

Neonatal Preferences (Innate Abilities)

Newborns arrive with built-in sensory preferences that help them survive and bond with caregivers. Advances in measurement (tracking gaze duration, sucking intensity, brain electrical responses) allow researchers to detect these abilities even when infants cannot speak.

Sight

Faces Over Parts

Prefers whole, organised faces over jumbled face arrangements (Fantz, 1985). Innate ability to recognise facial patterns — maximises chance of bonding with caregiver. Nearsighted at birth; visual convergence develops as nervous system matures.

Hearing

Voices & Motherese

Prefers human voices and recognises mother's voice shortly after birth (heard in womb from ~week 20). Responds best to "motherese" — drawn-out vowels, exaggerated syllables, rhythmic inflection. Prefers soft, rhythmic sounds.

Smell

Mother's Scent

Breastfed infants distinguish their own mother's smell from a stranger's at just 3 days old. Preference for banana-like smells. Helps locate caregiver and nourishment.

Taste

Sweet Preference

Taste buds functional at birth; strongly prefer sweet tastes. Adaptive — breast milk is sweet, steering infant toward nourishment.

Motor Development Milestones

Physical development follows ordered stages — readiness of the body and nervous system determines timing. Each stage builds on the previous one. Notably, crawling develops peripheral vision which then enables depth perception — once infants can crawl, they hesitate at the "visual cliff" (Gibson & Walk, 1960).

Attachment

Attachment = the emotional bond between the child and the primary caregiver (1 month to ~18 months). It lays the foundation for all close relationships that follow. Early attachment quality affects: aggression, quality of friendships, job satisfaction, relationship choices, and intimacy experiences in adulthood.

Harlow's key finding: Attachment is built on contact comfort, not feeding. Baby monkeys spent time with the cloth surrogate (warm, soft) not the wire one with food. Closeness and warmth — not nourishment — drive bonding.

Two hallmark signs of attachment in the first year:

Separation anxiety — distress when separated from the primary caretaker. Appears ~6–7 months; peaks 14–18 months; gradually fades through preschool years.
Stranger anxiety — distress when approached by unfamiliar people. Appears ~8–10 months; subsides over the second year.

Ainsworth's Strange Situation — observes how infants behave when caregiver leaves and returns, revealing four distinct attachment patterns:

StyleBehaviourLong-Term Implications
SecureExplores confidently with parent present; mild distress at separation; quickly soothed on returnCurious, resilient, better social skills, stronger academic performance, healthier adult relationships
AvoidantIgnores parent; no distress at separation; no emotional reaction at returnDifficulty with closeness; emotional distance in adult relationships ("I don't like it when someone wants to know all about me")
Resistant / Anxious-AmbivalentClingy; intense distress at separation; angry and hard to soothe at returnHypervigilant about abandonment; intense neediness can push partners away ("my desire to be close sometimes scares people away")
Disorganised / DisorientedConfused; looks away while being comforted; blank expression after being calmedMost at-risk for emotional and behavioural difficulties in later life
CHILDHOOD

Childhood Development

The three developmental tasks of childhood build sequentially on each other: Language Acquisition → Cognitive Development (thinking, perceiving, remembering) → Social Relationships.

How Children Acquire Language
Nature Perspective

Built-In Mental Structures

Language structures are wired into the brain. These enable comprehension and sound production. Children worldwide follow the same sequence: cooing → babbling → single words → two-word sentences. The universal sequence suggests a biological developmental timetable — both brain maturation and muscle development in the mouth and tongue are involved.

Nurture Perspective

Imitation & Environment

Language is learned through imitation. The dominant language in the child's environment by the 6th month shapes which sounds develop. Note: children say "drawed" not "drew" — they've internalised grammatical rules and overapply them. This shows active learning, not mere copying.

Interaction Perspective

Critical Period + Environment

Both biology and environment work together. There is a critical period for language development. When biology is ready and the environment is conducive (rich exposure), the child excels. Neither alone is sufficient.

Piaget's Theory of Cognitive Development

Three Core Concepts:

Schema — any mental concept or framework (e.g., a mental folder called "dogs")
Assimilation — fitting new info into an existing schema. Seeing a cow and calling it a "doggy" — the new animal gets stuffed into the existing folder.
Accommodation — modifying or creating a schema to fit new experience. The cow moos — not a dog sound. Brain creates a new "cow" folder.

Disequilibrium = mental discomfort when experience doesn't match schema. It's the engine that forces accommodation — the brain is wired to resolve this discomfort.

The Island Test — what Piaget gets right: A child raised completely alone would still develop object permanence and basic symbolic thinking through their own biological engine. Piaget is describing real, inborn cognitive hardware.

But the ceiling is low without others: Without cultural tools — language, numbers, writing — the island child can never reach formal operational thinking. They'd reinvent everything from scratch every generation. This is Vygotsky's challenge to Piaget.

StageAgeKey AbilityKey Limitation
Sensorimotor Birth–2 yrs "Very little thinking." Relies on innate motor responses to stimuli. Major achievement at ~8 months: object permanence — objects exist even when hidden. Leads to goal-directed behaviour and then symbolic thinking. "Out of sight, out of mind" before 8 months. Cannot represent absent objects mentally.
Preoperational 2–7 yrs Rapid symbol use and language (~10,000 words by age 6); pretend play. Egocentrism: cannot take another's perspective (three mountains task — child thinks everyone sees what they see). Trial-and-error thinking. Centration: focuses on one feature only. No conservation: tall glass looks like "more." Cannot distinguish appearance from reality.
Concrete Operational 7–11 yrs Logical thinking; masters conservation; mental operations; thinks before acting; less impulsive. Can consider others' perspectives — empathy and humour grow. Still limited to concrete, observable situations. Cannot reason abstractly — hence hands-on activities are essential in primary school.
Formal Operational 11+ yrs Abstract thought appears. Can reason about hypotheticals, handle science and maths. Can build and debate arguments effectively. Explores identity. Return of egocentrism (imaginary audience, personal fable). Not all adolescents reach this — depends on education and culture.
Piaget's stages: Sensorimotor → Preoperational → Concrete → Formal — "Small People Can Fly"
Vygotsky's Sociocultural Theory
Piaget — The Lone Scientist 🔬
  • Cognition develops through internal schemas
  • Universal stages; fixed biological sequence
  • Child discovers the world independently
  • Think: self-updating app — the brain rewires through solo experience
  • Other people helpful but not essential
Vygotsky — The Apprentice 🛠
  • Mental processes begin outside in social interaction, then move inward
  • Culture shapes direction and ceiling of development
  • Child guided by more capable others
  • Think: you need WiFi — without people, the hardware barely runs
  • Other people absolutely essential

Vygotsky's deeper point: You cannot think thoughts your culture hasn't given you tools to think. A 16-year-old today can grasp calculus not because they're smarter than Newton — but because centuries of human thinking have been packaged into textbooks, teachers, and language. You're not reinventing the wheel. You're downloading it through scaffolding.

Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD) — the gap between what a child can do alone and what they can do with help. Best teaching targets this gap — not too easy (boring) and not too hard (impossible).

Scaffolding — adult holds the bike seat, runs alongside, then gradually lets go. Skill moves from shared → owned.

Private speech — "okay, first I do this, carry the one..." That inner voice during hard tasks was once someone else's voice (parent, teacher) that got internalised. Peaks 5–7 years; internalises by ~9; returns during difficult problem-solving at any age.

Where Piaget fights back
  • Why do students in the same classroom develop so differently?
  • Biological readiness matters — you can't scaffold algebra to a brain not yet ready for abstract reasoning
  • Vygotsky underestimated the hardware stage
The Synthesis
  • Piaget = hardware — biological stages of readiness
  • Vygotsky = software — cultural tools and social input that run on the hardware
  • Key question: is the bottleneck the child's readiness, or the quality of scaffolding?

Kohlberg's Moral Reasoning

The key distinguishing question across all three levels: Who are you trying to please, and why does their opinion matter to you?

LevelDriving QuestionBasis for Moral DecisionTypical For
Preconventional "What happens to ME?" Avoid punishment / gain rewards. Pure self-interest. No genuine moral values. Would steal every time if zero consequences — same logic as training a dog. Two sub-stages: (1) obedience/punishment orientation; (2) naively egocentric self-interest orientation. Most children
Conventional "What will people think of ME?" Social approval ("good boy/good girl"), avoid disapproval, uphold laws. Cares about reputation and belonging. Follows rules even when nobody watches — because shame is real. Two sub-stages: (1) good boy/girl orientation; (2) law and order orientation. Most adolescents and adults
Postconventional "What is actually RIGHT?" Social contract and universal ethical principles. Willing to defy laws or social pressure when principles demand it. Internal moral compass overrides external opinion. Two sub-stages: (1) contractual/legalistic; (2) universal principal orientation — personal ethics. Some but not all adults

Why pre-conventional and conventional are NOT the same:
Pre-conventional avoids physical consequences to self — scolding, loss of privileges. Other people's feelings don't exist in this calculation.
Conventional avoids social consequences — shame, disapproval, loss of belonging. The person has joined a moral community and their identity is tied to it.

Same outward behaviour (not stealing), completely different internal engine.

Pre-conventional example
  • Find $500 wallet, no cameras, no witnesses → Keep it. Zero consequences to me.
  • Morality = consequences to my body, freedom, stuff
Conventional example
  • Same wallet → Return it. "I'm not a thief. What if someone found out?"
  • Morality = consequences to my reputation and relationships

Post-conventional in action: Citizens in Nazi-occupied Europe who hid Jewish families risked death and defied their entire society's approval. No pre-conventional logic (gained nothing, risked everything). No conventional logic (society said don't). They acted from a pure internal principle: this is wrong and I will not participate.

Three levels: Pre = "What do I lose?" · Con = "What will they think?" · Post = "What is right?"

Gilligan's critique: Kohlberg's model is biased toward the male perspective — emphasising justice and individual rights. Women, Gilligan argued, more often reason from care and relationships. However research has not consistently supported clear gender differences — both males and females use both justice and care reasoning. Also criticised for Western cultural bias: postconventional reasoning emphasises individual rights, which fits Western cultures more than collectivist ones.

Erikson's Childhood Psychosocial Stages

Each stage is a developmental crisis. Healthy resolution strengthens capacity for the next stage. Unhealthy resolution can echo throughout life — but damage can sometimes be repaired later.

StageAgeWhat Produces Healthy ResolutionHealthy → Unhealthy
Trust vs. Mistrust 0–1½ yrs Caregiver consistently meets needs → child develops trust in the world. Also lays foundation for secure attachment. Trust → Mistrust and insecurity
Autonomy vs. Shame & Doubt 1½–3 yrs Freedom + encouragement to do things independently → autonomy. Restriction, criticism, harsh demands → shame, no perseverance when mastering new tasks. Autonomy → Shame and self-doubt
Initiative vs. Guilt 3–6 yrs Encouraged to be independent and make decisions → initiative, ambition. Overcontrolled, discouraged → feeling of guilt, inadequacy, and less confidence. Initiative → Guilt and inadequacy
Industry vs. Inferiority 6 yrs–puberty School, sports, and peer interactions provide feedback. Success in these arenas → sense of competence. Repeated failure and negative feedback → feelings of inferiority. Competence and industry → Inferiority
ADOLESCENCE

Adolescence

Adolescence brings three simultaneous challenges: Physical changes → Cognitive changes → Socioemotional pressures.

Physical Changes: Puberty & Brain Development

Puberty = process of sexual maturation. Average onset: females ~10 yrs, males ~12 yrs. Growth spurt adds ~10 inches and ~40 lbs. First menstruation = menarche.

Brain development continues into adulthood. The frontal lobe — responsible for judgment, impulse control, and planning — is still maturing in adolescence. This explains impulsive teenage behaviour. The amygdala (emotional reactions) is more active and larger in teens than adults, contributing to emotional intensity.

Cognitive Changes: Formal Operations & Adolescent Thinking

Abstract thought enables teenagers to reason about hypotheticals, tackle complex subjects, and debate effectively. But it also brings a return of egocentrism:

Feature

Idealism of Youth

Abstract reasoning creates a sense of having discovered answers to the world's problems. Heightened sensitivity to injustice and what "could be" vs. what "is."

Feature

Imaginary Audience

Belief that others are constantly watching and evaluating them. "Everyone noticed my pimple." Self-consciousness peaks. Performing for a mostly imaginary audience — hiding in class to avoid being called on.

Feature

Personal Fable

Belief in one's own uniqueness and invincibility. "It won't happen to me." Drives risk-taking — unprotected sex, drug use — because addiction and consequences "happen to other people."

Socioemotional Challenge: Identity vs. Role Confusion (Erikson)

Identity vs. Role Confusion — the defining crisis of adolescence. Teenagers must form a stable sense of who they are across: occupation, gender role, sexual behaviour, religion, and peer relationships. Those who cannot settle on a stable identity experience role confusion — constantly shifting, never grounded. Identity formed here is not permanent — it continues to be refined through adult roles and responsibilities.

ADULTHOOD

Adulthood

Adulthood is where life stabilises. Physical and cognitive abilities peak. The three major tasks are: Advanced Education → Career → Intimate Relationship.

Midlife in Detail: Generativity vs. Stagnation
Generativity ✓
  • Focus beyond self — raising children, volunteering, mentoring
  • Making meaningful, lasting contributions to family, work, society, future generations
  • Virtue of "care" developed
  • Increased life satisfaction
  • Skills cultivated: reflection, analysis, stress coping, problem-solving, healthy lifestyle, social support, sense of well-being
Stagnation ✗
  • "Empty nest syndrome" — feeling purposeless when children leave
  • Questioning and regretting past choices
  • Self-indulgent focus — absorbed in own comfort and needs only
  • Midlife crisis — dramatic attempts to reclaim youth
Late Adulthood in Detail: Ego-Integrity vs. Despair
Ego-Integrity ✓
  • Look back on life without regrets — sense of wholeness
  • Appreciate things that turned out well
  • Accept things that did not turn out well
  • Feel whole, complete, satisfied with achievements
  • Serenity and contentment — associated with psychosocial maturity
  • Preceded by: well-developed identity, meaningful close relationships, sense of contribution to the next generation
Despair ✗
  • Feel many decisions were wrong — but time is too short to change
  • Bitter and unaccepting of coming death
  • Expressed as anger and contempt for others
DEATH & DYING

Death & Dying — Kübler-Ross

Kübler-Ross interviewed 200+ terminally ill patients and identified five emotional reactions to impending death. These are not a fixed sequence — not everyone experiences all stages, nor in this order. The same reactions can also apply to other major losses (divorce, job loss, loss of a loved one).

Stage 1

Denial

Behaves as if diagnosis wasn't received. Seeks second opinions. Continues life normally. Adaptive — creates time to adjust.

Stage 2

Anger

"Why me?" Lashes out at loved ones and medical staff. Blames others. Expressing the unfairness and injustice of death.

Stage 3

Bargaining

Tries to negotiate more time — with doctors, God, the universe. "Let me live to see my child graduate." Often unrealistic. A way of holding on.

Stage 4

Depression

Deep sadness, withdrawal, loss of interest in activities. Mourning one's own impending death before it happens.

Stage 5

Acceptance

Peace and calm. Withdrawing from most relationships except the very close. Approaches death with equanimity. Terminal patients' words tend to become more positive as death approaches (Goranson et al., 2017).

DABDA — Denial · Anger · Bargaining · Depression · Acceptance
RESEARCH METHODS

Cross-Sectional vs. Longitudinal Research

Developmental psychologists use two main methods to study change over time. Choosing between them involves trade-offs of time, cost, and data quality.

LongitudinalCross-Sectional
What it is Same group of individuals tracked over a long time period (e.g., teenagers from age 13 tracked until age 35) Different age groups studied at the same point in time (e.g., group 1: teens 13–18 yrs; group 2: young adults 21–25 yrs; group 3: adults 35–40 yrs — all tested simultaneously)
Advantages Tracks individual patterns over time; provides the best data on continuity/discontinuity of behaviour; rich, detailed data Cost-effective; completed quickly; no drop-out problem
Disadvantages Very time-consuming and expensive; high drop-out rate (participants move, die, withdraw); participants may "wise up" to the study (contamination of results) Less rich data; highly susceptible to cohort effects
Cohort concern The tracked group may share unusual characteristics not typical of that age group (e.g., tracked group lived through a war) Differences between age groups may reflect their different cultural/historical upbringing rather than true developmental change

Cohort effects — differences between age groups that stem from growing up in different social, cultural, or historical conditions, rather than from developmental change itself. A cohort is a generation or age group (e.g., baby boomers, those who grew up during a war, the digital-native generation). Always ask: are these differences due to development, or just different life experiences across generations?
