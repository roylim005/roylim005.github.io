1.1
What Is Psychology?
▼
Psychology

The scientific study of behavior and mental processes. Behavior includes actions, feelings, and biological states (e.g. sleep). Mental processes include problem solving, intelligence, and memory.

Misconceptions

Popular media focuses heavily on therapy and mental health — but psychology is a broad science. Most doctoral-level psychologists complete rigorous scientific training alongside clinical work.

Psychology is research-based. Every idea in the field is subject to scientific study and peer-reviewed scrutiny.

Confirmation Bias (watch out for this!)

We tend to interpret behaviour in ways that confirm our existing beliefs — overlooking disconfirming evidence. Psychologists use systematic methods to guard against this.

Scope of the Field

Neuroscience Learning & Memory Development Motivation & Emotion Gender Criminal Behaviour Aging Sports Psychology Clinical Practice Education

Pseudopsychology vs. Psychology

✓ Psychology
Grounded in scientific method; conclusions limited to data; subject to replication and peer review.
✗ Pseudopsychology
Sounds persuasive but lacks scientific basis. Example: "We only use 10% of our brain" — false and debunked.
Critical Thinking — Analysing and evaluating information and applying it to other situations. Being a skeptical, not merely accepting, consumer of information.
1.2
The Science of Psychology: Goals, Hypotheses & Methods
▼

4 Goals of Psychology

1. Describe
Observe and record events. Understanding how events relate to one another.
2. Predict
If two events co-occur reliably, we can anticipate future occurrences.
3. Explain
Understand the causes of behaviour, usually via theories.
4. Control/Change
Apply understanding to modify behaviour for better outcomes.
Theory

An explanation of why and how a behaviour occurs. Provides general guidelines, not universal rules. Subject to revision.

The Scientific Method — 5 Steps

  1. Define the issue. Review prior literature; formulate a question about behaviour.
  2. Form a testable hypothesis. Operationally define variables so the study can be replicated.
  3. Choose a research strategy. Select participants, setting, and method.
  4. Conduct the study. Collect data systematically.
  5. Analyse & conclude. Use statistics to support or reject the hypothesis; replicate.

Types of Hypotheses

Predictive Hypothesis
States expected relationships between variables. Cannot establish cause-and-effect. Used with descriptive/correlational methods.
Causal Hypothesis
States how one variable causes change in another. Requires experimental control and random assignment.

Replication & the Replication Crisis

Only ⅓ to ½ of psychological studies produce similar results in large-scale replication projects. Causes include confirmation bias, small samples, and questionable research practices. Meta-analyses (combining findings from multiple studies) help establish reliability.

1.3
Research Methods & Sampling
▼

Selecting Participants

Population vs. Sample

Population of interest — everyone the researcher wants to study.
Sample — the subset actually tested; should be representative.
Random sample — every member has an equal chance of selection; minimises sampling bias.

Samples of convenience (e.g. college students) limit generalisability. The more representative the sample, the more generalisable the findings.

Research Methods at a Glance

Method What it does Strength Limitation
Naturalistic Observation Observe behaviour in its natural setting High ecological validity Reactivity; no cause-effect
Case Study In-depth study of 1 or few participants Rich detail on rare conditions Poor generalisability
Survey Questionnaire with large groups Fast, broad data collection Wording bias; self-report honesty
Correlational Measure relationship between ≥2 variables Enables prediction Cannot establish causation (3rd-variable problem)
Experiment Manipulate IV; randomly assign participants Only method for cause-effect Artificial lab settings; ethical limits
Quasi-Experiment Manipulate IV but no random assignment Useful when randomisation is unethical Confounds reduce causal confidence

Key Experimental Vocabulary

Independent Variable (IV)

The variable the experimenter manipulates — the presumed cause.

Dependent Variable (DV)

The variable that is measured — the presumed effect.

Confounding Variable

Any variable (other than the IV) that could explain changes in the DV. Must be controlled.

Placebo Effect

Participants show change simply because they believe a treatment is working. Controlled by double-blind designs.

Correlation Coefficients

Range: −1.00 to +1.00. Closer to ±1.00 = stronger relationship. Closer to 0 = weaker.

Positive correlation: variables move in the same direction (e.g. perceived stress ↑ → blood pressure ↑).
Negative correlation: variables move in opposite directions (e.g. video game time ↑ → school performance ↓).

⚠️ Correlation ≠ causation.
1.4
Ethical Principles of Research
▼

Oversight Bodies

IRB / HREC
Reviews research proposals using human participants to ensure ethical compliance.
IACUC
Reviews research using non-human animal subjects.

Core Ethical Principles (APA, 2002)

  1. Participant welfare — protect from harm; researcher must correct any negative effects.
  2. Informed consent — participants must be clearly told the study's purpose, risks, compensation, and right to withdraw.
  3. Confidentiality — personal data cannot be shared without consent or legal order.
  4. Deception — only permitted when necessary and when no non-deceptive alternative exists; must be justified by research value.
  5. Debriefing — after any deception, researchers must fully disclose the true purpose and correct misconceptions.
  6. No coercion — participation must be fully voluntary; withdrawal must be permitted at any time.
Milgram (1963) — classic example of ethical violation. 65% of participants shocked to maximum levels. Would NOT be approved today due to psychological harm.

Animal Research Guidelines

Animals must be treated humanely. Discomfort, illness, and pain must be minimised and justified by expected knowledge gains. Researchers are responsible for daily care.

Online/Digital Research — Special Challenges

Collecting data via smartphones or social media raises unique consent and privacy issues. Researchers must ensure data security and adequately inform participants — even when data is collected passively.

1.5
History of Psychology
▼
"Psychology has a long past but only a short history" — Ebbinghaus (1910). Formal beginning: Wilhelm Wundt's laboratory, University of Leipzig, 1879.

Early Schools of Thought

Structuralism — Wundt & Titchener
Used introspection (trained self-observation) to identify 3 elements of consciousness: sensations, images, and feelings. First formal school of psychology.
Functionalism — William James
Focused on how mental processes operate and help organisms adapt to their environment. Linked to Darwin's natural selection. Forerunner of evolutionary psychology.
Behaviourism — Watson & Skinner
Only observable behaviour is valid data. Stimuli → Responses → Consequences. Dominant force in American psychology until the 1960s.
Psychoanalysis — Freud
The unconscious (impulses, desires we're unaware of) drives behaviour. Conflict between conscious reasoning and unconscious instincts. Led to psychodynamic perspective.

Little Albert (Watson & Rayner)

Conditioned a 9-month-old to fear a white rat by pairing it with a loud noise. Demonstrated that observable stimuli and responses are the appropriate focus of psychology — but this study would be considered deeply unethical today.

1.6
Modern Psychological Perspectives
▼

Most modern psychologists take an eclectic approach — combining multiple perspectives for a fuller understanding of behaviour.

Biological
Genes · Brain · Neuroscience
Looks for physical/neurological causes. Includes brain imaging (EEG), genetics, and biochemistry.
Evolutionary
Natural Selection · Adaptation
Behaviours that increase survival are selected. Asks: what is the survival function of this behaviour?
Psychodynamic
Unconscious · Childhood · Conflict
Internal, often unconscious motives and early experiences drive behaviour. Rooted in Freud, modified by successors.
Behavioural
Stimuli · Responses · Reinforcement
Behaviour is learned through environmental stimuli and consequences (rewards/punishment).
Sociocultural
Culture · Gender · Ethnicity
Cannot fully explain behaviour without understanding culture, gender identity, ethnicity, and social context.
Humanistic
Free Will · Self-concept · Growth
Behaviour stems from individual choices shaped by self-concept and self-esteem. Maslow, Rogers. Emphasises inner resources.
Cognitive
Thoughts · Memory · Decision-making
Behaviour explained through how people process information — beliefs, expectations, problem solving.

Positive Psychology

Grew from the cognitive emphasis on thinking patterns. Led by Seligman and Diener. Scientifically studies happiness, well-being, and human flourishing to help individuals, families, and communities thrive.

Eclectic approach — most real-world psychologists integrate multiple perspectives rather than adhering rigidly to one.

Gender, Ethnicity & Milestones in Psychology
▼

Pioneer Women

NameMilestone
Margaret Washburn (1871–1939)First woman to earn a doctorate in psychology (1894, under Titchener). Research on animal behaviour and motor-mind connections.
Mary Calkins (1863–1930)First female APA President, 1905. Memory research. Studied under William James at Harvard.
Christine Ladd-Franklin (1847–1930)Research on colour vision in the early 1900s.
Karen Horney (1885–1952)Environmental and cultural factors in personality development; challenged Freudian assumptions.

Pioneer Minority Figures

NameMilestone
Gilbert Haven Jones (1881–1966)First African American to earn a PhD in psychology — Germany, 1909.
Francis Sumner (1895–1954)First African American PhD in psychology from a US university (1920). "Father of African American psychology." Founded psychology department at Howard University.
Kenneth Clark (1914–2005) & Mamie Phipps Clark (1917–1983)Doll experiments showing Black children's self-perceptions. Cited in landmark Brown v. Board of Education (1954).
Inez Prosser (1897–1934)First African American woman to earn a psychology PhD (1933). Researched self-esteem in Black children at segregated vs. integrated schools.

Current State (approximate figures)

Women now earn ~73% of psychology doctorates — but female psychologists still earn less than male counterparts after controlling for age, race, hours, and education.

Minorities make up ~39% of the US population but ~16% of active psychologists. Graduate representation has grown from 8% (1976–93) to nearly 20% today, though underrepresentation persists.
