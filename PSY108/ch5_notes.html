Motive
The tendency to desire and seek out positive incentives or rewards and to avoid negative outcomes (Atkinson, 1958/1983; McClelland, 1987).
Emotion
A complex reaction to some internal or external event involving physiological reactions, behavioural reactions, facial expressions, cognition, and affective responses (Lazarus, 1991).

Why Do Emotions Matter?

Function of Emotions Emotions help us attend and respond to important situations and convey our intentions to others. Without the ability to feel, we lose the ability to: connect concepts/events to meaning, value one course of action over another, and prioritise decisions. "To know but not to feel" leaves us unable to function effectively in everyday life.

Basic Emotions — Theorists at a Glance

Plutchik (1980)
8 basic emotions in 4 pairs of opposites. All other emotions (e.g., envy, regret, thrill) are combinations of these basic eight — called secondary emotions.
Ekman (1993)
7 universal emotions: happiness, sadness, anger, fear, surprise, disgust, and contempt. Cross-cultural studies show people decode facial expressions in much the same way regardless of race or culture.
Cultural Influence
Secondary emotions vary considerably across cultures. Cultures also differ in norms for expressing emotions — some are open, others reserved. Shaped by both evolution and culture.
Masuda et al. (2008) — Cultural Study Japanese participants judged a central person's emotion by also looking at surrounding people's expressions (collectivist — group context matters). Western participants focused only on the central person (individualist — individual expression matters). Supported by eye-tracking data showing Japanese participants spent more time looking at surrounding faces.
5.1

Theories of Motivation

No single theory explains all motivation. Each has strengths and weaknesses.

Theory Core Claim Key Strength Key Weakness
Instinct (James, 1890) Motives are genetically determined instincts that evolved to support survival/procreation Explains innate behaviours Endless list; impossible to verify; many "instincts" may be learned
Drive Reduction (Hull, 1943) Motivation stems from reducing uncomfortable internal states (drives) caused by unmet needs Explains basic biological drives and homeostasis Cannot explain overeating, thrill-seeking, or arousal-increasing behaviours
Arousal Theory (Yerkes-Dodson, 1908) We perform best at moderate arousal; we seek/reduce arousal to stay at our optimal level Explains individual differences in sensation-seeking Optimal arousal level varies by person and task complexity
Self-Determination Theory (Deci & Ryan, 2008) Humans need competence, autonomy, and relatedness; motivation can be autonomous or controlled Distinguishes intrinsic vs extrinsic motivation Hard to measure autonomy and relatedness precisely
Maslow's Hierarchy (1970) Needs form a pyramid; lower needs must be met before higher ones motivate behaviour Intuitive; widely applied in business Research support is weak; people often skip levels

Drive Reduction: Primary vs Secondary

Primary Drives
Biological needs: hunger, thirst, warmth. Regulated by negative feedback loops maintaining homeostasis.
Secondary Drives
Learned through experience: need for achievement, affiliation, understanding (Murray, 1938). Vary across individuals.

Yerkes-Dodson Curve

Key Principle Too little arousal (sleepy) → poor performance. Moderate arousal (alert, calm) → best performance. Too much arousal (test anxiety) → poor performance again.
Simple Tasks — tolerate higher arousal
Driving on a quiet, familiar road is repetitive and low-demand. A little extra stimulation actually helps — like turning on upbeat pop music to stay alert and focused. Without it, you might zone out.
Complex Tasks — need lower arousal
Memorising a list of new vocabulary or studying for an exam requires deep concentration. Being too wired or anxious scrambles your thinking. A calm, quiet environment lets your working memory do its job properly.

Intrinsic vs Extrinsic Motivation

Intrinsic Motivation
Incentives from within — enjoyment, pride, sense of accomplishment. Predicts academic success, less drug use, less cheating, better exercise adherence.
Extrinsic Motivation
Incentives from outside — grades, money, peer approval. Can undermine intrinsic motivation if overused.

Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs

Hunger, thirst, warmth, sleep — must be satisfied first. Without these, higher needs cannot motivate behaviour.
Safe housing, physical security, stability. Activated once physiological needs are met.
Motivation to be with others, to be loved and appreciated. Social belonging.
Self-esteem, achievement, knowledge, beauty and order. Ladder of psychological growth.
Reaching full potential; spiritual fulfilment. Maslow believed few people reach this level.
Critical Evaluation Research support is limited. People frequently skip levels — e.g., skipping lunch to study (esteem without physiological satisfaction). The hierarchy is culturally biased toward individualist values.

Freud's Psychodynamic Theory of Motivation

Psychodynamic Theory (Freud)
Motivation comes from the depths of the unconscious mind. The Id contains two basic instinctual desires: Eros (erotic/sexual desires) and Thanatos (aggressive/destructive impulses). These unconscious drives still influence behaviour and sometimes surface in dreams, fantasies, or slips of the tongue.

Cognitive Theory & Locus of Control

Human behaviour is motivated by our cognitions and expectations — specifically, our beliefs about our ability to control events in our lives.

Internal Locus of Control
Belief that outcomes depend on your own ability and effort. "I can influence what happens to me." Associated with high achievers.
External Locus of Control
Belief that outcomes depend on luck, fate, or task difficulty. "Things happen to me regardless of what I do." Associated with low achievers.

Achievement Motivation

A desire to meet an internal standard of excellence. Follows the flow: Motivation → Behaviour → Achievement.

TypeGoal PreferenceLocus of ControlReason
High Achievers Moderate difficulty — challenging but achievable Internal (ability, effort) Too easy = no satisfaction; too hard = no hope of success
Low Achievers Too easy OR too hard External (luck, task difficulty) Either way, failure can be blamed on external factors — no personal accountability
Seligman — Optimism, Pessimism & Explanatory Style How we explain success and failure affects motivation and wellbeing.

Optimist failing a test: "The test was too hard" (external) or "I'll try harder next time" (changeable). Takes credit for successes.

Pessimist failing a test: "I guess I'm dumb" (internal, stable) or "Nothing I do will make a difference" (unchangeable). Attributes good outcomes to luck. Leads to poorer performance, mental health, and physical wellbeing.

Individualist vs Collectivist Cultures & Emotion Expression

Individualist Culture (e.g., Western)
Promotes individual needs and desires. Emotions are seen as personal expression of identity and power. People tend to be more expressive of emotions openly.
Collectivist Culture (e.g., Asian)
Prioritises the good of the group. Individual needs are minimised. Belief that expressing emotion disrupts intra-group relationships. Results in low self-disclosure, both verbally and non-verbally.
5.2

Hunger and Eating

Origins of Hunger: A Multi-Source Feedback System

Hunger does not have a single on/off switch. It results from a complex network of signals from multiple body sources to the brain.

Stomach BIO
Contractions signal emptiness. Releases ghrelin (hunger hormone). Distension signals fullness. Ghrelin drops when stomach fills with nutrients (not just volume).
Liver BIO
Monitors glucose (energy burned) and glycogen (energy stored). Converts excess glucose → glycogen (off signal). Converts glycogen back → glucose when reserves low (on signal).
Endocrine System BIO
Insulin (pancreas): moves glucose into cells → blood sugar drops → hunger. CCK (small intestine): released after eating; shuts off urge to eat. Leptin (fat cells): signals fat reserves are full → reduces hunger.
Hypothalamus BIO
LH (lateral): "on switch" for hunger — destroyed LH → rat stops eating and dies. VMH (ventromedial): contributes to satiety — destroyed VMH → rat overeats massively.
Set Point
The weight the body naturally tries to maintain through homeostatic regulation. When weight drops below set point, hunger increases, making dieting very hard.

Key Hormones at a Glance

Intuitive vs Non-Intuitive Eating

Intuitive Eating
Driven by physiological hunger and satiety cues. Associated with lower BMI, healthier outcomes.
Non-Intuitive Eating
Driven by emotional, cultural, or situational cues unrelated to energy needs. Linked to weight gain and disordered eating.

Why Dieting Fails

Biological Resistance
  1. Calorie deficit triggers increased hunger (body fights starvation)
  2. Metabolic adaptation: resting metabolic rate drops to conserve fat reserves. In plain terms — you eat less hoping to burn fat, but your body responds by burning less overall, quietly cancelling out the deficit you created. You want to burn; the lower metabolic rate means less gets burned. Combined with increased hunger, both forces work against you simultaneously.
  3. Set point pulls weight back up once deficit ends
Psychological Resistance Dietary restraint leads to bingeing. When dieters feel they've "ruined" their diet, they overconsume ("all or nothing" thinking). Emotional distress amplifies this. Solution: permanent eating habit changes + exercise (preserves muscle mass, combats metabolic adaptation).

5.2.5 Eating Disorders

Definition: Alternating bouts of bingeing + compensatory behaviours (purging, fasting, excessive exercise).

Profile: Typically young female, average–slightly above average weight. ~1–1.5% of young females in a 12-month period. 10× more female than male.

Causes/correlates: Low self-esteem, depression, perfectionism, troubled family background. Bingeing/purging used to cope with anxiety and loss of control.

Health risks: Dehydration, electrolyte imbalance, cardiac problems, oesophageal damage. 2.33× higher mortality risk vs control group.
Definition: Self-starvation, intense fear of weight gain, distorted body image. Client must be significantly below normal weight.

Profile: ~0.4% of females in a 12-month period; lifetime risk ≥1.42%. Predominantly middle/upper-class industrialised countries. 10× more female than male.

Causes/correlates: Perfectionism, low self-esteem, depression, faulty thinking, genetic predisposition (higher concordance in identical twins). Societal pressure for extreme thinness.

Health risks: 5–10% die from the disorder. BMI, alcohol use, and social adjustment affect prognosis.
Definition: Recurrent binge eating WITHOUT compensatory behaviours (no purging/fasting). Most common eating disorder.

Profile: Lifetime risk ~1.25–2.6%. 1.6% of adult females, 0.8% of adult males in 12-month period.

Causes/correlates: Higher impulsivity, motivational ambivalence about food, rumination triggers bingeing, poor emotional regulation. Bingeing as maladaptive coping.
5.3

Sexual Motivation

Sexual Desire
Motivation and interest in engaging in sexual activity; the first step toward sexual arousal. Influenced by biology, learning, and culture.

Biological Factors

Testosterone
Key hormone regulating libido in both sexes. Produced by testes (male) and adrenal glands (female). Low levels → disinterest in sex.
Estrogen
Regulates female estrus in most mammals. In humans, estrogen is not closely tied to libido — women can desire sex at any point in the cycle.
Oxytocin & Dopamine
Oxytocin: released during sex; promotes feelings of closeness. Dopamine: pleasure/reward neurotransmitter; influences desire.
Important Note Sexual desire is not merely chemical. Senses (visual cues, smell, touch, erogenous zones), personal thoughts, and cultural expectations all shape desire. Visual stimulation tends to have greater effect on men; olfactory cues are meaningful for both sexes.

Sexual Response Cycle (Masters & Johnson, 1966)

01
Excitement
↑ heart rate & BP. Genital engorgement, vaginal lubrication, penile erection.
02
Plateau
Arousal peaks & steadies. Rapid breathing. Full erection/vaginal expansion.
03
Orgasm
Peak BP/HR. Pelvic contractions. Ejaculation in males. Subjective experience similar in both sexes.
04
Resolution
Body returns to baseline. Males: refractory period. Females: can be re-aroused immediately.
Gender Difference The refractory period (inability to re-orgasm) is unique to males and increases with age. Females lack this, enabling multiple orgasms.

Sexual Orientation

Sexual Orientation
One's sexual attraction for members of the same and/or other sex. Exists on a continuum (Kinsey scale), not discrete categories. Can change over time, especially in women.
Causes of Sexual Orientation Note: Most research findings below specifically studied homosexuality — not sexual orientation broadly — because researchers historically treated heterosexuality as the default requiring no explanation. This is a known bias in the field. The general conclusion at the end applies to all orientations.

Genetics: Higher concordance in identical vs fraternal twins. Prenatal factors: Fraternal birth order effect; maternal immune hypothesis; left-handedness link. Gender-nonconforming childhood behaviour correlates with homosexuality (but not deterministically — many gay adults showed gender-conforming behaviour in childhood).

General conclusion (all orientations): Sexual orientation likely results from the interaction of biological, psychological, and environmental factors — a unique pathway for every person.
5.4

Theories and Expression of Emotion

Components of Emotion Physiological reactions · Behavioural reactions · Facial expressions · Cognition · Affective (subjective feeling) responses

Theory Comparison

TheorySequenceCore ClaimKey Critique
James-Lange (1884) Stimulus → Physiological response → Emotion Emotion is the pattern of physiological arousal Different emotions have very similar physiological profiles (Cannon); arousal sometimes follows emotion awareness
Cannon-Bard Stimulus → Brain → Emotion & Physiology simultaneously Emotion originates in the brain, not the body; physiological responses can precede or follow emotional awareness Doesn't fully account for body-emotion interaction shown in later research
Facial Feedback Hypothesis (McIntosh, 1996) Facial expression → Brain feedback → Emotion modified Smiling makes you happier; frowning makes you feel worse — via blood flow affecting brain temperature Effect sizes are debated; meta-analysis (138 studies) supports modest effect
Two-Factor / Schachter-Singer (1962) Stimulus → Undifferentiated arousal → Cognitive label → Emotion Emotion = arousal + cognitive interpretation of context Subsequent research found little support; people still feel emotion when arousal is pharmacologically blocked
Cognitive-Mediational / Lazarus (1995) Stimulus → Cognitive appraisal → Emotion Our appraisal of a situation determines which emotion we feel. Reappraisal can regulate emotion. Zajonc's mere exposure effect shows emotion without cognition is possible

Facial Feedback — Key Points

Mechanism
Facial muscle configuration → changes blood flow → alters brain temperature → affects neurotransmitter release → influences mood.
Evidence
Smiling while smelling → rates smells more positively. Frowning in sun → increased aggression. Meta-analysis of 138 studies supports the effect.

Schachter-Singer Classic Study

Epinephrine Experiment (1962) Participants injected with epinephrine (stimulant). Informed group: knew the cause of arousal → mood not influenced by confederate. Uninformed group: no explanation for arousal → used context to label emotion → matched confederate's mood (angry or happy). Supports that cognitive labelling of unexplained arousal shapes emotional experience.

Lazarus — Cognitive-Mediational Theory

Practical Application Same event → different appraisals → different emotions. Reappraisal (seeing an event as a challenge rather than a threat) is a key emotion regulation strategy. Both males and females can successfully regulate emotions through reappraisal, though brain regions engaged differ (McRae et al., 2008).

Schachter-Singer vs Lazarus — Key Distinction

Schachter-Singer: Arousal First
You first feel a vague, undifferentiated physical arousal (racing heart, tension). Then you scan the context to figure out why — and that label becomes the emotion.

"I feel jittery and the person next to me is angry… I must be feeling angry too."

Sequence: Stimulus → Arousal → Cognitive label → Emotion
Lazarus: Appraisal First
No undifferentiated arousal stage. Your evaluation of the situation comes first, before any arousal — and that appraisal directly generates the emotion.

"This bear is dangerous" → fear. No need to feel the racing heart first.

Sequence: Stimulus → Cognitive appraisal → Emotion
The Core Disagreement Both theories involve cognition — but when it happens differs. Schachter-Singer needs unexplained arousal to exist first before cognition labels it. Lazarus argues appraisal is primary and does not need prior arousal to trigger emotion.

Does Lazarus Ignore Physiological Arousal?

Important Clarification No — physiological arousal is not absent in Lazarus. It is simply not a prerequisite. Instead, arousal is bundled inside the emotion itself as one of its components. The appraisal generates the emotion, and the emotion includes the racing heart, muscle tension, and so on. The arousal didn't cause the fear — the appraisal did, and the racing heart is simply part of what fear feels like.

The Big Picture: Emotion Theories on a Spectrum

Use this three-way contrast as a backbone to organise all emotion theories for the exam:

TheoryWhat Comes FirstRole of Physiology
James-Lange Physiological arousal Physiology is the emotion
Schachter-Singer Physiological arousal Physiology is the trigger; cognition labels it to create emotion
Lazarus Cognitive appraisal Physiology is part of the resulting emotion, not its cause
Cannon-Bard Brain (simultaneous) Physiology and subjective emotion occur at the same time, both triggered by the brain
Exam Tip James-Lange and Lazarus are mirror opposites — one starts with the body, the other starts with the mind. Schachter-Singer sits in between, needing both. Cannon-Bard stands apart, rejecting the idea that one causes the other.

Zajonc — Mere Exposure Effect

Mere Exposure Effect
We tend to prefer stimuli we have encountered more frequently — even without knowing what they are. Suggests emotion can occur independently of conscious cognition.

Culture and Emotional Expression

Universal Aspects
Ekman (1993): 7 universal emotions — happiness, sadness, anger, fear, surprise, disgust, and contempt — recognisable across cultures. Happiness easiest to identify; fear and surprise most easily confused.
Cultural Display Rules
Each culture has norms about when/how to express emotions. E.g., displaying fear in a US business meeting is inappropriate. Arab culture requires anger response to insult. People may regulate appraisals to avoid violating display rules.
Individualist Cultures
Promote individual needs and desires. Emotions are expressions of personal identity. People tend to be more openly expressive of emotions. Focus on the individual's own facial expression when judging emotions in others.
Collectivist Cultures
Prioritise group harmony. Belief that expressing emotion disrupts intra-group relationships. Low self-disclosure verbally and non-verbally. When judging others' emotions, factor in the surrounding social context (Masuda et al., 2008).
