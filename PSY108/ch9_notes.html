OVERVIEW

What Is Development?

Development = changes in behaviour and abilities across the entire lifespan. It integrates physical, cognitive, and social dimensions simultaneously.

Central tension: Nature vs. Nurture — not an either/or. Genes and environment interact to shape who we become. Gene expression can be altered by environment; environment's effect can be moderated by genes.

9.1

Human Development: How Does It All Begin?

Prenatal Stages

At conception: sperm (23 chromosomes) + ovum (23 chromosomes) = zygote (23 pairs). Gestation ≈ 38–40 weeks, divided into three stages:

Stage Timing Key Feature Critical Notes
Germinal Days 1–14 Rapid cell division; zygote → blastocyst (≈100 cells) Blastocyst implants in uterine wall ~day 9
Embryonic Weeks 3–8 Formation of major organs & biological systems Most precarious; most miscarriages & genetic defects here; most sensitive to teratogens
Fetal Week 9 → birth Growth & maturation; movement begins by week 14 Viability at ~24 weeks; responsive to sound, light, touch in last trimester
Teratogens & Prenatal Environment

Teratogen = any environmental agent that can harm the developing embryo (drugs, alcohol, disease, chemicals). Timing is critical — most severe effects during embryonic stage.

Teratogen

Alcohol / FAS

Fetal Alcohol Syndrome: low birth weight, brain abnormalities, reduced intellectual functioning, facial/limb deformities

Teratogen

Smoking

Reduces oxygen to fetus → lower birth weight, respiratory problems, irritability

Teratogen

Cocaine / Crack

Premature birth, underweight, irritable, poor feeding; long-term impulsivity & behavioural problems

Other Factor

Maternal Stress

High cortisol → preterm birth risk, autism spectrum symptoms (males), behavioural/emotional problems in childhood

Chromosomal

Down Syndrome

Extra 21st chromosome; distinct facial features, heart defects, intellectual disability — detectable prenatally

9.2

Physical Development in Infancy & Childhood

Brain Development

At birth, billions of neurons exist but connections are limited and myelination is incomplete. Experience + learning drive rapid neural growth. Key principles:

Use it or lose it: Neural pathways used repeatedly become permanent; unused ones are pruned. Stimulating environments strengthen connections; impoverished ones weaken them.

Plasticity — young brains are highly adaptable; damaged areas can be compensated by other regions. Plasticity gradually decreases with age but persists throughout adulthood.

9.3

Cognitive Development in Infancy & Childhood

Perceptual Development
Sense

Vision

Nearsighted at birth; prefer complex, high-contrast stimuli; faces preferred; depth perception (visual cliff) by ~6 months; own-race face recognition by 3 months

Sense

Hearing

Responds to sound in utero (~week 20); recognises mother's voice shortly after birth; prefers "motherese" (baby talk); can discriminate consonants

Sense

Touch & Smell

Breastfed infants distinguish mother's smell at 3 days; massage accelerates neurological development and weight gain in premature infants

Sense

Taste

Functional at birth; infants prefer sweet tastes

Piaget's Theory of Cognitive Development

Three Core Concepts:

Schema — any mental concept or framework
Assimilation — fitting new info into existing schema (e.g., calling a cow a "doggy")
Accommodation — modifying existing schema to fit new info (creating "cow" category)

Disequilibrium drives accommodation — discomfort motivates us to restore mental harmony.

Stage Age Key Ability Key Limitations
Sensorimotor 0–2 yrs Learning through senses & actions; develops object permanence (~8 months); then symbolic thinking "Out of sight, out of mind" (before 8 months)
Preoperational 2–7 yrs Rapid language & symbol use; pretend play; ~10,000-word vocabulary by age 6 Centration, egocentrism, lack of conservation; illogical thinking
Concrete Operations 7–11 yrs Logical thinking; conservation mastered; reduced egocentrism; empathy grows Limited to concrete, observable situations — abstract reasoning still difficult
Formal Operations 12+ yrs Abstract reasoning; hypothetical thinking; scientific/mathematical concepts; identity exploration Return of egocentrism (imaginary audience, personal fable)
Preoperational Limitation

Centration

Focus on one feature only. Poured liquid looks like "more" in a tall glass → fails conservation test

Preoperational Limitation

Egocentrism

"If I can't see you, you can't see me." Cannot take another's perspective

Preoperational Limitation

Lack of Conservation

Cannot understand that quantity remains the same despite change in appearance

Piaget's stages in order: Sensorimotor → Preoperational → Concrete → Formal — "Small People Can Fly"
Vygotsky's Sociocultural Theory
Piaget (Internal)
  • Cognition develops through internal schemas
  • Universal stages; fixed sequence
  • Child as independent discoverer
  • Biology-focused
Vygotsky (Social/Cultural)
  • Mental processes begin externally via social interaction
  • Culture shapes the direction of development
  • Child guided by more capable others
  • ZPD + Scaffolding model

Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD) — the gap between what a child can do alone and what they can do with help.
Scaffolding — adult gradually reduces support as the child gains competence.
Private speech — self-talk that guides thinking; peaks at 5–7 years; internalises by ~9 years; returns in adults during problem-solving.

Moral Reasoning
Level (Kohlberg)Basis for DecisionsTypical Age
PreconventionalAvoid punishment / gain rewards; egocentricChildren
ConventionalSocial rules, approval of others, laws; rigid applicationOlder children – adults
PostconventionalUniversal ethical principles; considers circumstances; internal standardsSome adults (rare)

Gilligan's critique: Kohlberg's model reflects male perspective. She proposed women prioritise care and relationships vs. men's justice and fairness. However, research has not consistently supported clear gender differences — both males and females use justice and care reasoning.

Psychosocial Development in Infancy & Childhood

Temperament (Chess & Thomas)
Type

Easy (~40%)

Good mood, regular eating/sleeping, adaptable, approaches new stimuli readily

Type

Difficult (~10%)

Intense negative emotions, irregular schedule, withdraws from new situations

Type

Slow-to-Warm-Up (~15%)

Between easy and difficult; needs time to adapt to new situations

Temperament is biologically based. Goodness of fit between child's temperament and parental responses influences attachment and development.

Attachment (Harlow → Ainsworth)

Harlow's finding: Attachment is built on contact comfort, not feeding. Baby monkeys preferred cloth surrogate over wire surrogate with food.

Signs of attachment: Separation anxiety (~6–7 months; peaks 14–18 months) and Stranger anxiety (~8–10 months).

Gender-Role Development

Gender permanence (constancy) — understanding gender won't change; typically not grasped before age 6. Yet children are already rigid in gender stereotyping from around age 3.

Bem's Gender-Schema Theory combines social learning (modelling + reinforcement) with cognitive development: children observe which behaviours are reinforced for each sex, then build schemas that guide their own behaviour. These schemas are culturally defined and vary by society.

Nature + Nurture on Gender

  • Nature: Prenatal hormones influence gender-typed play; male rhesus monkeys prefer wheeled toys; female hormonal exposure increases male-typical behaviour
  • Nurture: Parents assign gender-specific toys & chores; teachers give more attention to boys in class; peer groups reinforce gender norms; media perpetuates stereotypes
9.5

Physical Changes in Adolescence & Adulthood

Puberty

Puberty = sexual maturation process. Females: avg onset ~10 yrs; Males: ~12 yrs (females ≈ 2 years earlier). Growth spurt adds ~10 inches and ~40 lbs. First menstruation = menarche.

Early maturation effects:
Females: Higher depression/anxiety, disordered eating, lower school performance, greater sexual victimisation risk
Males: More delinquency, alcohol/tobacco use, sexual activity
Supportive family = protective factor; deviant peers = risk amplifier

Brain Changes in Adolescence
Brain Region

Cerebellum

Neurons & connections increase → improved balance & motor coordination (explains early teen awkwardness)

Brain Region

Amygdala

More active in teens than adults; larger in males. Regulates emotional reactions → heightened emotional responses

Brain Region

Prefrontal Cortex

Last to mature (early 20s!); second wave of gray matter then pruning. Controls judgment, impulse control, planning → explains poor teen decision-making

Physical Changes: Early → Later Adulthood
PeriodKey Physical Changes
Early adulthood (20s–30s)Biological prime; peak strength, stamina, coordination; visual acuity peaks early 20s
Middle adulthood (40s–50s)Skin wrinkles; hair thins/grays; weight gain as metabolism slows; reading glasses needed (presbyopia)
Later adulthood (60s+)Muscle/weight loss; skin sagging; height loss (vertebral compression); age-related hearing loss

Menopause (~age 50): end of female reproductive years; declining estrogen; ovulation/menstruation stop.
Andropause (after ~60 in males): gradual hormone decline, reduced sperm concentration — but males can remain fertile into their 70s–90s.

9.6

Cognitive Changes in Adolescence & Adulthood

Adolescent Thinking: Formal Operations Revisited
Adolescent Cognition

Idealism of Youth

Abstract reasoning → belief they can solve world problems; heightened sensitivity to what's "right"

Adolescent Cognition

Imaginary Audience

Belief that others are constantly watching and judging them → self-consciousness peaks; performing for a perceived audience

Adolescent Cognition

Personal Fable

Belief that one is unique and invincible; "it won't happen to me" → fuels risk-taking behaviour (unprotected sex, drugs)

Postformal Thought (Adult Reasoning)
Adolescent: Dualistic Thinking
  • Black-or-white, right-or-wrong
  • One correct solution exists
  • Rules applied rigidly
Adult: Relativistic / Postformal
  • Multiple valid solutions may coexist
  • Context and circumstances matter
  • Pragmatic, experience-driven reasoning
Fluid vs. Crystallised Intelligence Across Adulthood
TypeWhat It IsNature/NurtureTrajectory
Fluid Intelligence Processing speed, attention, working memory, reaction time Primarily nature/biology Peaks with brain maturity; perceptual speed declines late 20s–30s; spatial/inductive reasoning declines after 50s
Crystallised Intelligence Knowledge, vocabulary, expertise, social judgment Primarily nurture/experience Increases into adulthood; mild decline after 70s
Fluid = flows fast but depletes early · Crystallised = grows richer with time

Emerging Adulthood

In developed nations, a transitional phase from late teens to mid-20s (emerging adulthood) — marked by extended education, delayed marriage, career exploration. Not universal: absent in many non-Western cultures and among economically disadvantaged youth in Western nations.

Relationships & Social Development

Key Relationship Facts

  • Marriage satisfaction predictors: Similar backgrounds, marrying in mid-to-late 20s, warm family history, supportive partner, financial stability, positive partner perception
  • Marriage dissatisfaction predictors: Boredom, contempt, defensiveness, criticism, negativity
  • Divorce risk factors: No children, lower education, young marriage age, parents' divorce, lower income
  • Parenthood: Correlates with lower marital satisfaction but higher meaning and happiness; life satisfaction decreases with baby's arrival but children enrich lives overall
  • Empty nest: Marital satisfaction increases; women report improved well-being
9.8

Death & Dying

Kübler-Ross's Five Stages
Stage 1

Denial

Behaves as though diagnosis wasn't received; seeks second opinions; allows time to adjust

Stage 2

Anger

"Why me?" Lashing out at loved ones, medical staff; seeking to blame

Stage 3

Bargaining

Attempting to negotiate more time with doctors, a deity, or the universe; often unrealistic goals

Stage 4

Depression

Sadness, withdrawal, loss of interest — mourning one's own impending death

Stage 5

Acceptance

Peace and calm; withdrawal from most relationships; approaching death with equanimity

⚠ Not all dying people experience all stages, nor in this order. Personality, support systems, and illness type all shape the process.

DABDA — Denial · Anger · Bargaining · Depression · Acceptance
Bereavement & Grief

Bereavement = the experience of loss. Grief = emotional reaction to loss.

PhaseExperience
Impact / ShockDisbelief, numbness, operating "in a fog" — adaptive dulling of pain; especially intense if death was sudden
ConfrontationDeep despair, weeping, anxiety, guilt, anger; difficulty concentrating, sleeping, eating; realising the loss is real
Accommodation / AcceptanceReengage with life; memories internalised; refocus emotional energy on living; grief subsides (though timeline varies greatly)
