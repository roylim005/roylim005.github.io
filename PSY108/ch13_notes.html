What Is Abnormality?

There is no sharp line separating normal from abnormal behaviour. A judgement call is made when two or more indicators are present, considering the degree and frequency of the behaviour.

Definition A mental health disorder = dysfunction in thinking, emotions, or social behaviour that (1) is not typical or culturally expected, (2) causes distress, and (3) results in dysfunction in daily functioning.
Key insight from SU3: Behaviour exists on a continuum. Normality is statistically defined (bell curve), but being statistically rare does not automatically mean abnormal — context, culture, and functioning all matter.

Three Indicators of Abnormality

IndicatorWhat it meansExample
Not typical / culturally expected Behaviour occurs far less or more often than most people do; violates social/cultural norms. Norms vary across cultures and historical periods. Washing hands 50 times a day when most people wash a handful of times.
Distress Causes significant personal pain, emotional suffering, or distress to those around the person. Feeling overwhelming terror that prevents leaving the house.
Dysfunction Interferes with ability to hold a job, go to school, or form close relationships. The key question: does it affect daily functioning? Unable to go to work or maintain friendships because of the behaviour.
Exam note: The APA (1994) adds that the problem must reside within the person (biological, learned, or mental) and must not be a deliberate reaction to conditions like poverty or prejudice.

Models for Understanding Mental Health Disorders

Three broad models explain how mental health disorders arise. No single model is complete on its own.

Historical

Demon Model

Mental illness seen as the work of Satan or evil spirits. Treatment involved exorcism or punishment. Now considered unscientific.

Biological

Medical Model

Mental disorders are diseases caused by genetics, brain chemistry imbalances, or physical dysfunction. Treated with medication or surgery.

⚠ Weakness: "Doctor knows best" — patient becomes passive, may lead to unnecessary drug therapy, does not build coping skills.

Psychological

Psychological View

Dysfunction results from internal or external factors. Four perspectives: psychoanalytic (past conflicts), social learning (learned behaviour), cognitive (faulty thinking), humanistic (distorted self).

The Four Psychological Perspectives

PerspectiveCore IdeaKey Mechanism
PsychoanalyticPresent problems caused by unresolved conflicts from the pastChildhood trauma / relationships buried in the unconscious. Treatment: make the unconscious conscious.
Social LearningAbnormal behaviour is learned, just as normal behaviour isThrough associations, reinforcements, and punishments (classical/operant conditioning, modelling).
CognitiveInternal processes — expectations, biases, and cognitive errors — produce disordered behaviourIllogical thinking ("cognitive errors") distort perception of reality.
HumanisticDisorder arises from incongruence between ideal self and actual selfDistorted feeling of being unable to achieve true potential or live a meaningful life.

Sociocultural Approach

Takes into account social and cultural factors including age, race, gender, social roles, and socioeconomic status. Internal biological and psychological processes can only be fully understood within the larger social and cultural context.

Biopsychosocial Model — Integrating All Perspectives

Biological

  • Genetics
  • Neurotransmitter imbalance
  • Brain structure / function
  • Chemical imbalance

Psychological

  • Low self-esteem / negative body image
  • Cognitive errors / distorted thinking
  • Avoidance behaviours
  • Emotional distress / maladaptive behaviour

Sociocultural

  • Others criticising / ridiculing
  • Cultural stereotypes
  • Social roles, poverty, discrimination
  • Age, race, gender
Key point: No single theory is correct. Most disorders result from a combination of all three domains — hence "biopsychosocial".

The DSM Classification System

The Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders is the standard classification system used by clinicians worldwide. The current version is DSM-5-TR (2022).

What the DSM does

  • Classifies disorders by mental and behavioural symptoms
  • Provides specific diagnostic criteria for ~300 disorders
  • Atheoretical — does not speculate on causes
  • Creates common language for clinicians and researchers
  • Has important legal implications (e.g., fitness to stand trial)

Criticisms of the DSM

  • Too many disorders listed
  • No clear boundary between normal and abnormal
  • Some disorders not easily distinguishable from others
  • Clinician subjectivity and bias in diagnosis
  • Labelling risk: stigma and self-fulfilling prophecy
  • Comorbidity: symptoms often overlap multiple disorders
Comorbidity — when a person meets the criteria for more than one disorder at the same time. Common across most disorder categories.
DSM-5 update: Used the "spectrum" concept to group related disorders (e.g., autism spectrum disorder, schizophrenia spectrum disorder) that share biological or psychological qualities.

Anxiety, OCD & Trauma-Related Disorders

Anxiety disorders are differentiated mainly by their focus (what they fear) and duration. Anxiety becomes disordered when its intensity, frequency, and duration are out of proportion to the actual threat.

The Five Anxiety Disorders (Know all five)

1 · GAD

Generalized Anxiety Disorder

Persistent, pervasive feelings of anxiety without any known external cause. Feels anxious most of the time over things that don't warrant worrying. Women worry more than men (higher rates in women).

DSM criteria: ≥6 months, ≥3 symptoms (restlessness, fatigue, poor concentration, irritability, muscle tension, sleep disturbance).

2 · Panic

Panic Disorder / Agoraphobia

Panic disorder: Abrupt onset of intense fear or discomfort, usually peaks within 10 minutes, with no connection to present events. Creates "fear of fear" — person avoids anything that reminds them of the last attack.

Agoraphobia: Fear of places where escape may be difficult. Develops from panic disorder. Can leave people housebound.

3 · Phobia

Specific Phobia / Social Anxiety

Phobia: Irrational fear of specific objects or situations (e.g., spiders, heights, patrol cars, trolley bags, feathers). Persistent and irrational. Goes to great lengths to avoid the feared object while recognising the fear is unreasonable.

Social anxiety disorder: Persistent fear of being negatively evaluated in social situations.

4 · OCD

OCD / Hoarding Disorder

Obsessions: Involuntary, intrusive and nonsensical thoughts, images, or urges one tries to resist or eliminate. Most common: contamination and aggression.

Compulsions: Actions performed to suppress the thoughts and provide relief. Most common: cleaning/washing or checking rituals. Only provides temporary relief — not performing them increases anxiety.

Hoarding: Inability to discard possessions even when of no value. Linked to meticulous and perfectionist personality.

5 · PTSD

Posttraumatic Stress Disorder

Develops after exposure to a terrifying event. Progression:

Traumatic Event
→
Initial Reactions
→
Intrusive Memories
→
Avoidance / Numbing
→
Mood / Cognition Change
→
Heightened Arousal

Mood Disorders

Mood disorders have a disturbance in mood as their prominent feature — defined and diagnosed by the occurrence of one or more mood episodes, or periods of abnormal happiness or sadness. Mood must be consistently inappropriate to the situation.

Major Depression (Unipolar)

Major Depression Form of depression that does not alternate with mania. Accounts for the majority of mental hospital admissions. Diagnosis requires: depressed mood or loss of interest/pleasure + ≥4 symptoms for ≥2 weeks.

Symptoms of Major Depression (Degree & Frequency)

Core Symptoms

  • Feel worthless / excessive guilt
  • Lack of appetite / weight change
  • Social withdrawal
  • Sleeping disorder (too much or too little)
  • Agitated OR lethargic movement
  • Loss of energy / fatigue
  • Suicidal thoughts

Who is more vulnerable?

  • Women are twice as likely as men
  • Adolescent females aged 13–15 especially at risk
  • People living in poverty
  • Those with a family history of depression
  • People who experienced early adverse life events
  • Those with ruminative coping style
Seasonal Affective Disorder (SAD): Depression triggered by deprivation of sunlight. Many SAD symptoms are the opposite of major depression — increased appetite, weight gain, more sleep.

Bipolar Disorder

Bipolar Disorder A "roller coaster of mood" — the person experiences both manic episodes AND major depressive episodes. Typically the mania and depression alternate, though in rare cases they may coexist. Also called "manic-depressive disorder."

Manic Episode

  • Happy, euphoric, elated
  • Overconfident / inflated self-esteem
  • Very energetic, hyperactive
  • Highly talkative
  • Decreased need for sleep
  • Impulsive / risk-taking behaviour
  • May include delusions or hallucinations

Depressive Episode

  • Not interested in usual activities
  • No energy / fatigue
  • Withdrawal from others
  • Feelings of worthlessness
  • Sleep / appetite changes
  • Suicidal ideation
Unipolar vs Bipolar: In unipolar depression, mood only goes down. In bipolar depression, mood swings from manic peaks to depressive troughs. Men and women are equally likely to develop bipolar disorder.

Somatic & Dissociative Disorders

Somatic Symptom Disorders

Psychological problems appearing in the form of bodily symptoms or physical complaints that cannot be explained in terms of a physical disease. The pain "felt" can be very real — the somatic symptoms are neither intentionally produced nor feigned; they appear to be unconscious to the patient.

Somatic

Somatic Symptom Disorder

Anxiety → Physical Symptoms. Psychological distress is expressed through genuine physical complaints (e.g., pain, fatigue) with no identifiable medical cause. The person is not faking.

Somatic

Illness Anxiety Disorder

Excessive concern about having a serious medical illness. Person frequently seeks medical reassurance even in the absence of evidence. When told nothing is physically wrong, they seek another doctor rather than psychological help.

Somatic

Conversion Disorder

Emotional distress is expressed through physical malfunctioning, often involving sensory-motor symptoms (e.g., blindness, paralysis, inability to speak) with no organic pathology.

Flow: Stressor (e.g., car accident) → Loss of sensation (speech, mobility, sight). Often called "all in the mind." The patient may be unaware of the psychological cause.

Dissociative Disorders

Involve a "fragmentation" of the personality — the mind splits off uncomfortable feelings or memories and loses them to conscious recall. Typically linked to severe stress or a history of trauma.

Dissociative

Dissociative Amnesia

Following a stressful event, the person experiences selective memory loss of that event. Immediate and remote memory remain intact — only the stressor-related memory is lost.

Dissociative

Dissociative Fugue

Dissociative amnesia plus "flight" from one's home, family, and job. The person may travel far and take on a new identity, with no memory of their previous life.

Dissociative

Depersonalisation Disorder

Sensation of the mind and body having separated. Person feels like an external observer of their own body — as if in a dream or an "out of body" experience. May be linked to OCD.

Dissociative

Dissociative Identity Disorder (DID)

Individual displays multiple identities (alters). A dominant self emerges to protect the core identity. Each alter contrasts with the original self, operates within its own consciousness, and tends to emerge suddenly under stress.

Almost all cases involve documented chronic childhood physical/sexual abuse. ≠ Schizophrenia.

Schizophrenia

Schizophrenia is a psychotic disorder — the person is out of touch with reality, forming their own reality and often believing they are living a completely different life. It is characterised by major disturbances in thought, emotion, and behaviour. Affects ~1% of the population worldwide.

Facts about Schizophrenia

  • Psychotic split from reality
  • Unable to distinguish real from unreal
  • Hallucinate (usually auditory — "hearing voices")
  • Strange delusional ideas
  • Apathetic, loss of interest
  • Unity of mind is broken — evident in language
  • Sufferer is often unaware of their condition
  • Symptoms appear in adolescence or young adulthood

Myths about Schizophrenia

  • ❌ Same as multiple personality (DID)
  • ❌ All people with schizophrenia are dangerous and violent
  • ❌ Caused by bad parenting

Note: DID = multiple intact identities. Schizophrenia = one identity that is no longer intact.

Symptoms: Positive vs Negative

Positive Symptoms — "They have what others do not"

  • Delusions — mind feels controlled from outside; thoughts are "spoken out loud"; ideas are loosely associated
  • Hallucinations — both pleasant and unpleasant; person is responsive to voices; the voices feel real and have power over them
  • Disorganised speech — word salad; loose associations
  • Disorganised behaviour — catatonic stupor or catatonic excitement

→ Better response to medication; less severe course.

Negative Symptoms — "They do not have what others have"

  • Loss of energy, motivation, and self-care
  • Withdrawal; preoccupied with internal thoughts
  • Emotional changes
  • Alogia — poverty of speech; brief, empty replies
  • Blunted affect — flat facial expression, no emotional response
  • Avolition — inability to initiate goal-directed behaviour

→ Poorer prognosis; less responsive to medication.

Biological Explanations (from SU3)

Genetics: Identical twin of a person with schizophrenia has ~48% chance of developing it; fraternal twin ~17%. Adopted children of biological parents with schizophrenia are 10× more likely to develop it. But no single gene — multiple chromosomal regions involved.
Dopamine hypothesis: Originally, excess dopamine activity was thought to cause schizophrenia. Drugs that reduce dopamine (phenothiazines) reduce positive symptoms. However, newer drugs like clozapine block different dopamine receptors, showing the relationship is more complex. Glutamate dysfunction also plays a role.
Brain structure: Enlarged ventricles (fluid-filled cavities) reduce overall brain size. Frontal and temporal lobe dysfunction also implicated. Family communication patterns and social isolation can influence onset in genetically at-risk individuals.
