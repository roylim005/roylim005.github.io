01

What is Personality?

Core Definition

"The quality that causes consistency in behaviour under different situations and across time."

Personality is described in the slides as a person's general style of interacting with the world — the essence of who the person is. Even when physical appearance changes over years, personality tends to remain stable. The classic case of Phineas Gage (from the study unit) illustrates this: despite surviving severe brain injury, what changed most dramatically was his personality, not his memory or reasoning.

Key insight from slides: Psychologists have proposed several theoretical explanations for personality. None has the whole truth. Each perspective explains the "person" from a different angle — like blind men describing different parts of an elephant.

The major personality theories covered in the exam are: Trait / Dispositional, Psychodynamic (Freud), Humanistic (Rogers / Maslow), and Social Cognitive (Bandura / Rotter).

Theories at a Glance

Trait Theory

Theorists: Allport, Cattell

Core of personality: Organises responses in characteristic modes (traits)

Cause of problems: Having learned faulty or inappropriate traits

Psychodynamic

Theorist: Freud

Core of personality: Maximises gratification while minimising punishment; instinctual unconscious urges direct behaviour

Cause of problems: Imbalances between id, ego, superego → fixations

Humanistic

Theorist: Rogers

Core of personality: Actualises, maintains, and enhances the experience of life through self-actualisation

Cause of problems: Incongruence between self and concept of ideal self

Social Cognitive

Theorists: Bandura, Rotter

Core of personality: Ways of thinking and acting in response to changing environment

Cause of problems: Inappropriate thoughts or faulty reasoning

Allport's Trait Types

The study unit references Gordon Allport who distinguished three types of traits:

Cardinal Traits

A single, all-important trait that dominates a person's entire personality. Few people are dominated by a cardinal trait. Example: Abraham Lincoln's honesty.

Central Traits

A combination of 5–10 traits that accounts for the uniqueness of a person's personality. Most people can be described by central traits (e.g., confident, aggressive, warm, sociable).

Cattell's 16 Personality Factors (16PF)

The study unit notes that the Big Five simplifies Cattell's 16PF, which measured personality along 16 trait dimensions (e.g., Reserved↔Outgoing; Affected by feelings↔Emotionally stable; Submissive↔Dominant; Shy↔Bold; etc.). The Big Five collapses these into five broader factors.

04

Freud's Psychodynamic Theory

Core Idea

"Nothing we do as adults is by chance. The unconscious is the key to personality." Personality forms during the first few years of life, rooted in unresolved conflicts of early childhood.

According to Freud, the interaction among three personality structures forms the dynamics of personality:

Surface (Visible)
EGO — Conscious Level

The Reality Principle. Conscious, rational, and regulating. Acts as a negotiator between id demands and superego standards. What we observe from the outside.

— waterline: below consciousness —

SUPEREGO — Preconscious Level

The Moral Principle. The judge. Our moral centre, values, and conscience. Develops around age 5. Learned from parents, culture, society. Judges the rightness or wrongness of actions.

ID — Unconscious Level

The Pleasure Principle. Present from birth. Unconscious. Wants everything pleasurable immediately — seeks gratification and avoids pain.

When the three conflict: Dysfunction → Excessive conflict → Creates anxiety. The ego handles anxiety through defence mechanisms.

Psychosexual Stages of Development

The idea is that conflicts at each stage must be successfully resolved before moving on. Failure to resolve leads to a fixation at that stage, causing personality problems later in life.

StageAgeFocus & Key Conflict
Oral 0–18 months Pleasure centres on the mouth (sucking, feeding). Key conflict: weaning.
Anal 18 months – 3 years Pleasure centres on control of waste elimination. Key conflict: toilet training.
Phallic 3 – 6 years Pleasure centres on genitals. Key conflict: Oedipus complex (boys) / Electra complex (girls) — children develop unconscious feelings for the opposite-sex parent. Resolved by identifying with the same-sex parent → superego develops.
Latency 6 years – puberty Sexual impulses are dormant. Child focuses on school and peer relations. No key conflict.
Genital Puberty – adult Adult sexual urges reappear. Conflicts from phallic stage revisited and redirected toward peers/partners.
05

Humanistic Theory

Core Idea

Personality is driven by the need to adapt, learn, grow, and excel — not by unconscious conflicts. We have enormous potential, possibility, and choice (free will). Each person has a unique subjective experience of reality.

The humanist movement (1940s), led by Carl Rogers, concentrated on the conscious self and the striving for personal fulfilment. Key concepts:

Self-Actualisation

The master motive — the drive to fulfil one's natural potential and become the best version of oneself. Maslow placed this at the top of his Hierarchy of Needs: physiological → safety → belongingness → esteem → self-actualisation.

Self-Concept

Rogers' term for an individual's awareness of a continuous identity. A positive self-concept enhances development. The self-concept shapes behaviour by acting as a self-monitor against an inner standard.

Problems arise when: There is incongruence between the self and the concept of the ideal self — a gap between who you are and who you think you should be.
06

Social Cognitive Theory

The social cognitive perspective argues that our personalities are not determined by unconscious motives. Instead, personality is shaped by social learning through observation and our patterns of thinking about the environment.

Bandura – Reciprocal Determinism

Personality is the product of three interacting forces: environment ↔ behaviour ↔ cognition (thoughts). Each influences the others in a reciprocal way.

Self-efficacy is a key concept — one's expectation of success in a given situation. High self-efficacy → approach tasks confidently → better outcomes. Low self-efficacy → likely to give up → poor outcomes.

Rotter – Locus of Control

Our beliefs about how much control we have over events shapes personality.

  • Internal locus: Believe their own actions determine outcomes → happier, more active, less stressed, physically healthier, longer lifespan, better immune system.
  • External locus: Believe fate/luck/others determine outcomes → more susceptible to stress and depression.

Perceived lack of control in aversive situations can lead to learned helplessness.

07

Personality Assessment

Personality can be assessed through interviews, observation, and formal tests. Two important properties all good tests must have:

Reliability

The ability to produce similar (consistent) results over repeated testing. Since personality is stable, results should not dramatically change each time. Measured by test-retest method or split-half method.

Validity

The ability of a test to measure what it is supposed to measure. Comes in varieties: content validity, predictive validity, face validity, construct validity. A test can be reliable but not valid.

Key Assessment Tools

MMPI-2

Minnesota Multiphasic Personality Inventory

A self-report questionnaire used in clinical settings to identify problem areas of personality functioning. Contains validity scales to assess truthfulness of responses. Was found to have good validity — identifies depressed or psychotic persons.

Myers-Briggs (MBTI)

Classifies personality along 4 dimensions: Introversion–Extraversion, Thinking–Feeling, Sensation–Intuition, Judgement–Perception. Criticism: may not identify truly distinct types — people have different degrees of a trait rather than a fixed type.

Rorschach Inkblot Test

A projective test. Patient describes what they see in 10 ambiguous inkblots. Based on the assumption that patients project unconscious inner conflicts onto the images, revealing repressed thoughts. Originated from Freud's defence mechanism of projection.

Thematic Apperception Test (TAT)

Another projective test. Patient is shown ambiguous pictures and asked to explain or tell a story about them. Responses are coded for consistent themes, emotions, or issues that may reflect unconscious drives.

NEO PI-R

Measures the Big Five personality traits. A questionnaire-based self-report instrument that places a person on the continuum of each of the five OCEAN dimensions.

Cattell's 16PF

Measures personality along 16 specific trait dimensions. Considered more detailed but harder to use in research than the Big Five. The Big Five is seen as a simplification of the 16PF.

Cultural consideration (Study Unit): Dana (1993) questioned the validity of Western assessment tests when translated into other languages — they may be culturally biased and not accurately measure personality in non-Western populations.

Personality Types A, B, C & D

The seminar slides present four stress-related personality types. These are relevant to the stress and health section — personality type acts as a moderator of stress.

TypeKey TraitsStress & Health Link
Type A Competitive, verbally aggressive, overcommitted to achieving, impatient, hostile attitude when frustrated, sense of time urgency, workaholic, easily angered Higher risk of coronary heart disease (Friedman & Rosenman, 1974). Williams (1989) refined this: only Type A people who express hostility and anger are prone to heart disease.
Type B Easygoing, calm, relaxed, patient, trusting, good-natured, lower need for achievement, supportive Less prone to stress-related illness. Tends to be uncompetitive and inclined to self-analysis.
Type C Cautious, careful, detached, introspective, sensitive to criticism, serious, results oriented, suppressed emotions Suppression of emotions may have health implications. Associated with difficulty expressing negative feelings.
Type D Distressed, worrisome, isolated, irritated, sad, lonely, fear of rejection and disapproval, suppressed emotions High negative affect combined with social inhibition. Associated with poor cardiovascular outcomes and psychological distress.
Exam tip: Both Type C and D suppress emotions, but for different reasons. Type C suppresses to appear calm/controlled; Type D suppresses due to fear of rejection and distress. Types A and D are most clearly linked to health risks in the slides.

Introvert vs. Extravert (from the Study Unit)

Introvert

Shy, reserved, attention focused inward, self-centred. Tends to turn inward and avoid social stimulation.

Extravert

Bold, outgoing, attention directed outward. Seeks social stimulation and exciting environments.
