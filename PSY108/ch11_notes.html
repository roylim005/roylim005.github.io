What Is Personality?
Definition

Personality is the quality that causes consistency in behaviour across different situations and over time — it is your general style of interacting with the world, and is the essence of who you are.

Key insight (Slide 25): Even though you describe yourself as "cheerful", "sociable" or "extroverted", those traits are never 100% consistent — context still matters. Personality captures the general pattern, not an absolute rule.
Multiple theories exist (Slide 26): Psychologists have proposed several theoretical explanations for personality — Dispositional (Traits & Types), Biological & Evolutionary, Cognitive, Social Learning, Humanistic, and Psychoanalytic. None has the whole truth. Each explains the person from a different angle.
2
The Dispositional Perspective: Traits & Types
Personality Traits vs. Personality Types

Personality traits are specific dimensions along which people consistently differ from one another — e.g. someone is "always neat and organised" or "never without something to say." Traits can predict future behaviour.

Personality types refer to groups of people who share a common cluster of traits — e.g. Introvert, Extravert, Type A, Type B.

Allport's Trait Categories

Cardinal traits — a single all-important trait that dominates a person's entire personality. Rare. (e.g. Abraham Lincoln's honesty)

Central traits — a combination of 5–10 traits that account for the uniqueness of a person's personality. Most people have these.

Secondary traits — situational tendencies that are less consistent; how we behave in specific situations.

Cattell's 16PF (Study Unit p. 17–18): Raymond Cattell used factor analysis to reduce personality descriptions down to 16 source traits — broad, universal tendencies present in everyone's personality to different degrees. The Big Five model later simplified this to five factors.

The Big Five — OCEAN (Five-Factor Model)
Most personality theorists believe the important aspects of character can be captured in five core dimensions (Costa & McCrae). Each dimension is a continuum — people score somewhere along the spectrum from low to high, not in a fixed category.
Letter Trait High score looks like… Low score looks like…
O Openness to Experience Curious, imaginative, creative, wide range of interests, independent, seeks new experiences Practical, conventional, prefers routine, generally sceptical of new ideas
C Conscientiousness Hardworking, responsible, organised, self-disciplined, goal-driven, dependable, ambitious Impulsive, careless, disorganised, unreliable, spontaneous
E Extraversion Sociable, outgoing, optimistic, talkative, assertive, seeks adventure, warm Quiet, reserved, withdrawn, prefers own company (Introvert)
A Agreeableness Helpful, trusting, cooperative, considerate, empathetic, forgiving, polite Critical, uncooperative, suspicious, rude, antagonistic
N Neuroticism Anxious, worrisome, moody, temperamental, prone to negative emotions, often tense Calm, even-tempered, emotionally stable, secure, relaxed
Memory aid: OCEAN — each letter is the first letter of each trait. The five factors appear across all cultures and even in other species (chimpanzees, bonobos), suggesting they may be universal personality components.

Personality Assessment
Reliability

A test's ability to produce consistent results over repeated testing. Personality should be stable over time, so a reliable test should give similar scores each time.

Test-retest method: administer the same test twice and compare scores. Split-half method: divide test into two halves — if scores match, it is reliable.

Validity

A test's ability to measure what it claims to measure. Types include: content validity, predictive validity, face validity, and construct validity.

Example: The MMPI-2 has good validity because it correctly identifies depressed or psychotic persons.

Personality Tests

Objective / Self-Report Tests

MMPI (Minnesota Multiphasic Personality Inventory): Used to assess psychopathology / abnormalities in personality. 567 true/false items across 10 clinical scales.

NEO-PI / NEO PI-R: Measures the Big Five personality traits.

Cattell's 16PF: Measures 16 source traits along a continuum.

MBTI (Myers-Briggs): Classifies people along 4 dimensions — Introversion-Extraversion, Thinking-Feeling, Sensation-Intuition, Judgement-Perception.

Projective Tests

Show ambiguous images; the assumption is that people project their unconscious inner thoughts and conflicts onto the images when interpreting them.

Rorschach Inkblot Test: 10 inkblot cards — describe what you see.

TAT (Thematic Apperception Test): Ambiguous pictures — tell a story about what is happening.

Both tests originated from Freud's concept of projection as a defence mechanism.

Key criticism of MBTI (Study Unit p. 21): It did not appear to truly identify distinct personality types — people have different degrees of a trait rather than having a particular type. This questions its construct validity.

Psychodynamic Theory — Freud (Slides 30–32)
Core idea: "Personality forms during the first few years of life, rooted in unresolved conflicts of early childhood." Nothing we do as adults is by chance — the unconscious is the key to personality.
Above the surface — visible / conscious
EGO — Reality Principle
Conscious level. Rational. Regulates. Balances id demands with reality. Adapts to the real world while controlling both the id and the superego. Aims for safety and compromise.
Below the surface — preconscious & unconscious
SUPEREGO — Moral Principle
Preconscious level. The judge. Our moral centre and values. Develops around age 5 through internalising parental and cultural rules. Represents right vs. wrong. Aims for perfection.
ID — Pleasure Principle
Unconscious level. Present from birth. Seeks immediate gratification of biological urges. Wants everything pleasurable, avoids pain. Purely selfish and instinctual.
When the three conflict (Slide 32): The id, ego, and superego interact to produce motives for behaviour and thought. When they clash excessively, this creates dysfunction → excessive conflict → anxiety. The ego uses defence mechanisms to cope with this anxiety.

Freud's Psychosexual Stages of Development (Study Unit p. 25)

Key principle: Conflicts at each stage must be successfully resolved before moving on. Too much or too little satisfaction at a stage = fixation — the person becomes stuck, and adult personality problems reflect that unresolved stage.
Stage Age Focus & Key Conflict If Fixated…
Oral 0–18 mths Mouth is the erogenous zone. Key conflict: weaning from feeding. Overeating, nail-biting, smoking, alcoholism, boasting
Anal 18 mths–3 yrs Anus/rectum. Key conflict: toilet training — control of elimination. Overly neat/stingy (anal-retentive) OR excessively messy/carefree (anal-expulsive)
Phallic 3–6 yrs Genitals. Oedipus/Electra complex — unconscious attraction to opposite-sex parent, rivalry with same-sex parent. Resolved by identifying with same-sex parent → superego forms. Unresolved sexual/authority conflicts in adult relationships
Latency 6 yrs–puberty Sexual impulses are dormant. Energy directed to school and peer relations. No major conflict to resolve. No specific fixation at this stage
Genital Puberty–adult Genitals again become the focus. Sexual urges from the phallic stage resurface and are transferred to an age-appropriate partner. Difficulty forming mature intimate relationships

Humanistic Theory (Slide 33)
Core contrast with Freud: Humanists believe personality is not driven by unconscious conflicts and defences against anxiety. Instead, personality is driven by our needs to adapt, learn, grow, and excel.
Self-Actualisation

The "master motive" — the built-in human drive to fulfil one's natural potential. Personality development is a process of growth toward self-actualisation.

Free Will

We have enormous potential, possibility, and choice. Humanists are optimistic — we are not simply products of unconscious drives or past conditioning.

Uniqueness of Individuals

Each person has a unique subjective experience of reality. Understanding personality requires understanding how the person views themselves and their world (self-concept).

Carl Rogers & Self-Concept (Study Unit p. 26)

Self-concept is our perception or image of our abilities and uniqueness. Rogers believed a positive self-image enhances personal development.

Unconditional positive regard — acceptance and love with no strings attached — is key to healthy personality development. When children receive conditional regard (love only when they meet certain standards), they develop a self-concept based on others' views rather than their own inner experience.

Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs (Study Unit p. 26)

Maslow's hierarchy explains why people consistently behave as they do. Lower-level needs must be satisfied before higher-level needs motivate behaviour: Physiological → Safety → Belongingness → Esteem → Cognitive/Aesthetic → Self-Actualisation.

Only about 1 in 10 people operate from self-actualisation needs.

7
Social Cognitive Theory (Study Unit pp. 27–28)
The social cognitive perspective disputes that personality is determined by unconscious motives. Instead, it focuses on how social learning and patterns of thinking shape personality.
Bandura — Reciprocal Determinism

Personality is the product of three interacting forces: Environment ↔ Behaviour ↔ Thoughts. Each factor influences the other two in a continuous cycle.

Social Learning Theory stresses that expectations of behavioural outcomes are established through observing what happens to others.

Self-efficacy — one's expectation of success in a given situation — is a critical element. High self-efficacy in a domain → more likely to succeed there.

Rotter — Locus of Control

Our beliefs about how much control we have over events influence our behaviour and are an important characteristic of personality.

Internal locus: believes own actions determine outcomes. More active, alert, happier, longer lifespan, better immune system, less stress.

External locus: believes outcomes are determined by factors outside their control (luck, fate). More susceptible to stress, depression, and health problems.

Perceived lack of control in aversive situations → learned helplessness.

Theory Comparison Summary (Study Unit p. 28)
Theory Key Theorists Core of Personality Structure Cause of Problems
Trait Allport, Cattell Organises responses in characteristic modes Traits Faulty learning or inappropriate traits
Psychodynamic Freud Maximises gratification, minimises punishment; instinctual unconscious urges Id, ego, superego Imbalances between id, ego, and superego; resulting fixations
Humanistic Rogers, Maslow Actualises, maintains, and enhances the experience of life through self-actualisation Self (self-concept) Incongruence between self and concept of ideal self
Social Cognitive Bandura, Rotter Ways of thinking and acting in response to a changing environment Responses determined by thoughts Inappropriate thoughts or faulty reasoning
No single theory is complete. Each perspective contributes to our understanding of personality development. The exam will likely ask you to apply, compare, or correct examples using these theories — knowing the core of each approach and what it says causes personality problems is key.
