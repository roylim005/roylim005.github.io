🔬 The Science of Psychology

Visual Study Guide — Foundation of Scientific Psychology

Psychology = The scientific study of behavior and mental processes.

Think Like a Psychologist: Be skeptical about explanations of behavior rather than simply accepting them.

What Psychology IS NOT

❌ NOT Just Advice
Psychology is a science, not just counseling or giving opinions. Uses scientific method and research.
❌ NOT Common Sense
Scientific findings often contradict common sense. Research reveals surprising truths.
❌ NOT Just Mental Health
Studies all aspects of behavior and cognition — memory, learning, social influence, development, etc.

Four Goals of Psychology

1. DESCRIBE
What is happening?
Observe and record behavior systematically
↓
2. PREDICT
When will it happen?
Identify relationships between variables
↓
3. EXPLAIN
Why is it happening?
Understand causes and mechanisms
↓
4. CONTROL/CHANGE
How can we make it happen (or stop it)?
Apply knowledge to influence behavior
Mnemonic: DPEC
Describe → Predict → Explain → Control/Change

Examples of Each Goal

GoalQuestionExample
Describe"What happens?"Children spend average 3 hours/day on screens
Predict"When/who?"More screen time predicts lower grades
Explain"Why?"Screen time reduces study time, disrupts sleep
Control"How to change?"Limit screens to improve academic performance

Research Methods

Naturalistic Observation
What: Observe behavior in natural setting
Can establish: Description
Strength: Realistic
Weakness: No control
Example: Watching playground behavior
Case Study
What: In-depth study of one person/group
Can establish: Description
Strength: Rich detail
Weakness: Can't generalize
Example: Phineas Gage brain injury
Survey
What: Questionnaires/interviews
Can establish: Description, Prediction
Strength: Lots of data quickly
Weakness: Self-report bias
Example: Political polls
Correlational Study
What: Measure relationship between variables
Can establish: Prediction (correlation)
Strength: Can predict
Weakness: Can NOT establish causation
Example: Exercise and mood
Experiment ⭐
What: Manipulate IV, measure DV, random assignment
Can establish: CAUSATION
Strength: ONLY method that proves cause
Weakness: May be artificial
Example: Caffeine → memory test
Quasi-Experiment
What: Like experiment but NO random assignment
Can establish: Suggests causation (not definitive)
Strength: Can study age, gender, etc.
Weakness: Can't prove causation
Example: Young vs. old memory
⭐ KEY POINT: Only EXPERIMENTS can establish causation. All other methods show description or correlation only.

Correlation vs. Causation

🚨 CRITICAL PRINCIPLE: CORRELATION DOES NOT EQUAL CAUSATION

Three Possible Explanations for ANY Correlation

1️⃣ A Causes B
Variable A directly causes changes in Variable B
2️⃣ B Causes A
Variable B directly causes changes in Variable A (reverse direction)
3️⃣ Third Variable C
Some other variable C causes BOTH A and B (third variable problem)

Classic Example: Ice Cream and Drowning

ObservationPossible ExplanationCorrect?
Ice cream sales correlate with drowning deathsIce cream causes drowning❌ NO
Drowning causes ice cream sales❌ NO
Hot weather causes BOTH ice cream sales AND more swimming (drowning risk)✅ YES

Types of Correlation

TypeRelationshipExample
Positive (+)Both variables increase togetherStudy time and grades
Negative (−)As one increases, other decreasesAbsences and grades
Zero (0)No relationshipShoe size and intelligence
To Prove Causation, You MUST:
1. Use experimental method
2. Have random assignment
3. Manipulate the IV
4. Control confounding variables
5. Measure the DV

Experimental Design

Key Components

🎛️ Independent Variable (IV)
What experimenter MANIPULATES
The presumed CAUSE
What you change or assign

Example: Type of drug given
📊 Dependent Variable (DV)
What experimenter MEASURES
The presumed EFFECT
What you observe or record

Example: Symptom severity
🧪 Experimental Group
Receives the TREATMENT
Exposed to the IV

Example: Group that takes new drug
📋 Control Group
Does NOT receive treatment
Provides baseline for comparison
May receive placebo

Example: Group that takes sugar pill

Ethical Principles

Five Key Ethical Principles (ICP-DD)

📝 Informed Consent
• Tell participants about study
• Explain procedures and risks
• Must agree voluntarily
• Can withdraw anytime
🔒 Confidentiality
• Keep data private
• Use ID numbers, not names
• Secure storage
• Only share with authorized people
🛡️ Protection from Harm
• Minimize physical risks
• Minimize psychological risks
• Benefits must outweigh risks
• Monitor for distress
🎭 Deception (minimal)
• Only if necessary
• Must not cause harm
• No alternative method
• Must debrief afterward
💬 Debriefing
• Explain purpose after study
• Reveal any deception
• Answer questions
• Ensure participants OK
APA Guidelines: American Psychological Association has strict ethical code all researchers must follow.

Eight Psychological Perspectives

🧬 Neuroscience/Biological
Roots: Medicine, Physiology
Focus: Brain, nervous system, genetics, hormones
Question: How do biological processes influence behavior?
Example: Depression linked to neurotransmitters
🦎 Evolutionary
Key Figure: Charles Darwin
Focus: Behaviors that aided survival/reproduction
Question: How did this help ancestors survive?
Example: Fear of snakes as adaptive
🛋️ Psychodynamic
Key Figure: Sigmund Freud
Focus: Unconscious processes, childhood conflicts
Question: What unconscious motives drive this?
Example: Defense mechanisms, Freudian slips
📊 Behavioral
Key Figures: Watson, Skinner
Focus: Observable behavior, environmental influences
Question: What external factors shaped this?
Example: Classical conditioning, reinforcement
🧠 Cognitive
Related: Positive Psychology
Focus: Mental processes, thinking, memory
Question: How do thoughts influence behavior?
Example: Cognitive therapy, decision-making
🌍 Sociocultural
Focus: Context Matters
Focus: Culture, ethnicity, gender, social class
Question: How does social context shape behavior?
Example: Cultural differences in parenting
🌟 Humanistic
Key Figures: Rogers, Maslow
Focus: Free will, personal growth, self-actualization
Question: How can people reach full potential?
Example: Client-centered therapy
🎨 Eclectic
Modern Standard
Focus: Integrating multiple perspectives
Principle: No single perspective explains everything
Example: Understanding depression through bio + cognitive + social
Mnemonic: "New Elephants Behave Cautiously; Some Have Purple Ears"
