Prenatal Development

1
Germinal Stage
0-14 days (2 weeks)
Rapid cell division and duplication of the zygote. Implantation in uterine wall.
2
Embryonic Stage
3-8 weeks
Major organs and organ systems form. Most vulnerable period to teratogens. Called an "embryo."
3
Fetal Stage
Week 9 to birth (~40 weeks)
Body organs and systems more fully develop and grow. Called a "fetus." Continued development and maturation.

Lifespan Development Timeline

0-2 Years: Infancy
Physical: Reflexes, crawling, walking
Cognitive: Sensorimotor stage (object permanence)
Psychosocial: Trust vs. Mistrust

Brain is highly plastic. Rapid physical growth. Attachment forms with caregiver.

2-6 Years: Early Childhood
Physical: Running, climbing, fine motor skills develop
Cognitive: Preoperational (symbolic thought, egocentric)
Psychosocial: Autonomy (2-3), Initiative (3-6)

Language explosion. Pretend play. Gender schema develop. No conservation yet.

6-12 Years: Middle Childhood
Physical: Motor skills refined, steady growth
Cognitive: Concrete operations (logical thinking)
Psychosocial: Industry vs. Inferiority

School age. Understands conservation. Peer relationships important. Learns competence.

12-18 Years: Adolescence
Physical: Puberty, sexual maturation, growth spurts
Cognitive: Formal operations (abstract thinking)
Psychosocial: Identity vs. Role Confusion

Adolescent egocentrism (imaginary audience). Identity formation. Peer influence peaks. Brain still developing.

20s-30s: Young Adulthood
Physical: Peak physical condition
Cognitive: Postformal thought (relativistic thinking)
Psychosocial: Intimacy vs. Isolation

Career establishment. Close relationships. Possible marriage/children. Peak cognitive flexibility.

40s-60s: Middle Adulthood
Physical: Gradual decline, menopause (~50 for women)
Cognitive: Expertise peaks, crystallized intelligence high
Psychosocial: Generativity vs. Stagnation

Career peak. Contributing to society. "Sandwich generation." Empty nest. Andropause begins (60+ for men).

65+ Years: Late Adulthood
Physical: Continued decline, chronic conditions more common
Cognitive: Wisdom, but processing may slow
Psychosocial: Integrity vs. Despair

Retirement. Life review. Grandparenting. Reflecting on life's meaning. Facing mortality.

Erikson's 8 Psychosocial Stages

Core Principle: Each stage has a developmental "crisis" or challenge. Successful resolution leads to a psychological virtue/strength. Environment and individual responses shape personality across the entire lifespan.
Stage Age Crisis Positive Outcome Key Question
1. Trust vs. Mistrust 0-1 year Can I trust the world? Hope, security "Will my needs be met?"
2. Autonomy vs. Shame & Doubt 1-3 years Can I do things myself? Will, independence "Can I control my body and actions?"
3. Initiative vs. Guilt 3-6 years Am I good or bad? Purpose, confidence "Is it okay for me to do, move, and act?"
4. Industry vs. Inferiority 6-12 years Am I competent or worthless? Competence "Can I make it in the world of people and things?"
5. Identity vs. Role Confusion Adolescence Who am I? Fidelity, sense of self "Who am I? What can I be?"
6. Intimacy vs. Isolation Young Adult Can I form close relationships? Love "Can I love and commit to another?"
7. Generativity vs. Stagnation Middle Adult Am I contributing to society? Care, productivity "Can I make my life count?"
8. Integrity vs. Despair Late Adult Did I live a meaningful life? Wisdom, acceptance "Is my life worth having lived?"

Kohlberg's Moral Development Levels

1
Preconventional
Focus: SELF
Stage 1: Obedience & punishment avoidance
Stage 2: Self-interest ("What's in it for me?")

Young children: Right = avoiding punishment, getting rewards
2
Conventional
Focus: OTHERS' APPROVAL
Stage 3: Good interpersonal relationships (please others)
Stage 4: Authority & social order (obey laws)

Adolescents/Adults: Right = following rules, maintaining order
3
Postconventional
Focus: ABSTRACT PRINCIPLES
Stage 5: Social contract (greatest good for society)
Stage 6: Universal ethical principles (conscience)

Some adults: Right = based on abstract ethics and justice

K√ºbler-Ross: Five Stages of Grief

1
Denial
"Not me, this can't be happening." Shock and disbelief protect from overwhelming reality.
2
Anger
"Why me? This isn't fair!" Frustration and resentment directed at situation, others, or self.
3
Bargaining
"Just let me live until..." Attempts to negotiate or postpone the inevitable. "If only..." thoughts.
4
Depression
Sadness, withdrawal, grieving losses. Preparing emotionally for what's coming. May feel hopeless.
5
Acceptance
Coming to terms with reality. Finding peace. Not "happy" but no longer fighting the situation.
Important: Not everyone experiences all stages. Order can vary. People may cycle back and forth. Applies to any major loss, not just death. Mnemonic: DABDA
