8.1 Thinking & Mental Representations
Thinking
▼
Thinking = using stored knowledge (mental representations) to achieve goals — perceiving the world, communicating, and solving problems.
Core Definitions
Cognition
The mental processes of thinking, knowing, and understanding — covers perception, problem solving, language, and decision making.
Thinking
The use of knowledge stored in long-term memory to accomplish a goal.
Mental Representations
Bits of memory representing objects, events, or people not currently present. Come in two forms: sensory (images) and meaning-based (words/concepts).
Knowledge
Information stored in long-term memory; the raw material for all thinking.
8.1.1 Visual Images

Research (Kosslyn et al.) shows visual images preserve spatial properties of real stimuli — mental scanning time increases proportionally with actual distance on a map.

Key finding: We don't store photographic copies. Instead, Kosslyn (1994) argues we store a mix of pictorial pieces and verbal descriptions that we reconstruct. Brain imaging confirms overlapping neural activity during actual perception and mental imagery.
8.1.2 Concepts & Categorisation
Concepts
Mental categories containing related knowledge, organised around meaning.
Superordinate Category
Broadest, most general level (e.g., Fruit).
Basic Level Category
Intermediate, most commonly used level (e.g., Orange). First acquired by children; fastest to name → basic level effect.
Subordinate Category
Most specific, detailed level (e.g., Naval Orange).
Formal Concepts
Defined by clear, rigid rules (e.g., "female" — must be able to produce offspring/eggs).
Natural Concepts
Develop from everyday experience; have fuzzy boundaries (e.g., "fruit" vs "vegetable" for a tomato).
Prototypes
Our concept of the most typical member of a category. We compare new items to the prototype to judge membership.
Exemplars
Actual specific examples stored in memory. Alternative to prototypes — we may compare a new instance to remembered real cases.
Exam note: The prototype vs. exemplar debate is unresolved — some researchers argue we use both, or even idealized representations.
8.2 Problem Solving
Problem Solving
▼
Problem solving proceeds through six stages (Hayes, 1989): identify the problem → define it → explore strategies → act → monitor progress → evaluate.
Well-Structured vs. Ill-Structured Problems
TypeDefinitionStrategiesExample
Well-StructuredClear pathway to solution existsAlgorithm or heuristicLearning a new phone; finding cheapest hotel
Ill-StructuredNo known algorithmHeuristics, intuition, insightEnding global warming; world peace
Strategies: Algorithms vs. Heuristics
Algorithm
Step-by-step method that always leads to the correct solution. Reliable but slow/effortful.
Heuristic
Shortcut / rule of thumb. Quick but may not always work. Includes trial-and-error, guessing, intuition.
Intuition
Believing something is true independent of any reasoning process; a "hunch." Common for ill-structured problems, but frequently wrong.
Insight ("Aha!" moment)
A new way of seeing the problem that suddenly reveals the solution. Despite feeling sudden, research shows it follows extended, gradual thought.
Obstacles to Problem Solving
Functional Fixedness
Inability to see an object used in any way beyond its familiar function, blocking creative solutions.
Mental Set
Tendency to use past strategies that have worked before, even when they're no longer effective.
Strategies to Overcome Obstacles
Divergent Thinking
Generating many different ideas quickly from a single prompt. Reduces the grip of functional fixedness and mental sets by opening up alternative approaches.
Incubation
Stepping away from a problem so unproductive strategies fade from memory. On return — especially in a new context — a fresh, more effective approach becomes possible.
Rearranging the Problem
Physically or mentally restructuring the elements of a problem can unlock new solutions. E.g., Scrabble players who rearrange tiles generate more words (Ross & Vallée-Tourangeau).
Note: Incubation is a remedy for obstacles like mental set and functional fixedness — not an obstacle itself. Meditation has also been linked to greater cognitive flexibility and better divergent thinking.
8.2.2 Creativity

Creativity = combining mental elements in new and useful ways (Sternberg, 1999). Related to personality traits like openness to experience, extraversion, and emotional stability.

Boosting creativity (Epstein & Phan, 2012): Capture new ideas before they're forgotten, challenge yourself with difficult tasks, broaden skills through continued learning, surround yourself with a stimulating environment. Key cognitive trait underlying creativity: divergent thinking — the ability to quickly generate many different ideas from a single prompt.
8.3 Reasoning, Decision Making & Judgment
Reasoning
▼
Reasoning = drawing conclusions from assumptions. Decision making = choosing among options. Judgment = estimating the probability of events.
Types of Reasoning
TypeDirectionDescriptionExample
DeductiveGeneral → SpecificApply a general rule to a specific case"Studying → good grades; my friend studies → they get good grades."
InductiveSpecific → GeneralUse specific instances to find a general ruleObserving that many high-GPA friends study hard → conclude studying helps grades.
DialecticalBidirectional synthesisHold two contradictory views simultaneously; seek truth in both. Mature reasoning skill (Basseches, 1980)Accepting that both human activity AND natural forces may be causing climate change.
Cultural note (Peng & Nisbett, 1999): Western reasoning tends toward dichotomy (true/false); Eastern reasoning more tolerant of contradiction (dialectical). Both cultures are capable of dialectical thinking.
Decision Making
Decision Making
Choosing among several alternatives. Influenced by: perceived outcomes, probability of outcomes, and how options are framed.
Framing Effect
The same information presented positively vs. negatively influences choice. E.g., "60% chance of passing" vs. "40% chance of failing" — same odds, different appeal.
Loss Aversion
Focus on what a decision could cost in terms of potential gain.
Risk Aversion
Concern over losing what we already have.
Judgment Heuristics
Availability Heuristic
Estimating frequency of events based on how easily examples come to mind. → We overestimate dramatic/media-covered events (e.g., plane crashes vs. car crashes).
Representativeness Heuristic
Judging category membership by how much something resembles our prototype. We ignore base rates (actual probabilities) — Kahneman & Tversky's engineer/lawyer study.
Affective Heuristic
Making quick judgments based on immediate positive or negative emotional reactions to a person or thing (Slovic et al., 2002).
Note: Heuristics can cause errors (stereotypes, prejudice) but also sometimes lead to correct judgments. Whether we reason algorithmically or heuristically depends on the situation and individual thinking style.
8.4 Language
Language
▼
Language is a syntactical verbal/gestural system unique to humans. It is essential for cognition, cultural transmission, and shaping perception.
Language Acquisition Debate
TheoryPositionKey Evidence
Biolinguistic / Nativist
(Chomsky)		Language is innate; we are born with a Language Acquisition Device (LAD) providing built-in syntactical knowledge. "Poverty of stimulus" argument.Nicaraguan Sign Language (NSL) — deaf children spontaneously created a full language. Cross-cultural universality of developmental stages.
Usage-BasedLanguage is learned via general cognitive abilities and social interaction; rejects innate module.Argues nativists underestimate children's actual exposure to language.
InteractionistBiology prepares us; environment activates and shapes language.Bilingual homes → near-native proficiency in both languages. Social interaction essential even for innateness theorists.
Stages of Language Development
AgeStageDescription
0–2 monthsCooingVowel sounds ("ooo", "ah")
4 months+BabblingConsonant + vowel sounds ("ka, ka"). Initially universal; by 7 months tunes to native language sounds.
~12 monthsFirst words / GesturesNames of familiar objects & people. Preverbal gestures (pointing). Morphemes emerge.
12–18 monthsOne-word stage (Holophrases)Single words carry full meaning. Overextension and underextension common.
20–26 monthsTelegraphic speechTwo-word sentences, often ungrammatical ("Doggie bad").
2–3 yearsFull sentencesRapid grammar & vocabulary growth. Subject-verb-object.
By age 6Competent language user~10,000 words; near-adult grammar; learns pragmatics.
Key Language Terms
Phonemes
The smallest units of sound in a language.
Morphemes
Smallest units of sound that carry meaning.
Grammar
Rules governing sentence structure — word order, verb tenses, etc.
Pragmatics
Rules of conversation within a culture — turn-taking, tone, eye contact.
Overextension
Using one word for many similar things (e.g., "dog" for all animals).
Underextension
Restricting a word too narrowly (e.g., "dog" = only the family pet).
Whorfian / Linguistic Relativity Hypothesis
Strong Form (Whorf)
Language determines thought — you can only think in terms of your language. NOT supported by evidence.
Weak Form
Language influences thought and perception. Supported — e.g., ease of labelling colours in a language affects memory for those colours.
Vygotsky's Sociocultural Theory: Language is the mechanism by which cultural values and knowledge are passed from generation to generation through storytelling and conversation.
8.5a Measuring Intelligence
Intelligence
▼
Intelligence broadly = abilities that allow adaptation to the environment and goal-directed behaviour.
Key Figures in Intelligence Testing
FB
Francis Galton (1822–1911)
Proposed intelligence is inherited and measurable via physical abilities (reaction time, eyesight). Early studies found little support; ideas fell out of favour.
AB
Alfred Binet (1857–1911)
Created the first practical intelligence test with Théodore Simon (1905). Introduced the concept of mental age (MA) — the age that reflects a child's mental abilities compared to average. 30-task test arranged in difficulty order.
LT
Lewis Terman (1877–1956)
Developed the Stanford-Binet (1916). Popularised the IQ formula: IQ = (MA ÷ CA) × 100. Average IQ = 100 (MA equals CA). Standardised the test on a large US sample.
DW
David Wechsler (1896–1981)
Objected to single-score IQ and mental age concept for adults. Created tests yielding subscale scores. IQ compared to population average (mean=100). Three tests: WPPSI-IV (2½–7), WISC-V (6–16), WAIS-IV (16–90). WAIS-IV measures verbal comprehension, perceptual reasoning, working memory, processing speed.
Test Quality: Reliability & Validity
Reliability
The test yields consistent measurements over time. Same person tested 6 months apart should score similarly.
Validity
The test measures what it claims to measure. E.g., a valid IQ test should correlate with GPA if intelligence → academic success.
Standardised Test
Uses uniform questions, procedures, and scoring for all test takers. Normed against a representative sample.
Cultural Bias
When a test's content reflects one culture's knowledge/norms, disadvantaging those from different cultural backgrounds.
8.5b Theories of Intelligence
Intelligence
▼
From a single "g" to multiple intelligences — how researchers conceptualise the nature of intelligence.
Theorists at a Glance
CS
Charles Spearman (1863–1945)
General Intelligence (g) — because scores on separate mental abilities correlate, there must be one underlying general intelligence that drives all cognitive performance.
LT
L.L. Thurstone (1938)
Rejected g; proposed 7 distinct primary mental abilities: reasoning, associative memory, spatial visualisation, numerical fluency, verbal comprehension, perceptual speed, word fluency.
RC
Raymond Cattell (1963)
Revived g but split it: Crystallised Intelligence (Gc) = accumulated knowledge (grows throughout life) + Fluid Intelligence (Gf) = speed/efficiency of processing new information (declines across adulthood).
HG
Howard Gardner (b. 1943)
Theory of Multiple Intelligences — 9 intelligences (linguistic, logical-mathematical, spatial, musical, bodily-kinaesthetic, interpersonal, intrapersonal, naturalistic, existential). Intelligence = biopsychological potential activated within culture. Values intelligence profiles, not a single score.
RS
Robert Sternberg (b. 1949)
Triarchic Theory — successful intelligence = three abilities: Analytical (logic, problem solving), Practical (common sense, adapting to environment), Creative (using knowledge in novel situations).
DG
Daniel Goleman (b. 1946)
Emotional Intelligence (EI) — awareness of own emotions, self-control, trustworthiness, adaptability, motivation, empathy, social skills. Better predictor of life success and resilience than IQ in some contexts. Can be taught.
Quick Comparison: Fluid vs. Crystallised Intelligence
DimensionFluid Intelligence (Gf)Crystallised Intelligence (Gc)
DefinitionSpeed & efficiency of learning/solving new problemsAccumulated knowledge & learned skills
Lifespan trendPeaks in young adulthood; declines with ageCan continue growing well into late adulthood
InfluencesPhysical health, cardiovascular health, lifestyle (yoga/meditation linked to slower decline)Education, reading, formal learning
8.5c Nature, Nurture & IQ
Intelligence
▼
The dominant view is interactionism — genes and environment interact to produce intelligence.
Key Concepts
Genes
DNA strands in cell nuclei directing development. ~30,000 in the human genome (Human Genome Project, 2003).
Genotype
The genetic code inherited for a particular trait.
Phenotype
The actual trait that develops — product of genotype and environment.
Natural Selection
Adaptive traits that aid survival/reproduction are passed on; maladaptive ones die out (Darwin, 1859).
Heritability
Proportion of trait variability explained by genetics. For intelligence: 0.4 to 0.8 range across studies. Higher in upper-SES children.
Interactionism
Today's dominant view: genes interact with environment to produce traits. Neither alone is sufficient.
Research Methods for Nature vs. Nurture
Twin Studies
Compare identical (100% shared genes) vs. fraternal twins. Greater similarity in identical twins → genetic basis. Identical twins raised apart vs. together isolates environmental effects.
Genetic / DNA Studies
Correlate specific genes/genetic markers with IQ scores. Intelligence is polygenetic (based on many genes). Scottish study: identified markers accounted for 40% of Gc variance and 51% of Gf variance.
Adoption Studies
Children adopted into higher-SES families show 12–18 point IQ increases (Nisbett et al., 2012) → strong environmental effect.
Environmental Predictors of Higher IQ
Parent verbal behaviour (using many words, positive tone, relational language, asking rather than telling) is a stronger predictor of child IQ than family wealth alone.

Other positive factors: Breastfeeding (Omega-3 fatty acids), interactive reading, musical training, quality schooling, preschool access, adoption into higher-SES family.

Negative factors: Prenatal fluoride / phthalate exposure, low-quality schooling, poverty-related stressors.
The Flynn Effect
Flynn Effect: Average IQ scores have risen ~3 points per decade across the 20th–21st century in many countries. The Black-White IQ gap has narrowed by 5.5+ points since the 1970s. Rapid environmental change (education, nutrition, exposure to abstract thinking) is the most likely explanation — genetic change is too slow to account for this shift.
