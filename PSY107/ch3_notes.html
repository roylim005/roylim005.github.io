3.1 Measuring Sensation — Psychophysics

How psychologists measure the limits of human sensory experience

Absolute Threshold Core Concept

The minimum intensity of a stimulus detectable 50% of the time. The 50% benchmark is used because detection varies trial-to-trial and person-to-person. Classic examples: a candle flame 30 miles away (vision); a watch ticking 20 ft away in silence (hearing); one teaspoon of sugar in 2 gallons of water (taste).

Signal Detection Theory Theory

Our ability to detect stimuli is affected by more than intensity. Attention, motivation, and fatigue all play a role. A fatigued air traffic controller may miss an alarm; an anxious one may falsely detect it — even though the alarm's volume is unchanged. Explains why thresholds are defined probabilistically (50%) rather than as fixed points.

Just Noticeable Difference (JND) & Weber's Law Law

The difference threshold is the minimum change in stimulus intensity detectable 50% of the time. Weber's Law: the amount of change required for a JND is proportional to the starting intensity of the stimulus. One teaspoon of salt in bland soup = noticeable. Same teaspoon in already-salty soup = unnoticeable.

Subliminal Perception Concept

Unconscious processing of stimuli below awareness threshold. James Vicary's 1957 "Eat Popcorn" experiment was fraudulent — admitted in 1962. Modern research shows subliminal persuasion can occur when: (1) messages match product dimensions being evaluated, (2) messages align with personality traits + situational factors. Subliminally perceived angry faces inhibit impulsive responses. Infants at 7 months detect trustworthiness in subliminal faces — suggesting evolutionary origins.

Extrasensory Perception (ESP) Evidence

Not supported by scientific evidence. The standard test (Ganzfeld procedure) shows receivers guess correctly ~50% of the time — no better than chance. Moulton & Kosslyn (2008) fMRI study found no brain activation differences between correct and incorrect trials, providing strong evidence against ESP.

3.2 Vision: Seeing the World

Light properties, eye anatomy, retinal processing, and color vision theories

Properties of Light

Wavelength → Hue

Distance between wave peaks (nm). Short wavelengths = cool colors (blues/purples). Long wavelengths = warm colors (yellows/reds). Visible spectrum: 360–750 nm.

Amplitude → Brightness

Height of wave peak. Higher amplitude = brighter perceived color.

Saturation → Richness

Purity of light. Single wavelength = richest color. Mixed wavelengths = less saturated (e.g., blue + white light = washed-out blue).

Electromagnetic Spectrum

Humans see only a narrow band (360–750 nm). Snakes detect infrared; spiders, butterflies, and rats detect UV light.

Anatomy of the Eye

Outer Eye Structures — Path of Light

  • Cornea: Clear bulging outer surface; protects eye and begins focusing by bending light
  • Pupil: Aperture (opening) in the iris that admits light
  • Iris: Colored muscle ring; dilates pupil in dim light, constricts in bright light
  • Lens: Flexible structure attached by ciliary muscles; undergoes accommodation (shape change) to focus image on retina
  • Sclera: White protective structure; light cannot pass through it
Retina and Photoreceptors

Retinal Layers (surface → back)

Ganglion cells → Amacrine → Bipolar → Horizontal → Rods & Cones. Light must pass through all layers before reaching photoreceptors. Signals then travel back out to ganglion cells.

Fovea, Blindspot & Optic Nerve

Fovea: Central point of sharpest vision; directly behind the pupil; highest cone density. Blindspot: Point where the optic nerve exits the retina — no rods or cones here. Located at the side of the visual field (not at fovea). Compensated for by using both eyes together. Optic nerve: Axons of ganglion cells bundled together; carries visual info to the brain.

Photopigments — Transduction of Light

Rods and cones contain photopigments (light-sensitive chemicals). Light energy splits these molecules apart, triggering a chain of chemical reactions that changes the rate of action potential firing. The brain interprets these firing patterns as visual information. Photopigments also regulate sleep and pupillary reflexes.

Light and Dark Adaptation

Dark Adaptation (~30–45 min)

In darkness, photopigments build up since they aren't being broken down by light. Eyes become progressively more sensitive. Entering a dark theater: wait for photopigments to accumulate before you can see clearly.

Light Adaptation (~1 min)

In bright light, large stored photopigment reserves are quickly broken down. Waking to sudden bright light = brief pain/flash as oversupply of photopigments is overwhelmed.

Color Vision Theories

Trichromatic Theory Helmholtz, mid-1800s

Three cone types sensitive to long (red), medium (green), and short (blue) wavelengths — corresponding to the three primary colors of light. Brain reads the ratio of cone activation to determine color. Explains color blindness. Limitation: does not explain negative afterimages.

Opponent-Process Theory Hering

Three types of opponent-process cells: red/green, yellow/blue, black/white. Each cell signals only one of its pair at a time — the colors "oppose" each other. Explains negative afterimages: staring at red exhausts red receptors → looking at white paper activates only the green side → you see green. Also explains why you cannot perceive red-green simultaneously.

Integration: Both theories are correct at different processing levels. Trichromatic theory describes the rods and cones. Opponent-process theory describes processing beyond — at the ganglion cells, amacrine/horizontal/bipolar cells, and visual cortex.
Color Blindness

Red-Green Color Blindness

Affects ~8% of U.S. males. Caused by absent or abnormally insensitive red or green cones. Rates vary across populations (higher in Turkey and Greenland, lower in Colombia, Spain, Italy), confirming a sex-linked genetic basis. Individuals distinguish stoplight status by position, not color.

Visual Pathway to the Brain

From Retina to Visual Cortex

Rods/Cones → Bipolar/Horizontal/Amacrine cells → Ganglion cells → Optic nerve → Optic chiasm (visual information crosses: right visual field → left hemisphere; left → right) → Thalamus → Visual cortex (occipital lobe) where meaning is interpreted.

3.3 Hearing: Listening to the World

Sound wave properties, ear anatomy, and pitch perception theories

Properties of Sound

Frequency → Pitch

Measured in Hz (cycles/sec). Human range: 20–20,000 Hz. High-frequency hearing declines with age. Tones above 16,000 Hz are inaudible to people as young as 24. "Mosquito" ringtones exploit this.

Amplitude → Loudness

Measured in decibels (dB). Any sound over 85 dB risks hearing damage with prolonged exposure. Rock concert (~120 dB): damage in 15 min. Firearms: 150+ dB.

60–60 Rule: To protect hearing, listen to personal listening devices at no more than 60% of maximum volume for no more than 60 minutes per day.
Anatomy of the Ear

Path of Sound through the Ear

  • Pinna: Outer ear; funnels sound waves into the auditory canal
  • Auditory canal: Amplifies sound before it reaches the eardrum
  • Eardrum (tympanic membrane): Thin membrane that vibrates in response to sound waves
  • Hammer, Anvil, Stirrup (ossicles): Three tiny middle-ear bones; mechanically amplify vibrations
  • Oval window: Connection point between middle ear and cochlea
  • Cochlea: Coiled, fluid-filled tube (~1.4 in); hair cells on the basilar membrane convert pressure waves to neural impulses
  • Auditory nerve: Bundles neural impulses from hair cells and relays to brain; information reaches both hemispheres after leaving brainstem
Pitch Perception Theories

Place Theory Helmholtz / Békésy

Different pitches excite hair cells at different locations on the basilar membrane. High frequencies activate the front (near oval window); low frequencies activate the far end. Hair cells at the front are most vulnerable — explains why high-pitch hearing deteriorates first with age. Limitation: explains pure tones well but not complex, multi-frequency sounds.

Frequency Theory Rutherford

Hair cells fire at a rate matching the incoming sound frequency (phase locking). Brain decodes pitch from firing rate. Limitation: hair cells max out at 1,000 Hz action potentials/sec, yet we hear up to 20,000 Hz.

Volley Theory Wever

"Teams" of hair cells fire in alternating volleys to represent frequencies above 1,000 Hz. Example: three cells each firing at 1,000 Hz collectively encode a 3,000 Hz tone. Extends frequency theory beyond its 1,000 Hz ceiling.

Integration: Current consensus is a dual mechanism — volley theory + place theory work together. Both the rate of neural firing AND the location of basilar membrane activation contribute to pitch perception.

3.4 Taste, Smell, Touch & Body Senses

Chemical senses, skin sense, and body orientation

Taste (Gustation)

Five Basic Tastes

TasteSubstanceSurvival Function
SweetSugarsSignals edible, energy-rich foods
SaltySodiumDetects sodium critical for nerve function
SourAcidsWarns of fermentation/spoilage
BitterAlkaloidsWarns of poisons
UmamiL-glutamic acid / MSGStimulates appetite; may reduce overeating in women

Umami and salty flavors activate the same brain areas on fMRI — may share a processing system.

Tongue Anatomy & Pathway

Papillae = visible bumps on tongue. Taste buds reside in the pits between papillae (2,000–5,000 total; can regenerate). Center of tongue lacks taste buds = "taste blindspot." Lowering then rewarming tongue temperature increases taste sensitivity. Pathway: taste nerves → medulla/pons → thalamus → somatosensory cortex (parietal lobe) + some to limbic system.

Smell (Olfaction)

Olfactory System

Chemical sense requiring airborne molecules to contact the olfactory epithelium (lining top of nasal cavity). ~400 receptor types detect 400,000+ distinct odors. Olfaction is the least understood of the senses. Pathway: olfactory nerve → olfactory bulb → bypasses thalamus → primary smell cortex (temporal lobe) and limbic system. Direct limbic connection explains strong emotional/memory links to odors. Women detect odors slightly better than men (hormonal basis).

Pheromones & Vomeronasal Sense

Airborne chemosignals that influence social and sexual behavior. Documented effects: menstrual synchrony among women; male cooperation increases near ovulating females; women's tears contain chemosignals that reduce male testosterone and sexual arousal (Gelstein et al., 2011); men exposed to fertile-phase sweat report higher sexual arousal. Presence of human vomeronasal organs debated (~27% incidence in one study of 966 patients).

Touch (Skin Sense)

Skin Structure & Neural Pathway

Epidermis (outer layer): contains touch receptors, especially at fingertips. Dermis (inner layer): contains most touch receptors. Separate receptor types for touch, temperature, and pain. Pathway: receptors → spinal cord → thalamus → somatosensory cortex (parietal lobe). Threatening stimuli also route to limbic system.

Gate Control Theory of Pain Melzack & Wall, 1965

Neural networks in the spinal cord can block ("close the gate" on) pain signals when stimulated by intense tactile input — e.g., rubbing an injured area. Basis for TENS units (transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation) used in chronic pain management.

Body Senses

Kinesthesis

Sense of body position and movement in space. Uses input from muscles, tendons, skin, and joints. Processed in somatosensory cortex and cerebellum. Critical for athletes, gymnasts, and divers.

Vestibular Sense

Sense of balance and body orientation. Uses fluid-filled semicircular canals and vestibular sacs in the inner ear — hair cells bend as fluid moves when the head moves. May also contribute to body ownership (awareness that your body belongs to you). Overstimulation causes dizziness and nausea.

3.5 Perception: Interpreting Your World

How the brain constructs meaning from sensory data

Top-Down Processing

Uses prior knowledge, expectations, and context to interpret stimuli. Allows filling in missing details. Can create perceptual sets. Risk: can produce errors, e.g., eyewitness misidentifications (majority of false convictions involve mistaken eyewitness ID — Wells & Olson, 2003).

Bottom-Up Processing

Builds perception purely from properties of the stimulus itself, without relying on stored knowledge. Used for ambiguous or novel stimuli. Both processes operate continually and interactively in daily life.

Perceptual Constancy

Types of Perceptual Constancy

Despite changes in the retinal image, the brain maintains stable perceptions of the world:

  • Size constancy: A person looks the same height at 3 ft and 6 ft, even though retinal image size halves
  • Shape constancy: A door appears rectangular even when viewed at an angle
  • Brightness constancy: Coal appears black in both dim and bright light
  • Color constancy: Colors appear stable despite shifts in ambient lighting
Depth Perception

Binocular Cues

Retinal disparity: Two eyes see slightly different images; disparity is greatest for near objects, least for distant ones. Brain calculates depth from the degree of disparity. Requires both eyes.

Monocular Cues

Relative size, interposition, linear perspective, height on horizon, texture gradient, light and shadow. Allow depth perception with one eye — used in art and photography. Artists with misaligned eyes (impaired binocular cues) may exploit monocular cues more skillfully.

Gestalt Principles of Form Perception

Gestalt Laws — "The whole is greater than the sum of its parts"

  • Figure-Ground: Perceptual separation of objects (figures) from their backgrounds
  • Closure: We mentally complete incomplete shapes (e.g., gap-filled triangles)
  • Proximity: Nearby stimuli are grouped together — allows word boundaries to be perceived while reading
  • Similarity: Similar stimuli are grouped together
  • Good Continuation: We prefer to perceive stimuli as part of a continuing pattern — basis of camouflage
Feature Detection Theory

Feature Detectors & Higher Processing

Specialized cells in the visual cortex fire only in response to specific visual features (shape, color, angle, movement). Multiple feature detectors combine to create coherent perception. Fusiform face area (temporal lobe): specialized for face processing, enlarges during childhood. Other dedicated areas process body parts and indoor/outdoor scenes. Simple feature detection does not fully account for the richness of human visual perception.

3.6 The Accuracy of Perception

Why and how our perceptions go wrong

Errors from Top-Down Processing

We perceive what we expect to perceive. The Georgia bear example: student assumed mature pine trees (as expected in Georgia mountains) → incorrectly scaled the bear. It wasn't until he drove back for a closer look that he realized his expectations had overridden his raw sensation. Perceptual sets and cultural expectations silently shape perception.

Perceptual Illusions — When Constancy Backfires

The Moon Illusion

Moon appears larger on the horizon than overhead, despite projecting the same retinal image size in both positions. Apparent distance theory: interposition cues on the horizon (trees, buildings) signal the moon as far away → brain inflates perceived size to maintain size constancy. Overhead moon has no such cues → no correction. Tip: view the moon between your legs — the brain stops compensating and the illusion vanishes.

The Ponzo Illusion

Two equal-length horizontal lines placed across converging lines appear unequal. Linear perspective signals the upper line as farther away. Same retinal size + greater apparent distance → brain inflates the upper line's perceived length to maintain size constancy.

The Müller-Lyer Illusion

Vertical lines with outward-pointing arrows appear longer than identical lines with inward-pointing arrows. Arrows serve as depth cues analogous to the inside vs. outside corners of a building — triggering size-constancy compensation, creating the illusion.

Cultural Influences on Perception

Culture & the Carpentered World Hypothesis

People in rectangular-building environments are more susceptible to the Müller-Lyer illusion because they have more experience with the angular depth cues it exploits. The Bashi people of Africa (round dwellings) and traditional Navajos in round hogans are measurably less susceptible than Europeans. Perception is not culturally neutral — environment and culture shape perceptual expectations.

  • ESP / Psi — Claimed ability to acquire information without known senses; not supported by science
  • Ganzfeld procedure — Standard scientific test for telepathy

  • Wavelength — Distance between light wave peaks; determines hue/color
